Diversified Gemini

"While Breznitz correctly critiques the Silicon Valley model, his strategy of specializing in fragmented stages of global production is outdated because the future of inclusive prosperity requires regional vertical integration to ensure economic resilience."

Dan Breznitz’s "What Silicon Valley Gets Wrong about Innovation" is a perceptive critique of the venture-capital consensus that has dominated economic development for the last three decades. His taxonomy of innovation into four stages—novelty, design, second-generation components, and assembly—provides a highly accurate map of how the globalized economy operated from the late 1990s through the end of the 2010s. However, as we look at the world in October 2021, it is becoming increasingly clear that the map Breznitz relies on is already out of date. The era of hyper-fragmented, globally dispersed innovation chains is coming to a chaotic end, and the local innovation strategies of the future will require re-integrating these stages, not merely picking one to specialize in.

Breznitz writes that "Globalization’s collapse of the conventional vertically integrated system... has subjected local communities to new forms of competition but also opened new entry points for innovation-based growth." He bases his entire thesis of inclusive prosperity on this premise: because production is fragmented across the globe, a region like Alto Livenza in Italy can thrive solely on Stage 2 design, while Taiwan thrives on Stage 3 components, and Shenzhen on Stage 4 assembly.

But this system is breaking down before our eyes. The supply chain crisis of 2021 is not merely a temporary pandemic blip; it is the exposure of the fatal flaw in the globally fragmented model. When you separate design, component manufacturing, and final assembly by thousands of miles of ocean, you introduce massive friction and fragility into the system. Shipping costs have exploded by hundreds of percent over the last year. Ports in Los Angeles and Long Beach are paralyzed. Energy shortages in China are causing rolling blackouts in the very Stage 4 manufacturing hubs Breznitz praises, abruptly halting production lines for companies worldwide.

Simultaneously, geopolitical tensions are making the global division of labor untenable. The United States and Europe are realizing that relying on strategic rivals for Stage 3 and Stage 4 innovation is a catastrophic vulnerability.

In this new reality, a local economic strategy based on hyper-specialization in one single stage of a global supply chain is a recipe for disaster. If your community focuses entirely on Stage 2 design, but the factories in Asia that manufacture your prototypes are shut down due to energy quotas or geopolitical sanctions, your local economy stalls. If your community specializes in Stage 4 assembly, but you cannot source the Stage 3 microchips due to a global shortage, your workers are furloughed.

The path forward for inclusive local prosperity in the post-pandemic, de-globalizing world is not to find a niche in a fragmented global chain, but to build regional, vertically integrated "circular economies." We need to bring the stages of innovation closer together geographically.

This doesn't mean every small town must mine its own ore, design its own chips, and build its own cars. But it does mean that at the macro-regional level—say, North America or Europe—we must strive to have all four stages of innovation present and deeply interconnected. We need the Stage 1 inventors in Boston or Austin working closely with Stage 2 designers in the Midwest, sourcing Stage 3 components fabricated in Ohio or Mexico, with final Stage 4 assembly happening locally to serve regional consumer markets.

This re-verticalization of the economy will undoubtedly be more expensive in the short term. We will lose some of the extreme efficiencies that came from exploiting cheap labor and lax environmental regulations in distant hubs. But we will gain immense resilience. We will also recapture the "industrial commons"—the shared knowledge and serendipity that occurs when the people who design a product are in close physical proximity to the people who manufacture it. As Breznitz notes in his example of Schwinn, losing the capacity to manufacture eventually destroys the capacity to innovate. Bringing production back closer to design will spark new waves of continuous, iterative innovation that are currently lost in the long feedback loops of trans-Pacific shipping.

Furthermore, a regional, vertically integrated approach is vital for the transition to a sustainable economy. We cannot build a green economy if the components of our wind turbines and solar panels are manufactured using coal power in one hemisphere and shipped on heavy fuel-burning cargo ships to another. We need localized, closed-loop systems where products are designed for durability, manufactured regionally, and eventually disassembled and recycled within the same economic ecosystem.

Breznitz is absolutely right to dismiss the Silicon Valley VC model as the sole engine of progress, and his focus on tech teens and continuous innovation is incredibly valuable. But his reliance on the globalized fragmentation of the past thirty years is a misstep. The future of local inclusive prosperity will not be found by isolating ourselves in one specific stage of a fragile global machine, but by weaving the entire process of innovation—from idea to assembly—back into the fabric of our own regional economies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current global supply chain crisis and rising shipping costs demonstrate that separating design, components, and assembly across vast distances creates fatal systemic fragility.
  2. 02Geopolitical tensions make relying on strategic rivals for specific stages of innovation a catastrophic vulnerability for national and local economies.
  3. 03Hyper-specialization in a single innovation stage leaves a community's economy at the mercy of disruptions in other parts of the global chain, such as chip shortages or energy blackouts.
  4. 04Re-integrating all four stages of innovation at a macro-regional level recaptures the 'industrial commons' and fosters iterative innovation through physical proximity between designers and manufacturers.
  5. 05A regional, vertically integrated approach is necessary for environmental sustainability, as it reduces the carbon footprint of trans-oceanic shipping and supports closed-loop recycling systems.
