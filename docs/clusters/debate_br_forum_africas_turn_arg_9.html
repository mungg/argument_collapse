"The question of whether it is "Africa’s turn" for development is a flawed outsider's perspective that overlooks the diverse realities of the continent and, more importantly, the central role of African agency in driving progress."

“Is it Africa’s turn?” is a question that reveals as much about outsiders as about Africa. It suggests a global queue: first Japan, then the Asian Tigers, then China and India, perhaps now Africa. But Africa is not a single late-arriving passenger waiting to be called. It is a continent of more than forty sub-Saharan countries, hundreds of languages, coastal and landlocked economies, oil exporters and oil importers, democracies and dictatorships, postwar recoveries and ongoing catastrophes. Some places may be turning; others are being turned upon.

Edward Miguel knows this, and his essay is more careful than its title. Still, the title matters because it reflects a persistent habit in development thinking: Africa is treated as an object of diagnosis. It is “rising,” “failing,” “trapped,” “hopeless,” “emerging,” or “at a crossroads.” The continent becomes a mood. In the 1960s the mood was hope; in the 1980s and 1990s despair; now perhaps hope again. Africans live with the consequences of these foreign mood swings, which influence investment, aid, diplomacy, and media attention.

The danger is that the search for a continental verdict can obscure African agency. Busia’s supermarkets, ATMs, and Internet cafés did not simply appear because China demanded copper or because Freedom House scores improved. They were built by Kenyan traders, shopkeepers, drivers, teachers, officials, migrants, farmers, and families making decisions under difficult conditions. The mobile-phone revolution in Africa is not only a story of technology transfer; it is a story of rapid adoption by people who recognized its value. Informal traders do not wait for development theories before integrating markets.

Similarly, democratization did not arrive as a benevolent global wave. Africans fought for it. Journalists were arrested, lawyers harassed, students beaten, trade unionists threatened, opposition leaders jailed, churches politicized, women’s groups organized, and voters stood in long lines. Kenya’s 2002 election mattered because many Kenyans had spent years pushing against one-party rule. Zimbabwe’s democratic movement, whatever its current prospects, has shown extraordinary courage under repression. Nigerians, Ghanaians, Senegalese, South Africans, Ugandans, and others have all contested power in ways that do not fit the stereotype of passive victims.

Miguel rightly emphasizes institutions, but institutions are not abstractions imported from textbooks. They are built through struggle. A free press exists because editors decide to publish and readers decide to buy. Courts gain independence when lawyers insist on procedure, judges take risks, and citizens refuse to accept arbitrary rule. Electoral commissions become credible not through donor workshops alone, but through domestic pressure. Kenya’s post-election violence showed institutional weakness, but the public challenge to the results showed institutional life. Both truths must be held together.

The China discussion also looks different when viewed through African agency. Western commentary often treats African governments as naïve recipients of Chinese strategy. Some are, but many are bargaining. They welcome Chinese finance partly because Western donors and investors have been slow, moralizing, and risk-averse. If a Chinese company will build a road that a European donor has studied for ten years, the attraction is obvious. Africans are entitled to seek alternatives.

That does not mean Chinese investment should be romanticized. Zambian miners have reason to protest dangerous conditions. Sudanese civilians have reason to fear arms and diplomatic protection flowing to Khartoum. But the solution is not for Western observers to scold Africa for choosing the wrong patrons. The solution is for African workers, unions, parliaments, journalists, and courts to demand standards from all investors. Western oil, mining, and agricultural companies have their own histories of exploitation. Chinese firms should be judged firmly, but not against an imaginary record of Western purity.

The lead essay’s strongest policy argument concerns vulnerability to shocks. Drought, commodity-price collapse, and disputed elections can unravel years of progress. Yet here too we should avoid treating African societies merely as fragile vessels. People have long managed risk through migration, kinship networks, rotating savings groups, religious institutions, livestock holdings, and informal trade. These systems are under strain, but they are real. Development policy should strengthen, not bulldoze, local resilience.

For example, drought-relief programs modeled on Botswana’s experience may help, but they must fit local political economies. Pastoralists in the Sahel do not only need food aid; they need mobility, negotiated access to water, veterinary services, and conflict mediation with farmers. Smallholders need storage, market information, and roads, but also secure land rights. Urban youth need jobs, but also political voice. Programs designed from rainfall maps alone may miss the social institutions through which people actually survive.

The same applies to climate change. It is morally grotesque that people who contributed least to greenhouse-gas emissions may suffer most from their effects. But adaptation cannot be reduced to improved crop varieties, though those matter. It will require local knowledge: which crops women plant in marginal soils, how herders read pasture conditions, how communities settle water disputes, how migrants send remittances, which customary authorities retain legitimacy, and where state intervention is trusted or feared. Climate policy that ignores politics may become another development fashion.

If there is a continental lesson, it is that the future will be uneven. Ghana’s prospects are not Chad’s. Botswana’s state is not Congo’s. Kenya’s crisis is not Mozambique’s recovery, and Zimbabwe’s collapse is not Tanzania’s gradualism. Oil may enrich one country’s ruling class while horticulture employs another’s workers. Democracy may consolidate in one place and be stolen in another. To ask whether Africa is rising is less useful than asking which coalitions, sectors, and institutions are gaining strength in particular countries.

Still, broad narratives have power, and perhaps a more hopeful narrative is overdue. For too long, Africa has been represented through famine, war, disease, and corruption. The changes Miguel describes—roads, media, cell phones, growth, civic courage—deserve attention. Hope can attract investment and reverse fatalism. But hope should be disciplined by respect. Africa is not waiting for its turn to be granted by China, donors, or commodity markets. Africans are making claims on their own future.

The real question, then, is not “Is it Africa’s turn?” It is whether African citizens will have the power to shape the turn: to hold leaders accountable, bargain with investors, manage resources, prevent violence, educate children, and adapt to a warming world. If they do, outsiders may look back and call it a miracle. Africans may call it something less mystical: politics, work, and persistence.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Treating Africa as a single entity or a "mood" for diagnosis ignores the vast differences between its more than forty countries and their unique economic and political contexts.
  2. 02Economic and technological advancements, such as the mobile-phone revolution, are the result of active decision-making and entrepreneurship by Africans rather than passive reception of global trends.
  3. 03Democratization and the building of state institutions are not gifts from the international community but are hard-won outcomes of domestic struggle and civic courage.
  4. 04African governments and citizens are active bargainers in the global economy, seeking alternatives like Chinese investment to bypass slow or moralizing Western donors.
  5. 05Development policies and climate adaptation strategies must be rooted in local knowledge and existing social institutions rather than being designed solely from external data like rainfall maps.
  6. 06The future of the continent depends less on global commodity cycles and more on whether African citizens have the political power to hold their leaders and international investors accountable.
