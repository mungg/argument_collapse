"While religious traditions should embrace effective altruism’s demand for evidence-based, impactful giving, they must also insist that the moral life requires a focus on the giver's heart, the pursuit of systemic justice, and the preservation of local community bonds."

Peter Singer’s essay is a secular sermon, and a powerful one. Like many sermons, it begins by exposing a gap between what we profess and how we live. We say every human life has equal worth, yet we spend lavishly on ourselves while others die for lack of cheap medicines, mosquito nets, or clean water. We say generosity is a virtue, yet we give in ways that please us rather than in ways that most help the needy. Singer’s effective altruism is, in this sense, an examination of conscience.

Religious traditions should recognize the challenge. Long before “effective altruism” acquired a name, Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, and other traditions taught that wealth is morally dangerous and that the poor have a claim upon us. The tithe, zakat, almsgiving, hospitality to strangers, care for widows and orphans, and the prophetic denunciation of those who “trample on the needy” all reject the idea that surplus income is simply ours to enjoy. Singer’s claim that a minimally acceptable ethical life requires substantial use of spare resources for others is not alien to religious ethics. It is embarrassingly familiar.

Indeed, effective altruism can correct a weakness in much religious giving. Congregations often direct money inward: buildings, programs for members, schools for the already privileged, aesthetic improvements, institutional survival. Some of this is necessary; communities need places to gather and rituals to sustain them. But the danger of self-serving piety is real. A church can spend more on its sound system than on the hungry. A synagogue or mosque can celebrate generosity while ignoring the global poor. Singer’s insistence on opportunity cost is a salutary rebuke. If a dollar can prevent blindness or malaria, then spending that dollar on religious comfort requires justification.

The Make-A-Wish example is also morally clarifying. Compassion naturally moves toward the child whose story we know. But the biblical command to love the stranger was given precisely because the stranger lacks the claims of familiarity. The Good Samaritan is good not because he follows his passion, but because he allows another’s need to interrupt his plans. Effective altruism extends that interruption across borders. The child under threat from malaria is also our neighbor.

Yet religious ethics also has something to say back to Singer. The first point is about love. Singer is careful to say that effective altruists need not be cold calculators, and he notes that many find joy in giving. Still, his framework tends to treat love as motivation and welfare as outcome. In many religious traditions, love is not merely the engine that produces good consequences; it is itself part of the good. To feed the hungry while despising them, or to save lives while remaining indifferent to persons as persons, would be spiritually disordered even if the external result were beneficial. Effective altruism can tell us where a donation may do most good. It cannot by itself form the heart to see recipients as brothers and sisters rather than impact units.

Second, religious traditions are wary of pride disguised as righteousness. The effective altruist who gives half his income may be genuinely admirable. He may also be tempted to divide the world into the enlightened and the sentimental, the rational and the warm-glow crowd. But moral accounting can become a subtle vanity. In the Gospel, the widow’s small coin matters not because it is cost-effective, but because it expresses trust and surrender. That story should not be abused to excuse rich people’s paltry giving. But it reminds us that generosity is measured not only by outcomes but by the transformation of the giver.

Third, justice must not disappear into charity. The prophets do not merely urge alms; they condemn unjust weights, corrupt courts, exploitation of workers, and rulers who neglect the poor. A society in which the wealthy make highly efficient donations while benefiting from unjust structures remains morally compromised. Singer’s essay mentions factory owners and Wall Street careers, but it does not sufficiently emphasize the moral question of how wealth is acquired. Restitution, fair wages, debt relief, and political reform are not optional supplements to benevolence. They are part of righteousness.

Fourth, religious life preserves the importance of community. Singer rightly criticizes gifts to already wealthy universities and cultural institutions. But not all local or communal giving is self-indulgent. A congregation that supports a recovering addict, visits prisoners, welcomes immigrants, or cares for an elderly member is doing something that cannot be replaced by a more efficient overseas transfer. The command to love the whole world does not abolish the obligation to love the person at one’s door.

So the proper religious response to effective altruism is neither dismissal nor surrender. We should accept its rebuke of wasteful, vanity-driven, and parochial giving. We should learn from its demand for evidence. Churches, synagogues, mosques, temples, and religious charities should be far more willing to ask whether their programs actually relieve suffering. Donors should direct much more money to interventions that save and improve lives among the poorest.

But we should also insist that the moral life is not exhausted by maximizing measurable benefit. It includes worship, gratitude, repentance, solidarity, justice, mercy, and love. Singer has given us a bracing rule of stewardship: do not squander resources that could save lives. Religious traditions can add a deeper discipline: give effectively, yes, but also give humbly, justly, and with a heart being remade.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Effective altruism serves as a necessary rebuke to religious institutions that prioritize internal comforts and aesthetic improvements over the urgent needs of the global poor.
  2. 02The moral value of an act is not found solely in its measurable outcome but also in the spiritual transformation of the giver and the quality of love shown to the recipient.
  3. 03A focus on cost-effective charity must not overshadow the prophetic duty to challenge unjust economic structures and the methods by which wealth is acquired.
  4. 04The obligation to maximize global welfare does not negate the essential moral duty to care for the specific individuals and neighbors within one's immediate community.
  5. 05Effective altruism can correct the tendency toward sentimental giving, but it risks fostering a sense of rational pride that overlooks the humility inherent in true generosity.
