"To prevent the Internet of Things from becoming a pervasive architecture of observation, we must establish baseline privacy legislation and legal rights that limit data collection and usage."

Before we celebrate smart dishwashers and self-reporting running shoes, we should ask a more basic question: who will control the record of ordinary life? The Internet of Things promises convenience, but it also promises a sensor network in the home, on the street, in the car and on the body. That is not merely a new consumer electronics category. It is a new architecture of observation.

The old privacy model — notice and consent — is already collapsing on the Web. It will be absurd when every light bulb, smoke alarm and toy has a privacy policy. No one will read all of those terms, and even if they did, they would have little bargaining power. This summer’s surveillance revelations have also taught a hard lesson: data collected for one purpose rarely stays confined to that purpose. It can be subpoenaed, sold, hacked, shared with insurers, used in divorce proceedings or folded into government databases.

Yes, there are exciting uses. Networked medical devices can save lives. Smart meters can help manage a cleaner electric grid. Sensors in bridges and water systems can warn of failures before people die. But these benefits do not require a free-for-all in personal data.

We need baseline privacy legislation, not just better manners from manufacturers. The rules should include purpose limitation: a baby monitor company should not become a behavioral advertising company because it happens to have access to a nursery. Data should be collected only when necessary, retained only as long as necessary and used for secondary purposes only with explicit, meaningful permission. Consumers should have rights to inspect, correct and delete information about themselves. Strong security should be a legal duty, not a marketing feature.

There should also be special protection for home and body data. A person’s sleep, movements, energy use and medical readings reveal religion, illness, sex, disability and political activity. Law enforcement access to such records should require proper judicial process. Employers and insurers should not be allowed to coerce people into wearing surveillance devices as the price of work or coverage.

The Internet of Things may bring useful tools. But unless we establish rights now, it will also normalize a world in which being alive means continuously generating data for someone else.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The traditional 'notice and consent' privacy model is insufficient for a world where every household object has its own complex terms of service.
  2. 02Data collected by networked devices is frequently repurposed for surveillance, legal proceedings, or commercial sales beyond its original intent.
  3. 03The significant societal benefits of networked infrastructure and medical devices do not necessitate the unrestricted collection of personal data.
  4. 04Legislation should mandate purpose limitation, data minimization, and the right for consumers to inspect or delete their information.
  5. 05Strong digital security should be a legal requirement for manufacturers rather than an optional marketing feature.
  6. 06Sensitive data regarding the home and body requires special legal protections to prevent coercion by employers and insurers and to limit warrantless law enforcement access.
