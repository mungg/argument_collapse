"A feminist politics of reproductive technology must integrate disability justice to ensure that assisted reproduction does not become a tool for eugenic optimization and the marginalization of disabled lives."

The lead essay offers a powerful correction to the fantasy that “natural” reproduction is unassisted. But its stories also reveal another problem that deserves more attention: assisted reproduction is increasingly organized around the detection, ranking, selection, and elimination of potential lives in the name of health. Any feminist politics of reproductive technology must therefore contend with disability justice. Otherwise, a movement that begins by challenging one hierarchy of bodies may end by reinforcing another.

The essay includes several moments in which disability hovers at the edge of reproductive desire. Sherri Finkbine sought an abortion after learning that thalidomide exposure would likely cause severe fetal deformities. K is told not to reproduce because she is overweight and because depression and anxiety might be passed on. B turns to IVF after miscarriage because she wants the viability of embryos determined outside her body. Her embryos are screened; three are deemed free of chromosomal abnormalities. N and K genetically test their remaining embryos after miscarriages and find one viable embryo. These are intimate, painful decisions. It would be wrong to judge them from a distance. But it would also be wrong not to notice the social logic into which they fit.

Reproductive medicine often presents screening as neutral information. Who would oppose knowledge? Who would deny patients the chance to avoid suffering? Yet information is never simply information. It comes packaged with medical assumptions, insurance incentives, cultural stigma, and the terrifying loneliness of being told that a responsible parent should choose correctly. Prenatal testing and preimplantation genetic diagnosis can expand autonomy, but they can also intensify the expectation that disability is a preventable tragedy and that good mothers are obliged to prevent it.

This is not an abstract concern. Disability rights advocates have long criticized the way selective abortion and embryo screening can express, and reinforce, the belief that disabled lives are less worth living. People with Down syndrome, spina bifida, deafness, dwarfism, psychiatric disabilities, and many other conditions live lives of attachment, humor, work, frustration, dependency, love, and meaning—as all people do. Yet reproductive counseling often reduces such lives to burdens. Prospective parents hear about medical complications, cost, divorce rates, caregiver stress, and shortened life expectancy. They hear far less from disabled adults, from families who are not ruined by disability, or from communities that understand disability as produced not only by bodies but by inaccessible environments and social contempt.

The lead essay’s anti-naturalism can help here. If “health” is treated as a natural fact, then selecting against disability appears merely rational. But health, like nature, is partly an ideology. It is shaped by architecture, labor policy, school funding, transportation, stigma, and medical violence. A child who uses a wheelchair is more disabled in a city without ramps. A child with autism is more disabled in a school that treats difference as disruption. A parent with depression is more disabled in a society without affordable mental health care, paid leave, and communal support. When K’s doctor warns that she might pass on “mental illness,” the cruelty lies not only in the eugenic undertone. It lies in the assumption that depression and anxiety disqualify someone from parenthood rather than indicting a society that abandons people with mental illness.

This is where reproductive justice and disability justice converge. The right to have children must include the right of disabled people to have children. It must include the right to be regarded as a potential parent rather than as a risk factor. It must include freedom from coercive sterilization, a practice with a long American history and not merely a distant past. It must include support for disabled parents who are too often presumed incompetent by child welfare systems. It must include respect for women whose pregnancies are treated as irresponsible because they are poor, disabled, fat, mentally ill, addicted, or dependent on public benefits.

At the same time, reproductive justice must include the right not to have a child, including after receiving information about fetal disability. That is the hard part. Disability justice cannot become a new instrument for policing pregnant people. A woman who chooses abortion after a diagnosis is not a eugenicist by definition. A person who screens embryos after repeated miscarriage is not necessarily rejecting disabled people. People make reproductive decisions under conditions of fear, grief, financial precarity, medical uncertainty, and limited support. The state must not force anyone to carry a pregnancy as a symbolic affirmation of disability rights. Anti-abortion politicians have already tried to use disability-selective abortion bans as a wedge, presenting themselves as defenders of disabled people while cutting Medicaid, special education, and health care. Feminists should reject that hypocrisy completely.

But rejecting coercion does not require silence about cultural pressure. We need a reproductive politics capacious enough to say: the decision must remain with the pregnant person or prospective parent, and the world in which that decision is made is saturated with ableism. The answer is not prohibition. It is better counseling, better support, and a broader imagination of what lives are livable.

Clinics should provide balanced information about disability, including contact with disabled adults and families who can speak honestly—not sentimentally—about their lives. Genetic counselors should be trained in disability studies as well as genetics. Success should not be measured only by the birth of a nondisabled baby. Patients should be told what tests can and cannot predict. A chromosomal screen cannot tell whether a child will be loved, whether a family will be supported, whether a school will be inclusive, whether a society will provide care.

We also need to examine the word “viable.” In reproductive medicine it has technical meanings, but in ordinary speech it easily becomes moral. B’s three embryos are “free of chromosomal abnormalities.” N and K have one “viable” embryo. These words offer relief because they seem to reduce uncertainty. Yet they also suggest that abnormality and nonviability are neighbors. Disabled people have spent decades fighting the presumption that their lives are mistakes, misfortunes, or failed versions of normal. Assisted reproduction can unintentionally renew that presumption at the earliest stages of life.

The lead essay argues that the natural makes some mothers feel authentic and others artificial. Disability adds another axis: some children are imagined as welcome futures, others as failures of risk management. When reproduction becomes a field of optimization, parents may feel responsible not only for having children but for having the right kind of children. This is an impossible burden. No technology can guarantee a child without vulnerability. Disability can arrive through birth, accident, illness, violence, or aging. Dependency is not the exception to human life. It is its structure.

A feminist strategy should therefore refuse both naturalism and perfectionism. It should defend access to IVF, genetic testing, abortion, fertility preservation, and prenatal care while resisting the fantasy that technology can rescue us from human fragility. It should insist that disabled lives are not tragedies and that parents deserve honest information rather than ideological scripts. It should fight for Medicaid, home care, inclusive schools, accessible housing, mental health services, and paid family leave as fiercely as it fights for clinic access. These supports are also reproductive assistance.

“All reproduction is assisted” becomes most radical when assistance is not limited to conception and birth. The disabled child, the disabled parent, the depressed mother, the chronically ill father, the trans woman preserving sperm, the fat lesbian dismissed by an obstetrician, the premature infant in an incubator—all expose the same lie: that independence is natural and dependency is defect. Reproduction is not the production of autonomous beings. It is the creation of new relationships of care.

If feminism is to develop better reproductive strategies, it must ask not only who gets to reproduce, but what kinds of bodies we are prepared to welcome after reproduction succeeds. A society that funds IVF but abandons disabled children has not embraced reproductive justice. It has embraced selection. The better demand is more generous and more difficult: every wanted child should be born into a world prepared to make room.
