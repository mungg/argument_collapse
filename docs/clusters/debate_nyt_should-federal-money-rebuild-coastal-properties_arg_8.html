"The federal government should restructure disaster relief to require local governments to bear a significant portion of rebuilding costs, forcing them to internalize the financial risks of their land-use decisions."

The root cause of our coastal development crisis is a dangerous disconnect between local land-use authority and federal financial responsibility. In the United States, zoning laws, building codes, and development permits are almost exclusively controlled by local and state governments. Yet, when the inevitable hurricane strikes, the financial burden of the disaster is immediately shifted to the federal taxpayer. This misalignment of incentives must be corrected.

Local mayors and city councils face tremendous pressure to approve coastal development. Beachfront properties generate massive local property tax revenues, which fund schools, police, and municipal services. Therefore, local governments continually incentivize building as close to the water as possible. They reap 100 percent of the tax benefits during the sunny years, knowing full well that when a storm like Sandy hits, FEMA and the National Flood Insurance Program will arrive with federal checkbooks to cover the losses.

The federal government should not unilaterally ban coastal rebuilding or mandate sweeping buyouts, as Washington is ill-equipped to make neighborhood-level land-use decisions. Instead, Congress should fundamentally restructure how disaster relief is distributed, requiring states and municipalities to bear a significant, painful percentage of the rebuilding costs for repetitive-loss areas.

If a town wants to permit a subdivision on a barrier island, it should be free to do so—but it must also be responsible for 50 percent of the disaster relief when that subdivision floods. If local governments are forced to internalize the true financial risks of their zoning decisions, you will see an immediate, localized shift toward smarter development. Towns will voluntarily institute their own buyouts, strengthen their own building codes, and pull development back from the shore. We don't need a heavy-handed federal mandate; we need local governments to pay for the risks they authorize.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01There is currently a dangerous misalignment of incentives where local governments reap the tax benefits of coastal development while federal taxpayers shoulder the disaster recovery costs.
  2. 02Local governments are incentivized to approve risky beachfront developments because they generate property tax revenue without the burden of future financial liability.
  3. 03The federal government is ill-equipped to make neighborhood-level land-use decisions or mandate sweeping buyouts from Washington.
  4. 04Requiring local governments to pay for a percentage of disaster relief would naturally lead to smarter zoning, stricter building codes, and voluntary buyouts at the local level.
