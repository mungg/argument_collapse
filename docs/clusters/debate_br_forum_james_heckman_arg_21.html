"While early childhood intervention is vital, James Heckman’s dismissal of later interventions is overly pessimistic and ignores evidence that well-designed programs for adolescents and young adults can effectively build skills and improve life outcomes."

James Heckman’s indictment of later interventions is so sweeping that it risks creating a dangerous fatalism about adolescence and young adulthood. He is surely correct that skill begets skill and that early foundations matter enormously. But his essay draws an overly sharp line between the malleability of the early years and the supposed inefficiency of later remediation. In doing so, he underplays evidence that well-designed programs for adolescents and young adults can produce meaningful returns, and he unfairly dismisses strategies that, combined with early childhood investment, could form a continuum of effective policy.

Heckman’s key exhibit against later interventions is the GED. He shows that GED holders, despite being as smart as high school graduates, earn like dropouts and have higher divorce and job-quit rates. From this he concludes that cognitive skills without non-cognitive ones are insufficient, and that the GED, as a later credentialing program, is essentially worthless. But the GED is not a program of intentional character education; it is a test. To treat it as representative of all later interventions misses the mark entirely. Effective adolescent programs, such as year-round youth employment initiatives coupled with mentoring, or high-quality career academies that integrate academic and technical training, have demonstrated improvements in non-cognitive skills like persistence and teamwork. The Quantum Opportunity Program, for instance, provided disadvantaged youth with mentors, educational support, and financial incentives from ninth grade through high school, yielding gains in graduation rates and college enrollment. These are not trivial outcomes.

Heckman’s own framework suggests complementarity: early skill formation increases the productivity of later investment. By that logic, we should not abandon later investment but rather pair it with early enrichment. Yet his policy recommendations lean almost entirely toward the preschool years, leaving adolescents who have already missed out to face a bare cupboard. This is a mistake. Even if early intervention is the most efficient marginal dollar, there is an ethical imperative to meet the needs of current teenagers who are not to blame for their adverse childhoods. A just society does not write off a 16-year-old because her brain’s architecture was shaped in a suboptimal environment. The evidence from adolescent brain science tells us the prefrontal cortex—the seat of impulse control and planning—remains plastic well into the twenties. This is precisely the window for interventions designed to build non-cognitive capacities.

Moreover, Heckman’s cost-benefit analysis of early programs, while impressive, is not the whole story. The Perry Preschool’s 6–10 percent rate of return is largely driven by dramatic reductions in male criminality and incarceration. But those effects were achieved in the 1960s and 1970s with a particular population and a particular curriculum. Replicating those crime reductions in today’s very different social landscape—with its gang structures, easy access to guns, and mass incarceration—is a leap of faith. Later interventions that directly engage youth in the labor market, such as apprenticeships, might yield comparable returns through employment effects that early childhood programs cannot easily target. In Germany, the dual education system has produced low youth unemployment and a smooth school-to-work transition, demonstrating that structured later learning environments matter.

I am not suggesting we abandon early childhood investment. On the contrary, I support a major expansion of home visiting and quality pre-K. But Heckman’s narrative that current job training, adult literacy, and prisoner rehabilitation programs “are not effective, at least not as currently constituted” is too dismissive. It reflects a selective reading of an evaluation literature that is itself marred by poor study designs and short time horizons. Programs like the National Guard Youth ChalleNGe for dropouts have shown rigorous evidence of raising graduation rates and earnings. Instead of abandoning later interventions, we should be experimenting with ways to integrate them with early investments, creating a seamless pipeline of support from birth to career. The binary between early and late is a false one. Our goal should be lifelong skill development, with the heaviest investments early but never a cutoff after which we give up on human potential.
