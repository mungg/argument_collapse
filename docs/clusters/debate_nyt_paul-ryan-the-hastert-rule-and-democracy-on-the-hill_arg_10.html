"Paul Ryan should reject an absolute commitment to the Hastert rule in favor of a qualified approach that allows for bipartisan votes on essential, must-pass legislation to prevent national harm and minority-faction paralysis."

The right way to think about the Hastert rule is as a guardrail, not a suicide pact. There are good reasons for a speaker to hesitate before bringing up bills opposed by most of his own party. Parties organize Congress. They help voters understand choices. They make it possible for a majority to pursue an agenda rather than dissolve into 435 separate negotiations. On ordinary legislation, a Republican speaker should not casually pass measures that most Republicans reject.

But debt-limit and government-funding measures are not ordinary legislation. They are the machinery that allows the constitutional system to keep operating. The question is not whether Republicans must support President Obama’s entire agenda. They do not. The question is whether a faction inside one chamber should be able to prevent the country from paying its bills or keeping basic services open when a majority of the House would vote to avoid that result.

In those circumstances, the Hastert rule becomes profoundly anti-majoritarian. It lets a majority of a majority function as a veto point. Worse, it does so invisibly. Members never have to vote yes or no; the speaker simply withholds the bill. That is a poor form of accountability. If conservatives believe a continuing resolution spends too much, they should vote against it. If moderates believe default would be reckless, they should vote to raise the debt ceiling. The public can then judge both sides.

Paul Ryan should therefore reject an absolute pledge. He could credibly tell Republicans that his default position is to move bills supported by most of the conference, while also announcing explicit exceptions for must-pass measures where inaction would cause serious national harm. That would be more honest than pretending that the rule is sacred until the last possible hour, then breaking it in a panic.

The details matter. To prevent abuse, a speaker might require that bipartisan bills brought up over majority-party opposition be limited to funding the government, honoring existing obligations, disaster response or similar necessities. He could insist on public notice and recorded votes. He could allow both parties to offer alternatives before the final vote. The goal should not be to empower leadership to spring surprise deals on members. It should be to ensure that the House can act when an actual majority exists.

So the answer is yes, but with qualifications. Ignoring the Hastert rule would make the House more democratic when the alternative is paralysis by an internal faction. It would make the House less democratic if it became a routine way to smuggle major policy changes past the majority party’s voters. The distinction between governing and brinkmanship is the line Ryan must draw.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01While the Hastert rule helps maintain party discipline and legislative agendas on ordinary matters, it should not be applied to essential functions like debt-limit and government-funding measures.
  2. 02The Hastert rule is anti-majoritarian and lacks accountability because it allows a faction to veto legislation invisibly by preventing a floor vote.
  3. 03Allowing bipartisan votes on critical fiscal matters is more honest and transparent than maintaining a rigid rule that is inevitably broken during a crisis.
  4. 04To prevent abuse, exceptions to the Hastert rule should be strictly limited to necessities like disaster response and honoring national obligations rather than routine policy changes.
  5. 05Ignoring the rule in specific instances of brinkmanship ensures that the House can function when a true majority exists to avoid national paralysis.
