Diversified Gemini

"While Merve Emre highlights the inclusive potential of assisted reproductive technologies (ART), she overlooks how these technologies are currently weaponized by capitalist structures to exploit workers and prioritize corporate productivity over genuine reproductive liberation."

In “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” Merve Emre offers a sweeping and compassionate narrative of the ways assisted reproductive technologies (ART) mediate the deeply personal desires of women, queer people, and trans people to build biological families. Emre’s central thesis—that we must abandon the exclusionary and consumerist myth of “natural” reproduction to achieve true reproductive solidarity—is compelling. However, by focusing so heavily on the emancipatory potential of technology, Emre severely underestimates the coercive nature of the capitalist structures that own, deploy, and profit from these technologies. If we are to achieve the reproductive justice Emre calls for, we must recognize that under our current economic paradigm, reproductive technology functions less as an engine of feminist liberation and more as an instrument of corporate control.

Nowhere is this clearer than in Emre’s profile of S, the Silicon Valley employee who benefits from her company’s $20,000 egg-freezing perk. Emre portrays S as a savvy navigator of “Silicon Valley’s marriage of capitalist competition and social justice,” dismissing critics of the practice as operating from a place of folksy ignorance or biological essentialism. But the critique of corporate egg-freezing is not rooted in a reactionary attachment to the “natural timeline of motherhood.” It is rooted in a materialist understanding of labor and bodily autonomy.

When tech giants like Apple and Facebook began offering egg freezing in 2014 and 2015, it was not an act of feminist benevolence. It was a calculated human resources strategy designed to extract maximum productivity from female employees during their prime childbearing years. The implicit message sent by a corporate egg-freezing perk is clear: the workplace is currently incompatible with motherhood, and rather than reform the workplace—by offering robust paid family leave, on-site childcare, flexible hours, and a culture that does not penalize parents—the corporation prefers to freeze the worker’s biology. The burden of accommodation is placed entirely on the individual woman’s body, which must endure the grueling, painful, and risky hormone injections that Emre herself so vividly describes.

Emre notes that S views her frozen eggs as an “insurance policy.” But insurance for whom, and against what? It is an insurance policy for the corporation against the loss of S’s unencumbered labor. Emre brilliantly catalogs the physical and emotional tolls of the IVF and egg-freezing process—the daily blood draws, the threat of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS), the fentanyl-hazed retrievals. Yet, she fails to draw the obvious conclusion: capitalism has found a way to commodify women’s anxieties about aging and career advancement, selling them back a highly medicalized, physically punishing "solution" that ultimately serves the employer's bottom line.

Shulamith Firestone, whom Emre cites admiringly, dreamed of cybernetic technologies freeing women from the tyranny of their biology. Firestone’s vision, however, was fundamentally socialist; it imagined these technologies being wielded in a post-capitalist utopia where the nuclear family had been abolished and childcare was fully collectivized. Divorced from this anti-capitalist framework, Firestone’s techno-optimism becomes a dystopian reality. When the means of reproduction are controlled by for-profit clinics and heavily leveraged tech companies, women are not liberated. They are merely tethered to a new master.

Furthermore, Emre’s essay glosses over the massive, unregulated profit motive driving the fertility industry. In the United States, ART is a multibillion-dollar industry characterized by aggressive marketing, opaque success rates, and exorbitant costs. B’s story—financing IVF through small loans and taking on massive debt—is framed by Emre as a poignant example of the invisible struggles of single women. But it is also a story of financial exploitation. The fertility industry preys on the desperate, biological desires of women like B, selling them expensive cycles of hope with statistically low probabilities of success (as evidenced by B’s final yield of three embryos and a 50 percent chance of implantation).

Emre argues that recognizing all reproduction as "assisted" could "close the gap between women of different classes, races, and sexes." But technology alone cannot close a gap created by capitalism; in fact, it often widens it. Because ART is so prohibitively expensive, it is overwhelmingly accessed by affluent, white, cisgender, heterosexual couples. While Emre does essential work in highlighting the queer and trans individuals (like N, K, and micha cárdenas) who navigate this system, their stories are largely characterized by exclusion, financial ruin, and medical trauma. This is not a glitch in the system; it is the system working exactly as designed. A healthcare model built on profit will always exclude the marginalized.

Dorothy Roberts’s framework of reproductive justice, which Emre invokes at the end of her essay, requires us to demand the right to have children in safe and sustainable environments. But if our vision of reproductive justice merely entails demanding universal access to the fertility industry's current offerings, we are aiming too low. We should not simply demand that the state or employers pay for us to undergo agonizing, highly medicalized procedures to bend our biological clocks to the demands of the forty-hour (or in Silicon Valley, eighty-hour) workweek. We must demand an economic system where women do not feel compelled to undergo such procedures in the first place—where society provides the material conditions necessary for people to have children when they want them, without sacrificing their careers or their financial stability.

To ignore the political economy of reproductive technology is to mistake a gilded cage for an open door. Until we decouple assisted reproduction from the predatory logic of late capitalism, the solidarity Emre hopes for will remain nothing more than a beautiful, elusive fantasy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Corporate perks like egg freezing are not feminist benefits but calculated HR strategies designed to delay motherhood and extract maximum labor from employees during their prime childbearing years.
  2. 02The burden of workplace incompatibility is unfairly shifted onto the individual woman’s body, forcing her to endure the physical and emotional risks of medical intervention to suit the employer's bottom line.
  3. 03Without the anti-capitalist framework originally proposed by Shulamith Firestone, reproductive technology becomes a dystopian tool of corporate control rather than a means of liberation.
  4. 04The unregulated and profit-driven fertility industry financially exploits the biological desires of women by selling expensive procedures with low success rates.
  5. 05Technology alone cannot bridge class and race gaps because the high cost of ART inherently reinforces existing social hierarchies and excludes the marginalized.
  6. 06True reproductive justice requires changing the economic system to provide the material conditions for childbearing, rather than simply expanding access to medicalized procedures that accommodate exploitative work cultures.
