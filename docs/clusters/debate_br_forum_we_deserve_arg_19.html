"Morozov’s concept of 'ecological reason' is flawed because it relies on a masculinist and individualistic model of intelligence that ignores the relational, interdependent, and social nature of care work."

Morozov’s essay is a brilliant polemic, but its vision of ecological reason rests on a curiously solitary figure: the Stone Age flâneur, strolling through a stonefield, “thinking preferably about nothing at all.” This image—drawn from Hans Otto Storm—is gendered, asocial, and utterly detached from the kinds of relationships that actually sustain human intelligence. If we are to take ecological reason seriously as an alternative to instrumental reason, we must first recognize that intelligence is not born in moments of idle curiosity alone. It arises in care, in interdependence, in the messy, embodied work of attending to others. A democratic AI must be designed not for the flâneur but for the caregiver, the community organizer, the parent, the nurse. Morozov’s framework, for all its radical pretensions, remains trapped in a masculinist imaginary of autonomous discovery.

Consider what is missing from the stonefield scene. The wanderer has no companions, no dependents, no one who depends on him for food or shelter. His curiosity is entirely self-directed; his goal is whatever the stone suggests. Contrast this with the daily reality of millions of people—disproportionately women—whose intelligence is exercised precisely in navigating the needs and desires of others. The mother who notices that her child’s cough has changed, the home health aide who senses a subtle shift in her patient’s mood, the neighbor who organizes a meal train for a grieving family: these are acts of profound, context-sensitive intelligence. They are not goal-oriented in the narrow sense of maximizing efficiency; they are oriented toward the flourishing of relationships. Yet they are also not the aimless play of the flâneur. They require attention, judgment, and a deep situated knowledge that is neither instrumental nor idle.

Morozov gestures toward intersubjectivity but never develops it. He notes, in passing, that Heidegger’s world of hammers and Dasein “might seem lonely” and that “similar reexaminations of our goals can be sparked by our interactions with each other.” Yet his own constructive proposal—ecological reason—remains stubbornly individualistic. Even his language app example is a project of personal self-improvement, built for one user, using privately owned services. The eolithic ethos, as he describes it, is about the relation between a person and a found object, not between persons. This is a striking absence for an essay that claims to be about democracy. Democracy is not just about the freedom to tinker; it is about the collective constitution of shared worlds. It requires technologies that support deliberation, mutual care, and the negotiation of common purposes.

Feminist science and technology studies has for decades offered exactly this kind of critique. Scholars like Lucy Suchman, Donna Haraway, and Madeleine Akrich have insisted that situated knowledges and relational ontologies must replace the figure of the autonomous rational actor. Suchman’s work on human-machine interaction, in particular, shows how even apparently simple tasks are woven into social and material networks of accountability. A system that amplifies ecological reason, on this view, would not be a workshop for the individual bricoleur but a platform for collective sense-making. It would make visible the relational infrastructure that sustains life—care networks, supply chains, ecological interdependencies—and invite people to deliberate about how to strengthen them.

If we take such a feminist reorientation seriously, the political project Morozov calls for must go beyond breaking with the Efficiency Lobby. It must also break with the patriarchal assumptions that equate intelligence with the solitary mind at play. This has concrete implications for AI design. Instead of building tools for personal productivity or curiosity, we might build systems that support mutual aid, community health, and cooperative decision-making. Imagine an AI that helps neighbors coordinate shared childcare, or that maps local food systems to identify gaps and redundancies. These would be eolithic in the sense of being open-ended and context-sensitive, but they would be rooted in care rather than individual discovery.

The Latin American experiments Morozov invokes offer partial inspiration, but they too were embedded in masculinist state-led projects. Cybersyn’s Operations Room was a space for (mostly male) managers and technicians; it was not designed for the domestic sphere or for frontline caregivers. To his credit, Morozov acknowledges that eolithism can be “excessively individualistic,” but he does not pursue the insight far enough. A truly ecological reason would recognize that human intelligence is always already distributed across bodies, genders, and species. It would draw on the tradition of care ethics, from Carol Gilligan to Joan Tronto, to ask how technology can help us attend better to one another and to the more-than-human world.

The greatest threat posed by AI is not that it operationalizes instrumental reason—though that is a serious concern—but that it intensifies what the feminist philosopher María Puig de la Bellacasa calls “carelessness.” When decisions are automated, when interactions are mediated by statistical patterns, the delicate attunement required for care is short-circuited. A democratic AI would resist this tendency, not by refusing automation altogether, but by designing systems that keep care work visible, valued, and under the control of those who do it. This requires a political economy that compensates care work fairly and a politics that amplifies the voices of caregivers in technological decision-making.

Morozov is right that we need a Humanity Lobby. But that lobby cannot be formed around the pleasures of the flâneur alone. It must be a coalition of the caring classes—nurses, teachers, domestic workers, parents, and all who labor to sustain life—demanding that technology serve the reproduction of a dignified, interdependent existence. Only then will we have an AI we can truly call our own.
