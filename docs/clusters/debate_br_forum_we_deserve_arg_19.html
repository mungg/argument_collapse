Morozov's Eolith and the Missing Workers

Evgeny Morozov has written the most intellectually ambitious piece on AI politics I have read this year, and I want to begin by saying what is too rarely said in these debates: he is largely right. The realist/refusenik dichotomy he sketches really does dominate the AI discourse, and both positions really do reify "AI" as a singular thing that one must accept or reject wholesale. His genealogy of teleological reasoning—from the General Problem Solver through the Coordinator to ChatGPT—is illuminating, and his recovery of Hans Otto Storm's eolithism is a genuine contribution. So is the reminder that the dream of computing as a tool for citizen deliberation was not always a fringe fantasy: Cybersyn, Bariloche, Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation" all happened, and they were defeated by tanks and structural adjustment loans, not by any technical impossibility.

And yet. For all his political sharpness, Morozov's essay has a curious lacuna at its center: the people who would actually do the work of constructing an "ecological reason" infrastructure are almost entirely absent. The Stormian flâneur strolls alone through the stonefield. Allende's workers appear briefly as managers in the Operations Room before fading back into the historical record. The "Humanity Lobby" is invoked as something that ought to exist but is never given social flesh. This is not a small omission. It is the omission that has haunted every left technology critique since the New Left, and it explains why Morozov's conclusion—"democratic AI requires actual democracy"—lands as a slogan rather than a strategy.

Consider what is actually happening in 2024 around AI labor. The Writers Guild of America just won, in their 2023 strike, the first major labor contract in any industry that explicitly constrains generative AI use. SAG-AFTRA followed. The Authors Guild is suing OpenAI. Kenyan workers who labeled toxic content for ChatGPT at $2 an hour have organized and gone public. Artists are mounting legal challenges to training-data scraping. None of these struggles fit cleanly into either the realist or refusenik camps Morozov describes, and none of them get a mention in his essay. They are precisely the kind of "problem-posing" politics he says we need—and they are happening, right now, without waiting for a Storm revival.

The point is not that Morozov ignores labor out of malice. The point is that his theoretical frame, focused on the cognitive history of "instrumental reason" and its alternatives, tends to locate politics in the realm of paradigm and Weltanschauung rather than in the realm of organized social conflict. When he writes that "we should turn ecological reason into a full-fledged research paradigm," the verb "we" is doing extraordinary work. Who is the "we" here? Critical theorists? Sympathetic computer scientists? Foundations? The state? Morozov knows perfectly well, having spent two decades arguing this point, that paradigms do not change because intellectuals propose better ones. They change because constituencies with material interests force them to change.

This matters because the eolithic moment Morozov celebrates—the flâneur stumbling on the stone, the language-app builder noticing a feature—is not actually politically neutral. It is, as he half-acknowledges in his self-criticism, a leisure-class activity. Storm's wanderer is, in Storm's own words, "fed, contented, thinking preferably about nothing at all." The capacity for idle curiosity is materially produced. It requires that someone else has done the cooking, the cleaning, the labeling of training data. Veblen knew this; that is what The Theory of the Leisure Class is about. An ecological reason worth having is one that universalizes the material conditions for idle curiosity, not one that merely valorizes the cognitive style of those who already possess it.

Here Morozov's essay would benefit enormously from confrontation with feminist work on social reproduction and care, which has been arguing for decades that "intelligence" understood as goal-directed problem-solving systematically devalues exactly the open-ended, contextual, relational forms of cognition his essay rehabilitates under the name "ecological reason." Joan Tronto, Nancy Fraser, Silvia Federici—none appear in Morozov's pantheon, which leans heavily on male pragmatists and male AI critics. This is not a complaint about representation; it is a complaint about analytic resources. The most sophisticated existing literature on non-instrumental reason is care-ethics literature, and it has the additional virtue of being attached to actually-existing political movements (care workers' unions, domestic workers' organizing, the social-reproduction-strike traditions of Latin American feminism).

I would also push Morozov on the political-economic plausibility of his alternative. He proposes "public, solidarity-based, and socialized infrastructural alternatives" to commercial generative AI. Wonderful. But the energy and compute costs of state-of-the-art models are enormous—a single training run for a frontier model now exceeds $100 million, and inference costs are non-trivial. Either the public alternative subsidizes this at vast taxpayer expense, or it accepts smaller, less capable models, or it embraces a fundamentally different research program. Morozov gestures at the third option ("surely there are far more technologies for fostering human intelligence than have been dreamt of by our prevailing philosophy") but does not specify what they might be. This is the question that any serious "ecological reason" research program would have to answer first: what are the technical artifacts that embody this paradigm? Cybersyn had its Cyberstride and CHECO; Varsavsky had his simulation models. What does the 2024 equivalent look like? Without a concrete technical proposal, the essay risks being what Morozov himself has elsewhere criticized: a humanistic gesture toward "alternatives" that exists primarily in the conditional mood.

Let me end on the note where I started. Morozov's essay is valuable precisely because it refuses the false choice between accommodation and refusal that has stifled AI discourse since ChatGPT's release. The eolith is a useful figure; the genealogy is illuminating; the Latin American examples are essential. But to convert this excellent essay into a political project, we need to ask what Morozov does not quite ask: which constituencies have an interest in ecological reason, what organizational forms could carry that interest into struggle, and which existing fights—over training data, over labor displacement, over public-sector procurement, over compute infrastructure—offer footholds for the larger transformation he envisions. The answers will not come from philosophy. They will come from organizers who treat Morozov's essay as a useful provocation rather than a finished program. I count myself among them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Morozov’s essay suffers from a 'curious lacuna' by ignoring the actual workers and labor unions currently organizing against generative AI, such as the WGA and Kenyan content moderators.
  2. 02The 'ecological reason' and 'idle curiosity' Morozov celebrates are leisure-class activities that depend on material conditions produced by the very labor his essay overlooks.
  3. 03The essay fails to engage with feminist scholarship on social reproduction and care ethics, which offers more robust analytic resources for understanding non-instrumental, relational forms of intelligence.
  4. 04Morozov provides no concrete technical or economic roadmap for how public, socialized AI infrastructure could realistically compete with or replace the high-cost models of the 'Efficiency Lobby.'
  5. 05The transition from a 'realist/refusenik' dichotomy to a transformative political project requires identifying specific constituencies and organizational forms rather than relying on philosophical paradigms alone.
