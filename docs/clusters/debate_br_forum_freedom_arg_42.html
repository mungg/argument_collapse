"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies institution-building as the key to reclaiming freedom, his vision is too focused on top-down state reforms and must instead prioritize the reconstruction of local, face-to-face associations in civil society."

Aziz Rana’s central claim—that the struggle for an inclusive, solidaristic freedom requires not clever communications strategy but the slow, patient construction of institutions where collective self-rule is actually lived—is a much-needed insight in a political culture obsessed with messaging. But reading his essay, one cannot help but notice a certain statism lurking within his vision of institution-building. The institutions he foregrounds are national-level reforms: the PRO Act, voting rights legislation, defense budget reallocation, sweeping immigration law changes. While all these have their place, they tend to reinforce a top-down model of social transformation that may inadvertently reproduce the very feelings of powerlessness and abstraction that plague the left’s freedom talk. A truly solidaristic freedom, I would argue, must be rooted much more deeply in the local, the communal, and the face-to-face—in the institutions of civil society that stand between the individual and the state and that are most capable of fostering the habits of shared responsibility and mutual respect.

Rana is right that Americans experience freedom more as a theory than a reality because they lack everyday sites of collective agency. The answer, however, is not simply to make the federal government more democratic and to mandate workers’ collective bargaining. Those are macro-level instruments that, at best, set the stage. What is missing from his analysis is an appreciation of the thick fabric of intermediate associations—neighborhood councils, community land trusts, cooperative enterprises, mutual aid networks, congregational communities, and civic organizations—that once provided many Americans with a tangible experience of self-governance. It was in these settings, not in the polling booth, that the connection between personal freedom and the common good was forged and sustained. The historian Alexis de Tocqueville famously identified this associational life as the distinguishing feature of American democracy, and its erosion is at the heart of our present discontent.

Rana’s historical narrative actually points in this direction, though he does not pursue it. He notes that the early American ideal of freedom was one of self-mastery and control over the basic conditions of life, and that this ideal was perverted by the settler project into a racialized zero-sum logic. But he also touches on moments when that ideal was, for brief periods, redirected toward solidarity—most notably in the labor movement’s building of unions, which were not merely bargaining units but dense social worlds, complete with halls, picnics, newspapers, and educational programs. The decline of unions is not only a matter of labor law; it is a decline of a whole way of life. Similarly, the early twentieth-century New Deal opening was preceded and accompanied by a remarkable flourishing of local self-help and cooperative movements, from the farmers’ cooperatives of the Plains to the mutualistas of Mexican-American communities. These were not just instrumental vehicles for policy demands; they were laboratories of democratic practice where people learned to deliberate, to compromise, and to exercise power collectively.

Today, the left’s institution-building energies should flow toward the reconstruction of this associational infrastructure. The policy wins Rana advocates—expanding union rights, reforming the electoral system—are important enablers, but they will fail to transform culture unless they are embedded in a dense network of community-based institutions. Efforts like the Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative in Boston, which put residents in control of redeveloping their own neighborhood, or the Evergreen Cooperatives in Cleveland, which link anchor institutions to worker-owned businesses, model what a grounded, solidaristic freedom looks like. They do not begin with a grand narrative about imperialism and intersectional solidarity; they begin with a particular place, concrete needs, and a commitment to doing things together rather than outsourcing decisions to distant markets or bureaucracies. That lived experience, multiplied across thousands of communities, is what would organically tie the word “freedom” to practices of mutual respect and common concern.

A communitarian approach also addresses one of Rana’s deepest concerns—the toxic linkage of freedom with racial resentment—in a way that top-down national policy cannot. When people of different backgrounds work together to manage a community garden, run a parent-teacher association, or govern a worker co-op, they develop what the sociologist Robert Putnam calls “bridging social capital.” The zero-sum perception that another’s gain is my loss begins to dissolve not through abstract moral exhortation but through repeated experiences of shared fate. The right’s cynical manipulation of racial fear thrives on isolation and ignorance; a thick web of local associations that cross lines of race and class is a powerful antidote. This is not to romanticize localism, which has its own history of exclusion, but to insist that the struggle over meaning happens in the everyday, and that it is there that the battle must be won.

This is not an argument against national or global action. It is an argument about sequence and priority. The left has spent decades perfecting its policy platforms and its media critique. What it has lost is the capacity to operate at the level of the lived neighborhood, the parish, the shop floor. Rebuilding that capacity—through training community organizers, funding cooperative development, reforming zoning codes to allow communal living arrangements, and protecting the right to protest and assemble—should be seen not as an add-on to a legislative agenda but as its core. When people feel that they are genuinely shaping the world around them, however local, the language of freedom ceases to be a cynical trope of the powerful and becomes a description of daily life.

Rana’s essay concludes that there is “no end-run around the hard political work of movement and coalition building.” Precisely so. But the most enduring movements have always been rooted in local institutional life—the Black church, the union hall, the settlement house. The left’s freedom project, if it is to be more than electoral sloganeering, must turn its attention there. It must trust that the seeds of a new society are planted not in the speech of a candidate but in the soil of a community that is learning, imperfectly and patiently, to govern itself.
