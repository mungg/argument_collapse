"To successfully dethrone fossil capital, climate strategy must move beyond technocratic policy and center organized labor through enforceable material guarantees and worker-led planning."

Táíwò’s argument is persuasive in its diagnosis of fossil capital’s power, but it needs a more grounded account of workers. The phrase “dethrone fossil capital” can sound abstractly correct while leaving unanswered the question every miner, refinery worker, pipefitter, power plant operator, truck driver, and fossil-dependent town will ask: dethrone it how, and what happens to us on Monday morning?

Climate politics has often treated labor as either a moral constituency to be protected after the real policy is designed or a conservative obstacle to be overcome. Both approaches are mistakes. Workers are not decorations on a transition plan. They are the people who build, maintain, operate, and sometimes block the energy system. If the first step is to defeat fossil capital, organized labor must be one of the forces doing the defeating. That will not happen unless the transition offers power, not pity.

The fossil economy is a labor regime as well as an energy regime. Its jobs are often dangerous and environmentally destructive, but many are unionized, skilled, and comparatively well-paid. They support municipal tax bases, schools, diners, churches, Little League teams, and mortgages. For generations, the bargain in many places has been brutal but intelligible: accept risk, pollution, boom-bust cycles, and corporate domination in exchange for wages and community identity. When climate advocates say those industries must decline, they are not merely proposing a change in energy inputs. They are threatening a whole local social order.

Fossil companies understand this and exploit it. They place workers in front of shareholders. They present pipelines as jobs programs, drilling as patriotism, and regulation as an attack on families. Often they are cynical: the same companies automate, subcontract, fight unions, abandon wells, and leave communities with polluted land. But cynicism does not make the jobs imaginary. A climate movement that responds only with aggregate statistics—more clean jobs than fossil jobs—misses the point. Jobs are not interchangeable units. A solar installation job in another state does not replace a refinery job with a pension. A temporary construction job does not replace a career. A promise of retraining does not replace collective bargaining power.

This is why any two-step strategy must begin with labor guarantees. Not vague “just transition” language, but enforceable rights: wage replacement, pension protection, health care, bridge-to-retirement programs, union neutrality in clean energy, project labor agreements, prevailing wages, local hiring, public employment options, and community benefit agreements. Workers in fossil industries should have first claim on decommissioning, remediation, grid construction, offshore wind, geothermal drilling, carbon management where appropriate, and industrial retrofits. Communities losing fossil tax revenue should receive long-term fiscal support, not competitive grants that pit towns against one another.

The New York Build Public Renewables Act, which Táíwò praises, matters partly because of its labor language. Climate legislation without labor standards can become a low-wage construction boom that alienates the very workers needed for political durability. Public power should mean public responsibility: union jobs, apprenticeship pipelines, safety standards, and democratic oversight. If clean energy is built on precarious labor, it will not be emancipatory and it may not be politically stable.

But the labor question also complicates the simple opposition between “all of the above” politics and climate seriousness. Many politicians use “all of the above” to avoid hard choices and protect fossil donors. That should be criticized. Yet energy systems cannot be transformed overnight, and workers know this. Grids require reliability. Industrial facilities have long investment cycles. Rural areas have different transportation needs than dense cities. Some fossil infrastructure will be retired before the end of its technical life; some may be needed for limited periods during transition; some may need to be repurposed. A credible climate program must distinguish between managed decline and magical shutdown.

The word “managed” is crucial. Unmanaged decline is what capital does to workers. It closes a plant, declares bankruptcy, raids pensions, externalizes cleanup, and leaves. Managed decline is what democratic power should do instead: set a schedule, secure funds, assign responsibilities, protect workers, and plan replacement industries before collapse. Fossil capital resists managed decline because it limits profit and exposes liabilities. That is why defeating fossil capital and empowering workers are the same fight.

One instrument should be a fossil transition authority with real money and labor representation. Its job would not be to write reports; it would plan region by region. Which facilities retire when? Which workers are affected? What are their skills? What infrastructure can be reused? What industries make sense locally? How will the tax base be replaced? What environmental remediation is needed? What union agreements govern new projects? This authority should include unions, local governments, Indigenous nations, environmental justice groups, and technical experts. Without such planning, transition becomes a slogan imposed from metropolitan centers.

The climate movement should also embrace industrial policy more explicitly. The transition requires manufacturing: transformers, heat pumps, batteries, railcars, buses, turbines, solar components, electrolyzers, insulation, high-voltage cables, and equipment for factories that produce low-carbon steel and cement. If those supply chains are left entirely to global low-cost competition, workers in wealthy countries will be told to support decarbonization while seeing the jobs go elsewhere, and workers in poorer countries may be subjected to exploitation. A pro-labor climate strategy would build manufacturing capacity with strong standards and international solidarity rather than nationalist resentment.

This is where unions must change too. Some labor leaders have defended fossil projects reflexively, even when long-term prospects were poor. That is understandable but not sustainable. If unions become the last line of defense for dying carbon assets, they will tie their members’ future to companies that will discard them. Labor should bargain over the transition, not deny it. The demand should be: no closures without worker protection; no clean energy without union labor; no public subsidy without public benefit; no community sacrifice without community power.

There is a deeper democratic point here. Workers possess knowledge that climate policy needs. Power plant operators understand reliability. Electricians understand bottlenecks in installation. Construction workers understand timelines and safety. Truck drivers understand logistics. Farmworkers understand heat and water stress. Nurses understand disaster health impacts. Teachers understand how climate anxiety reaches children. A state-centered climate politics that treats people only as beneficiaries will fail. People must be co-designers.

Táíwò’s two-step frame is useful because it identifies fossil capital as an adversary. But the coalition capable of dethroning fossil capital cannot be built only from climate activists, academics, and progressive politicians. It must include those who currently produce and maintain the fossil world. That is not a sentimental appeal to unity. It is a material fact. Workers can slow extraction through strikes, expose safety violations, refuse unsafe work, demand remediation, build clean infrastructure, and lend legitimacy to phaseout. Or, if alienated, they can become a mass base for reactionary climate politics.

The right understands this. It offers fossil workers recognition, even if it cannot offer a future. It says: you built this country; environmentalists hate you; elites want to take your truck, stove, and job. Much of that is manipulation, but it lands because liberal climate politics has too often been technocratic and culturally dismissive. A left climate politics should answer differently: you built the old energy system; you should build and govern the new one; the companies that endangered your lungs and pensions are the enemy, not your children’s demand for a livable planet.

This also means climate advocates should be careful with rhetoric about consumption. It is true that wealthy societies must change patterns of energy use. But moralizing about individual behavior can easily become class-coded: trucks, meat, and gas stoves become symbols of backwardness, while frequent flying, large homes, and financial portfolios escape scrutiny. A labor climate politics would focus on systems: housing that lowers bills, transit that improves mobility, food systems that support farmers and workers, and public goods that make low-carbon living easier rather than more punitive.

The state is indispensable, as Táíwò argues. But the state will not align itself with the people unless the people are organized at work and in their communities. Public power without worker power can become bureaucracy. Industrial policy without unions can become corporate welfare. Climate planning without local democracy can become imposition. The route to defeating fossil capital runs through collective bargaining, public ownership, community control, and enforceable guarantees.

A livable future will be built by workers or not at all. Dethroning fossil capital is a worthy first step. But the hand that removes the crown must be organized labor joined with climate justice movements, not state managers acting on workers’ behalf. Otherwise the transition will inherit the old hierarchy: executives decide, politicians announce, workers absorb the shock. That is the fossil order in green paint.
