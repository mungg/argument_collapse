"While institutional reform is necessary to address inequality, it must go beyond David Grusky’s focus on market competition to include the restoration of workers' countervailing power and the maintenance of progressive taxation."

David Grusky is right to insist that inequality is not merely a problem of tax rates. The startling rise in pre-tax inequality should make us wary of any politics that imagines the market as a neutral machine that produces incomes and the state as an outside actor that later tidies up the mess. Markets are made. They are structured by law, custom, bargaining power, corporate governance, monetary policy, intellectual property rules, immigration rules, public education, zoning, union law, and the rules governing finance. If those institutions are producing extreme inequality, then we should examine them directly.

But Grusky’s language of “market repair” risks being too narrow. The problem is not only that American markets are insufficiently competitive. It is also that American workers have lost countervailing power.

For much of the postwar period, the United States did not have anything like a perfectly competitive labor market. It had large firms, administered wages, internal labor markets, relatively strong unions in core industries, and a political consensus that mass prosperity required rising wages. That system had many exclusions and injustices, especially by race and gender, and no one should romanticize it. Still, it produced a more compressed wage structure than we have today. The decline of private-sector unionism, the erosion of the minimum wage, the fissuring of employment through subcontracting and temporary work, and the threat of offshoring have all weakened ordinary workers’ ability to claim a share of productivity gains.

A purely competitive labor market is not necessarily a worker-friendly labor market. If millions of workers are interchangeable and desperate, competition can push wages down, not up. Grusky’s educational bottleneck argument captures an important part of the story: unequal access to good schooling sorts children into unequal futures, and expanding real opportunity would reduce inequality. But education cannot be the whole answer. The United States has many low-wage jobs that will remain low-wage unless labor standards and bargaining institutions change. More college graduates will not by itself make home health aides, janitors, restaurant workers, warehouse workers, and retail clerks powerful enough to bargain for decent wages. Nor can every society educate its way out of the need to decide whether people who do necessary work deserve a secure livelihood.

This is where unions matter. They are not simply “interferences” with markets; they are institutions that make labor markets more democratic. They reduce arbitrary managerial power, raise wages for non-college workers, compress wage differentials, and create a political constituency for social insurance. The collapse of union density is therefore not a side issue in the inequality debate. It is one of the central institutional changes of the last generation.

If we take Grusky’s institutional critique seriously, labor law belongs at the center of the reform agenda. Current law gives employers strong incentives to resist organizing, delay elections, intimidate workers, and treat penalties as a cost of doing business. The formal right to organize exists, but the practical ability to do so is badly compromised. Repairing this market would mean making union representation a real option, not a theoretical one. It might mean card check or faster elections, meaningful penalties for illegal retaliation, sectoral bargaining in industries where firm-by-firm organizing is nearly impossible, and stronger protections for workers in subcontracted workplaces.

Similarly, the minimum wage should not be dismissed as old-fashioned redistribution. It is a wage-setting institution. Its real value has been allowed to erode even as productivity and top incomes have risen. Raising it would not solve inequality at the top, but it would set a floor beneath destructive competition at the bottom. Wage theft enforcement, overtime protections, predictable scheduling rules, and portable benefits would do the same. These are not merely transfers; they shape the market in which incomes are generated.

Grusky is strongest when he discusses CEO pay. The idea that executive compensation is always the product of arm’s-length bargaining strains credulity. Boards often have social and professional ties to the executives whose pay they approve. Compensation consultants benchmark against peers in ways that ratchet pay upward. Shareholders are dispersed, and workers have no formal voice. Reform should include stronger shareholder rights, greater independence of compensation committees, and perhaps worker representation on boards. Germany’s codetermination system is not an alien socialist fantasy; it is an institutional arrangement within a successful capitalist economy. Giving employees a voice in corporate governance would be a direct challenge to the “foxes guarding the henhouse” problem Grusky identifies.

Where I part company with Grusky is in his suggestion that institutional reform is politically easier than taxation because it can be presented as fair competition. Entrenched interests are not likely to surrender rents simply because reformers use market-friendly language. Employers who oppose unions will not become converts when told that unions equalize bargaining power. Elite universities will not happily expand access if doing so dilutes scarcity and prestige. Corporate boards will resist constraints on compensation. Finance will fight rules that reduce its profits. Institutional repair is not a technocratic substitute for class politics; it is class politics by other means.

Nor should taxes be demoted too far. A progressive tax system funds the very institutions that make equal opportunity possible: early childhood programs, schools, universities, health care, transportation, and income supports. It also limits the conversion of economic power into political power. Even a well-repaired market will generate unequal outcomes, and democratic societies have every right to decide how much inequality they are willing to tolerate.

The best response to inequality is therefore not taxes instead of institutions, or institutions instead of taxes. It is predistribution and redistribution together: strengthen labor standards and bargaining power before the paycheck is written; tax progressively after it is written; and invest the proceeds in the capabilities of the next generation. Occupy Wall Street’s genius was not that it produced a white paper. It was that it named a system in which wealth buys rules, rules generate wealth, and ordinary citizens are told to call the result freedom. Breaking that cycle will require more than market repair. It will require democratic power.
