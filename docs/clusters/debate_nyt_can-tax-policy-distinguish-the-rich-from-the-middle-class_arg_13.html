"The federal tax code already possesses the mechanisms to distinguish the rich from the middle class, but it currently uses those mechanisms to disproportionately benefit high earners through regressive tax expenditures."

We don’t need to invent new ways to distinguish the rich from the middle class in the tax code. We need to stop pretending the current code doesn’t already make that distinction, powerfully and perversely, in favor of the wealthy. The real question is whether we have the political courage to reverse it. Nearly every major tax expenditure—the mortgage interest deduction, the exclusion of employer-sponsored health insurance, the preferential rates on capital gains, and the sacrosanct 529 plans themselves—provides its largest benefits, in absolute dollars, to the top income quintile. A household earning over $200,000 receives an average annual tax subsidy for housing and health care that dwarfs what a middle-income family gets, because the subsidy is pegged to the size of the mortgage, the cost of the health plan, and the marginal tax rate. Eliminating or capping these expenditures would automatically make the code distinguish the rich from the middle class by removing the invisible architecture that currently shields high earners. The 529 proposal failed not because it was unfair, but because it threatened a concrete benefit that affluent families had already internalized as part of their financial planning. That political reality underscores the true obstacle: not a lack of policy instruments, but a collective unwillingness to ask something of the professional class. The heavy lifting of distinguishing “rich” from “middle” can be done by capping all itemized deductions at a dollar limit and converting the mortgage interest deduction into a flat credit. These moves would raise revenue almost exclusively from the top 10 percent while leaving the middle class’s reliance on the standard deduction untouched. The tax code already knows who is rich; it simply refuses to act on that knowledge.
