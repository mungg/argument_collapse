"A truly inclusive reproductive politics must support the right of queer and trans people to pursue biological or conventional family-building without forcing them to meet a standard of political subversiveness or "purity.""

One of the quiet strengths of the lead essay is its refusal to demand that queer and trans people reproduce queerly enough. This may seem like a small matter compared with insurance coverage, medical risk, or legal parentage, but it cuts deeply. People whose lives already unsettle gender and kinship norms are often asked to make their most intimate decisions into political demonstrations. If a lesbian wants a child genetically related to her partner, is she capitulating to biology? If a trans woman wants to bank sperm, is she affirming a reproductive logic that misgenders her? If a queer couple wants a baby who is “a mixture of us two,” are they betraying chosen family? The lead essay answers, correctly: no one owes the movement a perfectly subversive family.

This matters because queer politics has long contained an ambivalence about reproduction. For understandable reasons, many queer theorists and activists have criticized marriage, biological kinship, inheritance, domestic privacy, and the idealization of the child. The family has been a site of violence for many LGBT people. It has expelled queer children, disciplined gender, privatized dependency, and rewarded conformity. The AIDS crisis taught many queer communities to invent care networks where families and governments failed. “Chosen family” is not a slogan; it is a survival practice.

But survival practices should not become purity tests. The fact that queer people have made kinship beyond biology does not mean they must renounce biology. The fact that heterosexual culture sentimentalizes genetic relatedness does not mean queer desire for genetic relatedness is false consciousness. The fact that marriage equality became the public face of LGBT politics, sometimes at the expense of poverty, prisons, health care, and trans survival, does not mean every queer person who marries or has a child is a neoliberal subject in need of correction.

The stories of N and K, Joanne Spataro and Lara, and micha cárdenas show how reproductive desire can be both ordinary and politically charged. N and K want what many couples want: to have a child, perhaps more than one, and to see something of themselves in that child. Their problem is not that this desire is too conventional. Their problem is that medicine, law, and insurance are still organized around a model of reproduction that treats them as exceptions. Doctors assume they are friends. A resident asks for a semen sample from a nonexistent husband. Acquaintances ask why they do not “just adopt,” as if adoption were a simple consolation prize for people whose reproductive desires make others uncomfortable.

The adoption question is especially revealing. Straight couples experiencing infertility may be asked about adoption, but the question has a different force when directed at queer people. It often means: why are you insisting on biology when your family is already artificial? Why not perform altruism instead of desire? Why should you need what normal people need? The question also ignores the realities of adoption and foster care: cost, racial politics, trauma, legal uncertainty, and the fact that many systems privilege married, affluent, heterosexual applicants. To say “just adopt” is to treat existing children as solutions to adult discomfort rather than as people embedded in their own histories and losses.

Trans fertility raises another set of contradictions. For years, medical transition has been presented as though it requires renouncing reproduction. Doctors have told trans people, often inaccurately or incompletely, that hormones will make them sterile. Fertility preservation has not been consistently offered before transition, especially to young people. Insurance coverage is patchy. Clinics can be alienating. Yet when trans people do preserve fertility or try to conceive, public reaction becomes prurient and hostile. They are told, in effect, that their bodies are impossible: too reproductive to be truly trans, too trans to reproduce legitimately.

This is where the lead essay’s anti-naturalism does important work. “Natural” reproduction is often code for reproductive legibility. A cisgender man and woman have sex, the woman becomes pregnant, and the resulting child is read as the natural expression of sexual difference. But even that scene is culturally staged and medically supported. Meanwhile, a trans woman and her cis woman partner may use sperm and eggs—the allegedly most basic ingredients of “old-fashioned” reproduction—and still provoke outrage because their genders disrupt the story attached to those cells. The biology is not what scandalizes people. The failure of biology to enforce gender hierarchy is what scandalizes them.

A queer reproductive politics should therefore separate three things that are too often fused: bodies, identities, and parental roles. Sperm does not make someone a father. Eggs do not make someone a mother. Gestation does not exhaust motherhood. A person can contribute genetic material without occupying the gendered role historically associated with it. A trans woman who banks sperm is not less a woman. A non-gestational lesbian mother is not less a mother. A trans man who becomes pregnant is not less a man. Law and medicine should be built around consent, intention, and care, not around assumptions derived from gametes.

Practically, this requires reforms that are not glamorous but are essential. Fertility clinics should intake patients without assuming heterosexuality or cisgender identity. Forms should ask for relevant anatomy and desired language, not “husband” and “wife.” Staff should be trained to recognize partners, not erase them. Insurance coverage should not require proof of intercourse-based infertility. Fertility preservation should be covered for trans people before medical transition, just as it is often covered for cancer patients facing fertility-threatening treatment. Parentage law should allow children born to same-sex and trans couples to have secure legal relationships with their parents from birth, across state lines and without expensive litigation.

At the same time, queer reproductive politics should not stop at access to ART. If we merely demand entry into expensive clinics, we leave intact a system that excludes poor queer people, undocumented queer people, disabled queer people, and many queer people of color. The right to form a family must include housing, health care, protection from violence, parental leave, and freedom from child welfare systems that disproportionately police poverty. A trans woman’s fertility matters, but so does her survival. A lesbian couple’s IVF coverage matters, but so does whether they can afford rent and whether their child’s school is safe.

The lead essay’s use of cárdenas is powerful because it ties fertility to mortality. For many trans women of color, reproductive time is not simply a biological clock; it is shadowed by violence, poverty, policing, and inadequate health care. To speak of “family planning” without speaking of the conditions that allow people to stay alive is grotesquely incomplete. Reproductive justice for queer and trans people must mean not only the chance to make babies but the chance to live long enough to raise them.

Still, I want to defend the ordinariness of queer reproductive desire. There is something moving in Spataro’s image of a child as two kinds of sand blended in a glass. It is sentimental, yes. It borrows from a romance of biological mixture that queer politics has good reason to question. But why should queer people be barred from sentiment? Why should the desire to see a lover’s face in a child belong only to straight couples? The aim of queer liberation cannot be to abolish every longing that has also been cherished by heterosexuals. It should be to detach those longings from coercion and hierarchy.

“All reproduction is assisted” means that no family is self-evidently natural and no family should have to earn legitimacy by being radical. Some queer people will build chosen families without children. Some will adopt. Some will foster. Some will use donors, surrogates, IVF, sex, co-parenting agreements, or arrangements for which we still lack names. Some will care for friends, elders, students, neighbors, and lovers rather than children. The task is not to rank these forms from most to least queer. The task is to build a world in which each can be pursued without shame, poverty, medical insult, or legal danger.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Queer people should not be shamed for desiring biological kinship or genetic relatedness simply because those concepts have historically been used to marginalize them.
  2. 02The common suggestion that queer people should "just adopt" is a discriminatory double standard that ignores the systemic barriers and complexities of the adoption and foster care systems.
  3. 03Medical and legal systems must be restructured to recognize parental roles based on consent and intention rather than assumptions derived from gendered gametes or heteronormative definitions of infertility.
  4. 04Transgender healthcare should treat fertility preservation as a standard component of transition-related care, similar to how it is offered to cancer patients.
  5. 05Reproductive justice for the LGBTQ+ community must extend beyond clinical access to include the material conditions—such as safety from violence and economic security—that allow people to actually raise their children.
  6. 06The goal of queer liberation should be to detach ordinary human desires for family and connection from the hierarchies of the "natural" rather than abolishing those desires altogether.
