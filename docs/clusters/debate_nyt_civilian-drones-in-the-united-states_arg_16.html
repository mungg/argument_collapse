"The government must implement specific regulations on drone altitude and permitting to protect privacy, as drone technology presents a unique threat that existing legal doctrines cannot address."

The question framed here—privacy outdoors versus indoors—is a false dichotomy that lets drone operators off too easily. While it's true that courts have long held that activities visible from public spaces carry diminished privacy expectations, the drone problem is qualitatively different from a person walking by your house. A drone can hover at treetop level, peer over fences, and linger in ways that no passerby naturally would. The technology has outpaced the doctrine, and law needs to catch up.

Congress should establish clear altitude floors below which drones cannot operate without explicit property-owner consent. Forty feet seems reasonable—high enough for legitimate aviation safety purposes, low enough to prevent intimate surveillance. Additionally, any drone equipped with recording devices should require a permit, much like a film crew needs locations releases. This isn't an assault on innovation; it's the basic regulatory infrastructure that accompanies every other aviation technology.

Critics will argue this stifles a promising industry. But the same warnings were issued about automobiles, radio, and commercial aviation—and in each case, reasonable regulation didn't prevent growth, it channeled it. The agricultural and film industries can adapt. Meanwhile, the alternative—treating every backyard barbecue and family moment as fair game for aerial surveillance—erodes a privacy norm that the Supreme Court itself has recognized as constitutionally significant. We don't have to choose between drones and privacy, but we do have to act before the Wild West dynamic becomes permanent.

The FAA's ongoing rulemaking process offers the perfect vehicle. Now is the moment to shape those rules. Once industry investment solidifies and drones become ubiquitous, regulatory inertia becomes nearly insurmountable.

  1. 01Drones are qualitatively different from traditional public observation because they can hover and linger in ways that bypass physical barriers like fences.

  3. 03Drones equipped with recording devices should be subject to a permitting process similar to professional film crews.
  4. 04Reasonable regulation will not stifle the drone industry, but rather channel its growth as it has for other major technological innovations.
  5. 05Immediate regulatory action is necessary to prevent the erosion of privacy norms before the technology becomes too ubiquitous to control.
