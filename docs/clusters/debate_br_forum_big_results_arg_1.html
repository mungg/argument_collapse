"While behavioral insights and the removal of user fees are essential for improving public health, these interventions can only succeed if they are integrated into robust, reliably financed, and well-governed national health systems."

Glennerster and Kremer are right to attack the casual imposition of user fees on poor households. In preventive health especially, charging a few coins can be the difference between broad protection and spotty adoption. If a mosquito net, vaccine, deworming pill, or chlorine dispenser produces benefits not only for the user but for neighbors, then free or heavily subsidized provision is not charity; it is sound public health economics. Yet from the vantage point of health systems, the most difficult question begins where their essay leaves off: who will deliver these free goods, repeatedly, fairly, and well?

Public health is not simply a collection of commodities. It is a system of trust, logistics, labor, surveillance, finance, and accountability. Bed nets must be procured and distributed. Vaccines must be kept cold. Health workers must show up. Chlorine dispensers must be refilled. Mothers must believe that clinics are safe places. Data must be collected accurately. Stockouts must be prevented. When any link fails, demand-side insight cannot rescue the intervention.

The essay’s examples are compelling precisely because they combine a proven technology with a clever delivery mechanism. The Rajasthan immunization program did not only give mothers lentils; it also improved the reliability of immunization camps. The chlorine dispenser did not only make chlorine free; it changed where and how water treatment occurred. These successes should not be summarized merely as “small incentives work.” They worked because a behavioral insight was embedded in a functioning delivery arrangement.

That distinction matters for policy. Many countries have announced free health care with great fanfare, only to find that facilities run out of drugs, workers demand informal payments, or quality collapses under increased demand. User fees are bad rationing devices, but they often arise in systems starved of predictable public funding. A clinic may use fees to buy fuel, repair a roof, or supplement wages. A nurse may depend on them because salary payments are late. Removing fees without replacing revenue can injure the very service the poor are now encouraged to use.

This is not an argument for keeping fees. It is an argument for taking abolition seriously. If preventive interventions should be free, then governments and donors must finance the recurrent costs, not only the initial campaign. They must pay workers, monitor attendance, maintain supply chains, and prevent informal charges. “Free” at the point of use is a system design, not a price tag.

The history of primary health care offers a caution. Since Alma-Ata in 1978, the world has repeatedly rediscovered the importance of basic, community-based services. But the promise of “Health for All” was often undermined by fiscal crises, vertical donor programs, and weak national systems. Selective primary health care then focused attention on cost-effective interventions such as immunization and oral rehydration therapy. These saved lives, but sometimes at the expense of broader system strengthening. Today’s enthusiasm for randomized evaluations could repeat that pattern if it treats each intervention as a separable product rather than part of a public health platform.

Consider immunization. A small incentive can dramatically increase completion rates where mothers face time costs and procrastination. But the long-term success of immunization depends on routine systems, not episodic inducements alone. Children are born every day; vaccine schedules are continuous; disease surveillance must detect outbreaks; communities must trust health authorities. Incentives may help families overcome immediate barriers, but no country has achieved durable immunization coverage by incentives alone. It requires a public apparatus that makes vaccination ordinary.

The same is true of water. Chlorine dispensers at communal sources are a brilliant adaptation to rural conditions. They reduce packaging costs, create a reminder at the moment of collection, and permit social reinforcement. But they remain a second-best response to unsafe water infrastructure. In the long run, safe water and sanitation require pipes, wells, drainage, toilets, regulation, and maintenance. Point-of-use treatment can avert diarrhea now; it should not become an excuse to defer public investment in environmental health.

One should also be careful with incentives in health care. Small rewards can overcome present bias, but health behaviors are not all alike. Some are one-time or periodic actions, such as vaccination. Others require daily routines, such as handwashing, adherence to medication, or safe sex. Still others involve stigma, fear, or intimate power relations. A kilogram of lentils may bring a mother to an immunization camp; it will not necessarily enable a woman to negotiate condom use with an older partner. Glennerster and Kremer note that information about HIV risk reduced pregnancies by older men, an important result. But it worked because the information was specific, credible, and actionable. General exhortation rarely suffices.

From a health systems perspective, the most promising part of behavioral economics is its attention to the “last mile.” Public programs often fail not because the technology is unknown, but because the final step is badly designed. A clinic is open during working hours when women cannot come. A water treatment product is sold in a shop too far away. A test result requires a return visit. A mother must remember a vaccine schedule without reminders. These are not minor administrative details; they are the lived interface between state and citizen. Behavioral design can make systems more humane.

But the field should resist a consumer model of health. The poor are not merely consumers deciding whether to purchase chlorine, nets, or deworming tablets. They are members of communities whose health depends on collective action. Infectious disease control is inherently social. Sanitation is social. Herd immunity is social. A policy vocabulary centered on “demand” can obscure obligations of public provision. We do not ask middle-class residents of Boston to reveal their willingness to pay for each component of clean water before providing it. We build systems and finance them collectively.

The essay’s evidence should therefore strengthen, not weaken, the case for universalism in essential preventive health. If small charges deter use and if benefits spill over to others, essential preventive services should be provided free. But universal access must be matched by universal responsibility: stable budgets, competent administration, and political accountability. Donors can fund pilots; states must build services.

A useful analogy is vaccination in wealthy countries. Its success depends on science, yes, but also on school-entry rules, pediatric visits, public purchasing, record systems, reminders, and social norms. No single nudge explains high coverage. Rather, vaccination is woven into institutions. The challenge for poorer countries is not to find one clever incentive but to build routines that make healthy behavior easy, expected, and supported.

Glennerster and Kremer have done a service by showing that fees and hassles kill. In public health, the friction of a few cents or a few minutes can become the pathway of disease. But the remedy is not only to remove friction at the household level. It is to construct health systems in which the healthy choice is the default because safe water flows, vaccines arrive, workers are present, and care is financed before illness strikes. Small changes can produce big results when they are the visible edge of a larger system. Without that system, even the best-designed nudge may become another short-lived project in the long history of development disappointment.

"Effective development requires a synthesis of large-scale institutional commitments and behavioral insights that address the specific psychological and logistical constraints of real people."

The debate prompted by Glennerster and Kremer should not be framed as a contest between “small” and “big” development. The most successful development efforts usually combine both: large public commitments implemented through attention to small behavioral and administrative details. A national education policy fails if school fees are due before harvest cash arrives. A health ministry’s vaccination goal fails if mothers must travel far for uncertain service. A water strategy fails if households are expected to remember chlorine after returning home. Big promises reach poor people through small interfaces.

This is the best reading of “Small Changes, Big Results.” The essay does not prove that development can be reduced to nudges. It shows that many programs have failed because they were designed for imaginary people—people with perfect information, stable cash flow, unlimited attention, and no procrastination. Real people, rich and poor, are not like that. They delay, forget, follow peers, respond to hassle, and overweight immediate costs. In poor households, these ordinary tendencies meet extraordinary constraints. The result is underinvestment in schooling and prevention even when long-run benefits are large.

The evidence on user fees is especially important for policy. For years, cost recovery was defended as practical realism: governments were poor, services cost money, and users would value what they paid for. But if even tiny prices sharply reduce adoption of high-return preventive goods, the realism was false. A policy is not sustainable merely because it collects coins; it must also achieve its purpose. A clinic that balances a small budget by deterring immunization is not efficient. A water program that sells chlorine few people use is not sustainable in any meaningful public health sense.

At the same time, the critics of user fees must answer the supply-side question. If services are free at the point of use, they must be financed elsewhere. That means taxes, cross-subsidies, insurance, or aid. It also means administration capable of preventing informal fees and stockouts. The movement against user fees in health and education will succeed only if it is joined to institution building. Otherwise official abolition may become a sign on a clinic wall while patients continue to pay in cash, gifts, or waiting time.

Randomized evaluations are well suited to identifying these hidden barriers. One of their virtues is that they can reveal that a theory is wrong even when the theory feels intuitively right. Many development workers believed free nets would be wasted; trials suggest otherwise. Many economists believed those who most needed a product would pay; evidence suggests otherwise. Many educators assumed that information campaigns were too weak to matter; in some settings, information about returns to schooling mattered a great deal. These are useful corrections.

But experiments become most useful when they are part of a learning system rather than a hierarchy of evidence that dismisses other knowledge. A randomized trial can show that lentil incentives increased immunization in Rajasthan. Local knowledge helps explain why lentils were attractive, how mothers perceived the camps, what role village leaders played, and whether the approach would be acceptable elsewhere. Administrative data can show whether the program remains reliable at scale. Political analysis can tell us whether officials have incentives to maintain it. Cost-effectiveness analysis can compare it with alternatives. No single method carries the whole burden.

A practical synthesis would start with problem diagnosis. Why are children not in school? Is it cost, distance, teacher absence, poor learning, child labor, safety, language, disability, or low perceived returns? In one place, uniforms may be decisive; in another, school quality may dominate. Why are households not chlorinating water? Is it price, taste, inconvenience, distrust, or lack of reminders? Why are mothers not completing immunization schedules? Is it procrastination, unreliable clinics, opportunity cost, or fear of side effects? Behavioral economics supplies hypotheses; field evidence tests them; institutions must act on them.

The essay’s emphasis on similarity across contexts is welcome but should be handled carefully. People everywhere procrastinate and respond to convenience. But what is convenient, credible, or motivating depends on setting. Lentils work as an incentive where they are valued and logistically available. A public chlorine dispenser works where water is collected from shared sources. Peer pressure works differently in dense villages, urban slums, and dispersed pastoral communities. The universal lesson is not the object—lentils, uniforms, dispensers—but the design principle: reduce immediate costs, align timing with cash flow, make action easy, and use social cues wisely.

Scale is the next frontier. Pilots often benefit from exceptional implementers. Governments operate under routine constraints. If chlorine dispensers require refilling, who is responsible? If uniforms are subsidized, how are suppliers chosen? If transfers are timed to school fees, can payment systems deliver reliably? If information about returns to education is provided, who updates it when labor markets change? A clever intervention can become a mediocre program if the mundane machinery is neglected. The development community should be as interested in procurement, payroll, maintenance, and supervision as in behavioral insight.

There is also a need to connect demand-side programs to the quality of services. Increasing school attendance is valuable, but only if children learn. Raising clinic visits is valuable, but only if care is competent. Encouraging HIV testing is valuable, but only if counseling and treatment are available. Behavioral interventions can increase use of bad services as well as good ones. Therefore the same experimental discipline should be applied to teacher incentives, curriculum reform, health worker performance, drug supply, and governance. The poor should not be nudged toward empty promises.

The political economy of evidence matters too. Policymakers like low-cost interventions, especially in times of fiscal constraint. Donors like programs that can be measured and attributed. Researchers like clean identification. These preferences can bias attention toward marginal interventions even when larger reforms are necessary. The safeguard is not to abandon small, tested programs but to place them within explicit national goals: universal primary education with learning, universal access to essential health services, safe water and sanitation, and gender equality. Small changes should be judged by whether they advance those goals.

If there is a moral lesson in the essay, it is that dignity requires design. A policy that asks a poor mother to make repeated trips to an unreliable clinic is not respectful because it preserves her choice; it is careless. A program that charges for deworming on the theory that payment reveals value is not empowering if it leaves children infected. A school system that lets a uniform fee exclude a child has confused administrative convenience with education policy. Respecting the poor means taking their constraints seriously enough to remove unnecessary obstacles.

As of 2011, the world is still far from the Millennium Development Goals in many countries, but the progress in schooling, child survival, and disease control is real. The next gains will require both money and intelligence. Glennerster and Kremer show that intelligence often lies in details: timing, price, convenience, information, and social norms. Their critics rightly remind us that details live inside systems of power and finance. The sensible path is to refuse the choice. Build the systems, test the details, revise the programs, and keep asking not only whether an intervention works, but whether it helps create public institutions worthy of the people they serve.

"While behavioral economics and randomized trials provide essential insights into the practical frictions that hinder development, these small-scale interventions must be seen as supplements to, rather than substitutes for, addressing systemic political and institutional challenges."

Glennerster and Kremer are right to insist that development policy should take seriously the mundane frictions that keep people from using valuable services. A school uniform, a chlorine bottle, a walk to a clinic, or the timing of a fee can look trivial from an office in Washington or Nairobi; for a household managing irregular income, illness, debt, and daily labor, such details can be decisive. The great virtue of the experimental work they describe is that it has made these frictions visible. It has also punctured several durable myths: that charging small fees reliably screens for “serious” users, that free goods are necessarily wasted, and that information campaigns alone can overcome practical barriers.

But I worry that the lesson “small changes, big results” can be too easily misread. It is true that small design changes sometimes produce large improvements. It does not follow that poverty is mainly a problem of bad choice architecture, or that the most important obstacles to health and education are behavioral quirks shared by rich and poor alike. The poor may procrastinate, as all humans do, but they also face constraints that are sharper, more dangerous, and more political than the analogy to American retirement savings suggests.

Consider user fees. The evidence that even modest charges reduce use of preventive services is persuasive and important. But the reason to abolish fees is not only that households are present-biased. It is also that primary education, immunization, clean water, and malaria prevention are public goods, or at least goods with large social spillovers. They are part of the basic social minimum that governments should provide. A family that does not buy chlorine may be misjudging the future, but it may also be making a reasonable decision under scarcity: today’s coins buy food, transport, kerosene, or repayment to a lender; tomorrow’s diarrhea is uncertain. Calling this procrastination risks making an intelligible response to insecurity look like a cognitive defect.

The same point applies to schooling. Conditional cash transfers, scholarships, uniforms, and information about returns can raise enrollment, and that is welcome. Yet enrollment is not education. Many developing countries have achieved impressive gains in attendance while children learn little. In India, surveys by Pratham have repeatedly shown that many children in school cannot read simple texts or do basic arithmetic at grade level. In parts of Africa, teacher absenteeism and weak accountability remain severe. A free uniform can get a child into the classroom, but it cannot by itself ensure that a teacher is present, that the curriculum is appropriate, or that the child learns enough to alter her life chances. On the supply side, institutions are not small details.

This is where randomized trials are both powerful and limited. They can tell us whether a particular incentive raised immunization in Rajasthan, whether deworming reduced absenteeism in western Kenya, or whether a chlorine dispenser increased water treatment at communal sources. Such findings are far better than wishful thinking. But many of the most important development questions concern systems: how to build a competent civil service, how to finance universal health care, how to regulate private providers, how to create jobs for educated youth, how to make politicians answerable for service delivery. These questions can sometimes be studied experimentally at the margin, but they cannot be reduced to a sequence of marginal tweaks.

Nor is scaling up merely a technical exercise. An NGO can distribute incentives or monitor a program closely in a pilot. A ministry must operate through budgets, procurement rules, unions, local brokers, and electoral pressures. A program that is cheap in an experiment may become expensive when extended nationally; a behavior induced by novelty may fade; a subsidy may be captured; informal fees may replace formal ones. Glennerster and Kremer acknowledge these issues, especially on the supply side, but their essay’s emphasis falls on the elegance of design rather than the messiness of power.

Still, the behavioral evidence should change policy. Governments and donors should be much more skeptical of fees for basic preventive health and primary education. They should pay attention to timing: school costs due in a lump sum at the beginning of term will exclude children even when annual costs appear low. They should design services around actual routines: chlorinate water at the source, bring immunization closer to villages, make forms simpler, reduce waiting times. These are not second-order concerns. For people with little slack, convenience is substance.

The best conclusion, then, is not that small interventions are enough, but that justice often depends on small implementation choices. A right to education means less if a uniform fee blocks entry. A right to health means less if the clinic is open only when mothers cannot come, or if a nurse demands an unofficial payment. Behavioral economics helps us see these barriers without romanticizing the poor as perfect calculators or condemning them as irrational.

We should welcome randomized evaluations as a discipline of humility. They force experts to test assumptions and to notice when the poor reject programs designed for them. But humility cuts both ways. The success of a lentil incentive or a chlorine dispenser should not tempt us into a new technocratic confidence that development can be achieved by clever nudges alone. Small changes can produce big results when they are attached to larger commitments: public financing, reliable delivery, accountable institutions, and respect for the people whose behavior is being studied.

"While behavioral economics and randomized evaluations provide essential, realistic insights into how small changes can significantly improve development outcomes, these 'nudges' must be viewed as complements to, rather than substitutes for, addressing broader institutional and systemic failures."

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer make a claim that should be both modest and unsettling: modest because it says that small frictions matter; unsettling because many development policies have been built as if they did not. Their examples are persuasive. A six-dollar uniform, a few minutes’ walk, a thirty-cent bottle of chlorine, a date on which school fees come due—none looks decisive in a textbook model. Yet in the lives of poor households, and in the psychology of ordinary human beings, such details can determine whether a child sits in a classroom or stays home, whether water is treated or not, whether an immunization course is completed or abandoned halfway through.

The essay’s central achievement is to restore realism to the economics of poverty without abandoning rigor. Too often development debates swing between two caricatures: the poor as heroic rational calculators who should be left to respond to prices, and the poor as passive victims of culture or tradition. The evidence Glennerster and Kremer summarize points to a more recognizable human portrait. Poor people respond to incentives and information. They also procrastinate, underestimate future benefits, face cash-flow problems, and are deterred by inconvenience. In this respect they resemble the rest of us, except that their margin for error is far smaller.

That last point is crucial. When an American delays enrolling in a retirement plan, she may lose employer matching funds or compromise her old age. When a Kenyan family delays buying a bed net, the cost may be a child’s illness or death. Behavioral economics is sometimes presented as an amusing catalogue of human quirks. In poor settings, those quirks collide with malaria, diarrhea, worm infections, and school exclusion. A policy that reduces one small hassle can therefore have consequences wildly disproportionate to its apparent size.

The strongest part of the argument concerns user fees for preventive health. The old claim that people must pay in order to value a product has always had more moral appeal than empirical foundation. It flatters donors and administrators because it seems to promise both thrift and discipline: the poor will not waste what they have purchased. But the evidence reviewed here shows that small charges can sharply reduce take-up, while payment itself does not reliably improve use. The case is especially powerful where there are external benefits, as with infectious disease. If my neighbor’s child sleeps under a net or receives deworming treatment, my household benefits too. In such cases, charging user fees is not merely harsh; it is economically confused.

The same logic applies, though somewhat differently, to schooling. Schooling produces private returns, but it also generates public goods: more literate citizens, lower fertility, better health practices, and stronger participation in civic life. If uniforms keep children out of school, then uniforms should be supplied or their cost offset. If families underestimate the returns to schooling, information campaigns may be among the cheapest development interventions available. If adolescents respond to peers, then a program that brings some children into school can alter the whole social environment.

Still, the essay’s title, “Small Changes, Big Results,” risks being misunderstood. Small changes can produce big results when the underlying technology is sound, the service is available, and the barrier is genuinely marginal. A chlorine dispenser works because chlorine kills pathogens. A lentil incentive works because vaccines work and because immunization teams are present. A uniform subsidy works because there is a school to attend. Behavioral design cannot compensate for a collapsed clinic, an absent teacher, a corrupt procurement system, or a civil war. The power of small changes depends on the existence, however imperfect, of larger systems.

Glennerster and Kremer acknowledge supply-side questions, but these deserve even more emphasis. Abolishing user fees is often right, yet clinics that lose fee revenue need replacement funding. Teachers induced to attend school by incentives may still teach poorly. Vaccination campaigns require cold chains, record keeping, transport, and trust. In many countries, the central problem is not only that people fail to demand services; it is also that states fail to deliver them. The danger is that policymakers will seize on the inexpensive behavioral tweak while neglecting the harder institutional work that makes the tweak possible.

There is also a political reason to be careful. Development agencies love interventions that are discrete, measurable, and cheap. Randomized evaluations understandably favor programs whose effects can be isolated. But poverty is not a series of isolated mistakes. It is a condition shaped by land rights, labor markets, gender hierarchies, public finance, infrastructure, and political power. A child may miss school because of a uniform fee; she may also miss school because the school is unsafe, because her labor is needed at home, because the teacher is absent, or because there is no job waiting even for graduates. An experimental result can tell us which lever moved attendance in one setting; it cannot by itself tell us what justice requires.

Yet this is not an argument against the experimental approach. On the contrary, one of the virtues of randomized evaluation is that it can puncture ideological certainty. It has already challenged the belief that free distribution inevitably produces waste. It has challenged the assumption that information never matters. It has challenged the claim that the poor who most need a product will reveal that need by paying for it. Evidence cannot settle every political dispute, but it can retire some bad arguments.

The right lesson, then, is not that development should shrink its ambitions to small nudges. It is that large ambitions often fail because they ignore small realities. A government may declare universal primary education, but if a uniform fee remains, enrollment may lag. A donor may fund a water campaign, but if chlorine must be purchased in town and remembered at home, adoption may remain low. A ministry may order free immunization, but if mothers must lose a day’s work and receive no compensation, completion rates may disappoint.

The essay invites a welcome humility. Poor people do not need lectures about rationality from economists whose own societies struggle with obesity, debt, and low savings. They need policies designed for human beings as they actually are. Sometimes that means cash. Sometimes it means information. Sometimes it means removing a fee, moving a dispenser, timing a payment, or offering lentils. These are not trivial details. They are the channels through which policy touches life.

If the new experimental development economics can keep that humility—if it treats small interventions as part of a broader struggle rather than a substitute for it—then it will be genuinely transformative. The best development policy will combine moral seriousness about poverty with empirical seriousness about what works. Glennerster and Kremer have shown that, at least in many cases, the distance between failure and success is shorter than we imagined. We should walk it.

"While randomized evaluations provide credible evidence for specific interventions, they are insufficient on their own to guide development policy because they often fail to account for institutional complexity, scalability, and long-term systemic effects."

The most important sentence in Glennerster and Kremer’s essay is not one of its more quotable lines about lentils, chlorine, or school uniforms. It is their acknowledgment that whether findings generalize across contexts is “an empirical question.” That is exactly right. But it is also where the case for randomized evaluations becomes both strongest and most vulnerable. Randomized trials can tell us with unusual credibility what happened in a particular program at a particular time. They are much less able, by themselves, to tell us what will happen when a different government, with different administrative capacity and political incentives, attempts to scale a similar program elsewhere.

This distinction is not pedantic. Development policy is full of failed replications. A program succeeds under the supervision of a motivated NGO, with researchers carefully monitoring implementation, only to disappoint when adopted by a ministry with weak logistics, patronage pressures, and overburdened staff. A subsidy that works when temporary may produce different behavior when it is expected indefinitely. A conditional transfer that increases attendance where schools have room may have different effects where classrooms are already bursting. To say these are empirical questions is correct; to answer them requires more than accumulating small trials.

The randomized evaluation movement has been salutary in one respect: it has raised the burden of proof. Before the recent wave of experiments, many development programs were justified by plausible stories and weak correlations. If children with textbooks learn more, provide textbooks. If healthier children attend school more, teach hygiene. If people say they value health, sell them health products. Experiments have shown that plausibility is not enough. Some interventions work; many do not; some work for reasons different from those imagined. That is progress.

Yet the rhetoric surrounding randomized evaluations sometimes implies a hierarchy of knowledge in which experimental estimates are “hard” and everything else is soft. This is misleading. A randomized trial gains internal validity by controlling the assignment of treatment, but it often purchases that clarity by narrowing the question. We learn whether a particular package changed a measured outcome over a given period. We may not learn why, for whom, under what institutional conditions, at what political cost, or with what long-term consequences. Those questions require theory, qualitative observation, administrative data, historical comparison, and sometimes plain institutional judgment.

Take the example of conditional cash transfers. The evidence that they increase enrollment is strong, and the randomized evaluation of Mexico’s PROGRESA was rightly influential. But the policy question is not simply, “Do transfers raise attendance?” It is also: Should transfers be conditional or unconditional? How large should they be? Should they be targeted or universal? Do they reinforce mothers’ unpaid caregiving burdens? Do they improve learning or merely attendance? Are the fiscal costs sustainable? What happens when politicians control beneficiary lists? The experimental answer to one component cannot settle the policy architecture.

Similarly, the evidence on user fees for preventive health is powerful, but the policy problem has multiple margins. Glennerster and Kremer are right that small prices sharply reduce demand and that the “paying makes people value it” argument is weak. However, prices also interact with supply chains, staff incentives, leakage, and public budgets. If abolishing fees leads to stockouts or informal charges, the intended behavioral effect may be blunted. A trial of demand at different prices cannot substitute for an analysis of public finance and governance.

One methodological concern is the difference between efficacy and effectiveness. Medical trials distinguish between whether a drug works under controlled conditions and whether it improves health in ordinary practice. Development experiments should be equally cautious. A chlorine dispenser placed at a communal water source, maintained by a diligent implementing organization, may be highly cost-effective. But the effectiveness of a national dispenser program depends on maintenance, refilling, local responsibility, vandalism, procurement, and the political salience of rural water. These are not nuisances to be added after the “real” result; they are central to the intervention.

Another concern is equilibrium effects. Small experiments often estimate partial-equilibrium impacts. Provide scholarships to high-performing girls in some schools, and effort rises among girls, boys, and teachers. That is encouraging. But what if many schools adopt similar scholarships? Does competition motivate, or does it discourage those unlikely to win? Do teachers shift attention toward exam performance at the expense of broader learning? Do local labor markets absorb the additional educated youth? In the short run, attendance is a meaningful outcome. In the long run, the returns to schooling depend on economic structures beyond the school.

A third issue is ethics. Random assignment is often ethically defensible, especially when resources are scarce and no one knows whether a program works. But the growth of experimentation should not dull our sensitivity. If there is already strong evidence that user fees deter life-saving prevention, how many more trials of charging poor households are justified? If a control group lacks access to an intervention likely to save children’s lives, what obligations do researchers have after the study? Ethical review boards can handle some questions, but development experiments occur in settings of vast inequality between researchers, implementers, and subjects. Consent and accountability deserve more than procedural treatment.

None of these criticisms means randomized evaluations should be abandoned. On the contrary, they are indispensable when the question is well suited to them. We should want to know whether small incentives raise immunization rates, whether information about HIV risk changes sexual behavior, whether free nets are used, and whether timing transfers around school fees matters. These are precisely the sorts of claims that governments and NGOs often get wrong. Experiments can save money, improve programs, and overturn damaging conventional wisdom.

The issue is not experiments versus theory; it is experiments within theory. Behavioral economics provides one useful theory: present bias, attention, hassle costs, social norms. But institutional economics, political economy, sociology, and anthropology provide others. A household’s failure to adopt a health technology may reflect procrastination; it may also reflect distrust of outsiders, gendered control over cash, prior experience with failed programs, or rational skepticism about product quality. Experiments can distinguish among mechanisms only if designed with these possibilities in mind.

The authors’ iterative vision—try, learn, adapt, test again—is the best defense of the experimental approach. It is more modest than claiming universal laws from scattered trials. But iteration must occur not only across products and prices; it must also move up levels of complexity. We need experiments and quasi-experiments on provider incentives, bureaucratic monitoring, decentralization, public finance, and political accountability. We need to know not only how to get households to use services, but how to get states to provide them reliably.

“Small Changes, Big Results” is therefore both convincing and incomplete. It is convincing about the importance of behavioral frictions and the folly of small user fees for high-return preventive goods. It is incomplete if read as a manifesto for a new experimental technocracy. The right conclusion is epistemological humility. Randomized evaluations are powerful flashlights, not floodlights. They illuminate sharply whatever they are pointed at. Development, however, is a landscape. We should use the flashlight often, but we should not mistake its beam for the whole terrain.

**From the Field: What the RCTs Don't See**

I have spent the better part of twenty years implementing health and education programs in East and West Africa, much of that time working closely with researchers running randomized evaluations. I read Glennerster and Kremer's essay with appreciation for its clarity and some concern about what it leaves out. Their findings are real. The complications I want to describe are also real, and they are visible mostly from inside the operations of programs rather than from the analyst's desk.

Take the chlorine dispenser, which Glennerster and Kremer present as a model of behaviorally-informed design. It is. I have seen them work, and the cost-effectiveness numbers are genuinely impressive. But I have also seen what happens between the evaluation phase and the scale-up phase, and the gap is larger than the published numbers suggest.

During the trial, dispensers are installed by careful field teams. They are maintained on a defined schedule. When one breaks, it is replaced quickly, because the integrity of the study depends on continuity. The chlorine supply chain is managed by people who understand that the experiment depends on it. The local promoters who encourage use are trained, paid, and supervised. Under these conditions, the dispenser is a beautiful intervention.

At scale, almost none of these conditions hold. Dispensers break and are not replaced for months. Chlorine runs out because the district health office did not place its order in time, or because the order was placed but the truck did not arrive. Promoters, if they exist, are unpaid volunteers whose enthusiasm wanes after the first year. The visual reminder still sits on the spring, but it is now an empty plastic box, and the social-norm effect runs in reverse: a broken dispenser becomes a symbol of donor projects that come and go. I have watched this happen in three different countries.

This is not a criticism of the chlorine-dispenser approach. It works when it works. It is a criticism of the inference from "this intervention had a large effect in the trial" to "this intervention should be the policy." The trial measures the efficacy of the intervention under near-optimal implementation conditions. Policy needs to know its effectiveness under the actually-available implementation conditions, which are usually much worse. The two numbers can differ by a factor of five or more. I have seen it.

The authors gesture at this issue when they note that "the details of institutional circumstance can matter a lot" for supply-side reforms. But they understate how pervasive the issue is. Even consumer-side interventions like the chlorine dispenser have crucial supply-side components that are invisible to the demand-side trial. If the system cannot keep chlorine in the dispenser, the demand findings are moot.

A second observation from the field: the same intervention often produces very different effects depending on the implementing organization. A free uniform distribution program run by a high-capacity NGO with deep community relationships produces different results than the same program run by a government ministry under political pressure to distribute uniforms by election day. Both are "the same intervention" in the language of the trial. They are different in practice. The RCT cannot easily distinguish them, and the published effect size depends, often invisibly, on which kind of implementer was randomized.

This matters for how findings should be communicated. When Glennerster and Kremer report that a Kenyan free uniform program raised attendance by 6.4 percentage points, the policy reader hears: free uniforms work. The field practitioner hears: free uniforms worked when distributed by this particular partner under these particular conditions to this particular population. The gap between these two readings is where many "evidence-based" programs go to die.

Third, I want to push back on the framing of behavioral barriers. The authors describe parents who delay immunization as procrastinators whom small incentives can mobilize. In my experience, parents who delay immunization in the villages I have worked in have specific, often well-founded reasons: the clinic is often closed when they arrive; the nurse may be rude; their last child had a bad reaction; the rumor mill has been activated by some local controversy. The lentil incentive may overcome these reasons by raising the expected payoff of attending. But it does not address the reasons themselves. A program that fixes the clinic — that ensures it is open, that the nurse is present and respectful, that side effects are explained honestly — may produce equally large effects without bribing parents to overlook the real problems. We do not know, because the experimental literature has not, on the whole, randomized clinic quality with the same intensity it has randomized incentive structures. The result is a literature that emphasizes demand-side fixes for what are often supply-side failures.

Fourth: scale. The published RCTs tend to operate on populations of thousands or tens of thousands. National programs operate on populations of millions. Almost everything changes between these scales: the quality of staffing, the political visibility of the program, the capacity of monitoring systems, the integrity of supply chains, the temptations of corruption. Glennerster and Kremer treat scale-up as a relatively unproblematic extension of the trial. In practice, scale-up is a different operation entirely, and many cost-effective interventions in trials become mediocre or worse at national scale.

None of this means the experimental program is wrong. It means it is incomplete. The findings are real but they are inputs to design, not blueprints. The field needs more humility about the gap between trial and policy, more attention to implementation systems, and more partnership with practitioners who can see what the data alone cannot. Glennerster and Kremer would, I think, agree with most of this. I wish their essay had said it more loudly. "Small changes, big results" is a wonderful slogan. "Small changes, big results, sometimes, under conditions we do not fully understand, when implemented by partners we cannot always replicate" would be more accurate. It would also be more honest about what we have learned.

**Missing the Politics**

There is much to admire in Glennerster and Kremer's careful synthesis of a decade's worth of experimental development research. The findings are real, the methodology is rigorous, and the policy implications — particularly on user fees — are humane. And yet, reading the essay, I could not shake the feeling that something enormous was missing from the picture. That something is politics.

Notice what the essay does not contain. It does not mention the state, except as a vague provider of services or remover of fees. It does not discuss why some governments deliver public goods competently and others do not. It does not ask why, after sixty years of independence, parts of sub-Saharan Africa still lack functional primary health systems while others have built them. It does not consider that the chlorine dispenser at the Kenyan spring exists in a country whose chronic underinvestment in rural water infrastructure is itself a political fact, the product of decisions about who counts and who does not. The dispenser is a clever workaround for a political failure, and the authors describe it as if politics had nothing to do with it.

This silence is not accidental. It is a feature of the experimental method. RCTs work best when the intervention is bounded, the implementer is reliable, and the political context can be held constant by design. NGOs make ideal partners precisely because they are flexible, project-shaped, and uninterested in confronting the larger structures around them. The randomista program has, in effect, carved out the depoliticized portion of development and studied it intensively. What it has learned in that bounded space is valuable. But the bounded space is not where the action is.

Consider the conditional cash transfer literature, which the authors rightly celebrate. PROGRESA in Mexico worked. So have its imitators in Brazil, Colombia, and elsewhere. But none of these programs is primarily an "intervention." They are pieces of a broader political project — typically associated with center-left governments responding to mobilized constituencies, building electoral coalitions among the poor, and constructing the administrative capacity to deliver benefits at scale. The cash transfer is the visible tip; the iceberg is a state capable of identifying poor families, transferring money to them reliably, monitoring conditions, and absorbing the political backlash from those who object. When other countries try to copy the cash transfer without the underlying political infrastructure, they often fail. The "treatment effect" is not portable because what is being transferred is not just a policy but a state capacity.

Or consider user fees. The authors present the evidence against fees as a triumph of behavioral economics over a flawed economic model. But the user-fee regime was never the product of an honest economic argument. It was imposed on dozens of African and Latin American countries during the 1980s and 1990s by the World Bank and the IMF as part of structural adjustment, against the wishes of national health ministries and despite warnings from many economists at the time. The reason fees persisted for decades was not that economists believed they worked. It was that they were embedded in a global political structure that constrained what indebted governments could do. Showing in a trial that fees reduce uptake is useful, but it is responding to a political imposition with a technocratic finding. The political imposition is the deeper story.

The same pattern shows up in education. Glennerster and Kremer report that providing information about returns to schooling, or recruiters visiting villages, raises enrollment. Excellent. But why do families underestimate returns in the first place? Often because the labor markets they observe really do have low returns to education — because the formal sector is small, because credentialing is rationed by ethnicity or politics, because the schools available to the rural poor are so bad that the returns to attending them are genuinely low. These are political facts about how labor markets and school systems are organized. An information campaign can shift beliefs, but if it shifts beliefs that were tracking reality, the effect will fade.

I do not mean to suggest that any of this invalidates the experimental findings. It does not. But the framing in which "small changes" produce "big results" can become misleading when readers conclude that the small changes alone are the explanation. The chlorine dispenser produces big results because it is solving a real problem of water contamination — a problem created by the political failure to provide safe piped water. The conditional cash transfer produces big results because it is delivering resources to households that have been politically excluded from the gains of growth. The deworming pill produces big results because the parasites have been allowed to flourish in the absence of adequate public health systems. Each "small change" is, in a sense, an admission of a large failure.

A more candid framing would acknowledge this. The experimental literature has identified a class of cheap, effective workarounds for the failures of weak states and ill-functioning markets. These workarounds are worth pursuing. They save lives. But pursued in isolation, they risk substituting for the harder political project of building states that can provide clean water, decent schools, and functional clinics without requiring an NGO with a randomized trial to figure out the optimal incentive structure.

Albert Hirschman warned long ago about the seduction of small, technical projects in development, and the way they could displace attention from the larger institutional questions. Glennerster and Kremer's program is not guilty of Hirschman's worst fears — the rigor is real and the humility is genuine. But the displacement effect is worth worrying about. We are producing a generation of development economists exquisitely trained to evaluate interventions and barely trained to think about politics, the state, or power. The "small changes" agenda needs to be paired with a serious account of where the big changes come from. Otherwise we will keep learning, with ever greater precision, how to cope with structures we have given up trying to change.

**The Limits of the Petri Dish**

Glennerster and Kremer's essay is admirably clear, but its clarity is purchased at the price of evading the central methodological question their movement has raised: do the findings of randomized controlled trials generalize, and if not, what exactly have we learned? The authors gesture at this issue and then declare it "empirical" — to be settled by accumulating still more trials. I want to suggest that the question is not merely empirical, and that the accumulation strategy is less reassuring than it sounds.

Begin with what an RCT actually estimates. In its purest form, it produces an unbiased estimate of the average treatment effect of a specific intervention, delivered by a specific implementer, in a specific population, at a specific time, under conditions partly shaped by the experiment itself. That is a precise and valuable thing. But it is not, on its own, a parameter of human nature. When the Mexican PROGRESA program raised secondary school enrollment among girls by 14.8 percentage points, that number is a fact about Mexico in the late 1990s, with its particular labor market, its existing school infrastructure, its specific cash transfer amount relative to local wages, and a competent federal bureaucracy capable of delivering the transfers reliably. Take any of these away and the number changes — perhaps slightly, perhaps drastically. There is no way to know in advance which.

Glennerster and Kremer counter this concern by pointing to patterns: across multiple trials in multiple countries, small fees reduce uptake, small incentives increase it, convenience matters, present bias is pervasive. These are real patterns, and they are valuable. But notice what has happened in the argument. The defense of the experimental program is no longer that any individual RCT tells us what will happen elsewhere — that defense was abandoned long ago — but that the accumulated body of RCTs reveals stable behavioral regularities. This is, in fact, the kind of inductive generalization that the randomista movement was supposed to improve upon. When we say "small fees reduce uptake of preventative health products," we are no longer in the world of clean causal identification. We are in the world of meta-analysis, narrative review, and judgment about which contexts resemble which — exactly the kinds of inferential leaps the RCT was meant to discipline.

This does not make the leaps wrong. It makes them ordinary social science, with all of its attendant uncertainty. The randomistas have produced excellent natural-history specimens; the question of which specimens describe a general phenomenon remains as hard as it ever was.

Consider also the selection of questions. Lant Pritchett has been pointing out for some years that the topics amenable to randomization tend to be narrow, project-shaped interventions: deworming pills, bed nets, conditional cash transfers, information campaigns. The questions that have historically driven large changes in human welfare — industrialization, urbanization, the building of effective states, the demographic transition — are not amenable to randomization at all. The countries that have lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty in the past thirty years (China most spectacularly) did so through policy choices that no RCT could have validated in advance. If our knowledge becomes increasingly weighted toward what we can randomize, we risk producing a development economics that knows a great deal about how to deliver chlorine and very little about how a society develops.

The user-fees finding is a useful illustration. Glennerster and Kremer report convincingly that even small fees collapse demand for preventative health products. Granted. But the policy question is not "does demand drop?" It is "what is the best use of scarce public budget?" The trial tells us about the demand response; it does not tell us about the opportunity cost of free provision, the supply-side incentives created when clinics lose their flexible cash, the political economy of who captures free goods in distribution chains, or the long-run consequences for the relationship between citizens and providers. The authors acknowledge some of these complications, but only in passing, and the headline message — "abolish small fees" — has already escaped into policy circles with the imprimatur of "evidence-based."

I am also uneasy with the framing of behavioral findings. Glennerster and Kremer move smoothly from "the poor are sensitive to small upfront costs" to the implication that policy should provide small upfront incentives. There is a paternalist undertone here that deserves attention. We have decided, on the basis of our own theory of what is good for the poor (more immunizations, more schooling), that we will design choice architectures to nudge them toward our preferred outcomes. This may be defensible — most policy is paternalist in some sense — but it is a different argument from the one the authors are making. They present their findings as if the optimal behavior were known and only the behavioral hurdles remained. Yet the parents who delay immunization may know things about provider quality, side effects, and local circumstances that the visiting researcher does not. Treating their behavior as a procrastination problem rather than a rational response to bad providers begs the question.

None of this is to suggest that the experimental program should be abandoned. The careful causal evidence Glennerster and Kremer summarize is genuinely better than what came before, and the discipline it has imposed on the field is welcome. But the proper attitude toward this body of work is more cautious than its champions suggest. We have learned a great deal about how particular interventions worked in particular places. We have learned somewhat less about why. We have learned still less about whether the same interventions will work elsewhere or at scale. And we have learned almost nothing, by this method, about the questions that drive the long arc of development.

The petri dish is real, and what it grows in is real. But a petri dish is not an ecosystem.

Once you try to scale, the scaffolding becomes the binding constraint.

It is true that hundreds of RCTs are now finding family resemblances — price-sensitivity for preventive health, present bias, peer effects. But "family resemblance" is doing a lot of work. The Malawi finding that the smallest CCT achieved the average effect, the Kenyan finding that uniforms reduced teen pregnancy, the Indian call-center result on girls' schooling — each of these depends on local elasticities that we have no theoretical reason to expect to be stable across settings. The inferential step from there to "people procrastinate, so put deadlines on chlorine adoption everywhere" is not itself randomized. It is an act of theoretical interpretation, and behavioral economics is still a thin enough theoretical structure that competing interpretations are easy to construct. Is low chlorine uptake about present bias, or about (rational) skepticism that the water is actually unsafe, or about distrust of outside products, or about taste? Different stories imply different policies.

Glennerster and Kremer have produced an elegant summary of what randomized evaluations have taught us about individual decision-making among the global poor. Consider what we know about the past sixty years of development. The policies that supported this transformation were not designed by behavioral economists. They involved exchange rate management, industrial policy, infrastructure investment, land reform, education systems geared toward an industrializing economy, and the painful construction of effective state institutions. None of these were tested in randomized trials. Many of them violated the contemporary best practice as understood by Western economists at the time.

They have remained dependent on commodity exports, have not built domestic manufacturing capacity, have suffered from chronic state weakness, and have seen their educated youth emigrate. Behavioral interventions can do little against these structural constraints. A chlorine dispenser does not industrialize a country.

Over time, the discipline becomes shaped by what its methods can study, and the questions its methods cannot study become marginalized.

For the design of small projects, the behavioral image works well. For the design of national strategy, the rational-actor image, with its emphasis on aggregate incentives and structural constraints, is often more illuminating. The current intellectual climate, in which behavioral findings are everywhere and macroeconomic analysis is recessed, suggests we have lost the balance.

From a macroeconomic perspective, the question is different: what are the implications of free public health and education provision for the size of the state, for tax capacity, for the relationship between citizens and the fiscal contract? The relevant question for development is not whether Kenyans and Americans share psychological dispositions. They do. The relevant question is why one country has the institutions to make those dispositions productive and the other does not. The answer is not behavioral. It is structural, political, and historical, and the experimental program has, by design, almost nothing to say about it.

Within a year, the dispensers are broken, empty, or stolen. The "small change" that produced a 90 percent adoption rate in the pilot yields zero results at scale.

This is not a hypothetical concern; the history of development is littered with successful pilots that died upon scaling. The authors mention the challenge of abolishing user fees, noting that "many countries have abolished them on paper only to see informal fees creep back." This passing acknowledgment points to exactly the kind of institutional reality that thwarts behavioral interventions. Why do informal fees creep back? Because front-line health and education workers are often trying to survive in a broken system. If you remove their ability to charge formal fees without drastically reforming government payroll systems to ensure they receive a living wage on time, they will inevitably find other ways to extract rent from consumers. You cannot nudge a consumer to use a free clinic if the nurse is demanding a bribe at the door.

Furthermore, the political economy of scaling up behavioral interventions is fraught with difficulty. Take the celebrated example of providing a kilogram of lentils to rural Rajasthani mothers who complete their children’s immunization schedules. In the context of an RCT, this is a brilliant, cost-effective way to overcome procrastination. But imagine a state government trying to implement this as a universal policy. The logistical nightmare of procuring, storing, and distributing millions of kilograms of lentils without massive leakage and corruption is staggering. Politically, the government would immediately face backlash from families whose children were already immunized without the incentive, or from citizens demanding lentils for other civic behaviors. What works as a temporary, targeted experiment often becomes an unwieldy entitlement when translated into public policy.

Glennerster and Kremer are right to push the development community toward evidence-based interventions. We absolutely must stop wasting money on programs that do not work. But generating evidence on consumer behavior is only half the battle. If we want these "big results" to be permanent and widespread, the next frontier of development economics must move beyond the individual consumer and focus relentlessly on institutional capacity and the mechanics of implementation.

We need rigorous evaluations not just of whether a bag of lentils motivates a mother, but of how to reform procurement systems so government clinics don't run out of vaccines. We need to study how to motivate middle-management bureaucrats, how to insulate supply chains from political interference, and how to maintain quality control when a program expands from ten villages to ten thousand. Until the RCT movement applies the same level of scientific rigor to the messy plumbing of public administration as it does to the psychology of the poor, the promise of "small changes" will remain confined to the artificial perfection of the pilot project.

  2. 02The transition from pilot to policy often fails because underpaid and unmotivated civil servants lack the incentives and oversight that make NGO field staff effective.
  3. 03Institutional realities, such as broken payroll systems and informal rent-seeking by workers, can undermine behavioral nudges like the abolition of user fees.

"While behavioral nudges and demand-side incentives are valuable, they must not distract from the fundamental necessity of addressing supply-side constraints and institutional capacity in development policy."

While Glennerster and Kremer provide valuable evidence about consumer behavior and demand-side interventions, their essay understates the importance of supply-side constraints in limiting the effectiveness of health and education programs. A focus on behavioral nudges and small incentives can distract attention from the fundamental need to build institutional capacity and ensure that services are actually available to the people who need them.

Consider the immunization example that the authors cite with enthusiasm. Raising immunization rates from 5 percent to 38 percent by providing lentils and plates to Rajasthani mothers is impressive, but it presupposes that the vaccines themselves are available and that the cold chain for storing them is functioning. In many of the world's poorest regions, the primary constraint on immunization coverage is not parental procrastination but broken supply chains, missing health workers, and clinics without basic equipment. If we redirect development resources toward incentive schemes for demand while neglecting the infrastructure needed to deliver services, we may end up with more people seeking care that the system cannot provide.

The authors acknowledge that "we don't know the impact of fees on the supply side" and that small payments can "motivate workers—who often pocket them—to show up." This is an understating of the problem. In many developing countries, health and education workers are poorly paid, inadequately supervised, and often absent from their posts. The authors note that "a number of studies are now focused on this problem"—but this research focus comes late, after years of designing demand-side interventions based on behavioral economics insights.

The chlorine dispenser example itself illustrates the supply-side challenge. The authors describe how dispensers reduce packaging costs and make chlorine available at communal water sources. But someone has to refill those dispensers, maintain them, and ensure a consistent supply of chlorine. The remarkable 93 percent adoption rate in communities with dispensers is a tribute to the program design, but it also depends on a reliable supply chain that the authors do not fully describe. Scaling this approach to thousands of villages would require building logistics systems that are far more complex than the behavioral interventions themselves.

A related supply-side issue is quality. Even when people access health or education services, the quality may be so low that the interventions have minimal impact. Millions of children in developing countries attend school but learn little; millions receive health care but are misdiagnosed or receive ineffective treatments. Addressing these quality failures requires investments in teacher training, curriculum reform, clinical supervision, and facility maintenance—interventions that are institutionally intensive and resist the kind of small-scale testing that randomized evaluations enable.

The behavioral economics perspective, by focusing on individual decision-making, may inadvertently blame the victim. When poor families fail to adopt beneficial health practices, we attribute this to procrastination or cognitive limitations. But sometimes the rational response to a low-quality, unreliable, and unresponsive health system is to invest one's limited resources elsewhere. Improving demand-side uptake without addressing supply-side quality is like putting a beautiful facade on a building with a crumbling foundation.

Development policy needs both: better understanding of how people make decisions, and sustained investment in the institutions that deliver services. The authors' work on the first is valuable; what is needed now is a parallel effort to understand how to build supply-side capacity in ways that work with the grain of human behavior rather than against it.

"While behavioral economics offers a powerful, evidence-based method for improving development outcomes, it must be integrated with other economic frameworks to address the structural and institutional causes of poverty that individual-focused interventions overlook."

The Glennerster and Kremer essay describes a powerful approach to development that deserves serious consideration alongside other frameworks for understanding poverty and designing interventions to address it. Comparing the behavioral economics paradigm to its main alternatives reveals both its distinctive strengths and its characteristic blind spots.

The main alternative frameworks in development include: neoclassical economics (which emphasizes market liberalization and getting prices right), institutional economics (which focuses on property rights, rule of law, and governance quality), human capital theory (which emphasizes education and health investments), and more structuralist approaches (which focus on political economy, class relations, and the historical roots of underdevelopment). Each of these has contributed insights; each has also failed in characteristic ways.

The behavioral economics approach has a distinctive advantage over pure neoclassical economics: it recognizes that markets do not always produce optimal outcomes even when prices are correct, because individuals themselves may not behave as the models assume. This is a genuine advance that explains why many market-oriented reforms have underperformed expectations.

The approach also has advantages over purely structuralist frameworks: by focusing on measurable interventions and rigorous evaluation, it avoids the vagueness and ideological contestation that often characterize political economy analysis. When the authors show that small incentives increase immunization rates, that evidence is hard to dispute and relatively easy to act on.

But the behavioral approach has characteristic limitations. By focusing on individual decision-making, it tends to treat poverty as a condition of individuals rather than as a product of social structures. The authors note that "the poor weigh economic factors and make decisions about whether and how to invest in education and health" and that "their decisions are not always optimal for them or for society." But this framing implicitly blames the poor for making suboptimal choices rather than asking why the choices available to them are so constrained.

The structuralist alternative would ask: what political and economic forces have produced a situation in which a school uniform represents a significant barrier to education? Why does a family lack the cash to pay $6 for a uniform that would increase their child's lifetime earnings substantially? Why is clean water not available through public systems that would eliminate the need for individual treatment? These questions point toward different interventions—land reform, progressive taxation, public investment in infrastructure—than the behavioral framework generates.

A mature development strategy would draw on multiple frameworks rather than privileging any single one. Behavioral economics can tell us how to improve the uptake of existing services; institutional economics can tell us how to build the governance capacity that makes services available; structural analysis can tell us why the underlying distribution of assets and power produces poverty in the first place.

The authors make a contribution by demonstrating what behavioral economics can offer. They would make a greater contribution by acknowledging what it cannot provide and by building bridges to other approaches that address the dimensions of poverty that their framework cannot reach.

  2. 02The use of rigorous, randomized evaluations provides clear, actionable evidence that avoids the ideological vagueness of structuralist political economy analysis.
  3. 03The behavioral framework tends to treat poverty as a result of suboptimal individual decision-making rather than as a product of constrained choices and social structures.
  4. 04By focusing on small-scale incentives, the approach may ignore broader solutions like public infrastructure investment, land reform, and progressive taxation.
  5. 05A comprehensive development strategy requires a multi-disciplinary approach where behavioral insights improve service uptake while institutional and structural analyses address governance and power imbalances.
Vanilla Gemini

"While behavioral economics and randomized controlled trials offer valuable tactical tools for improving specific welfare metrics, they cannot replace the large-scale structural, institutional, and macroeconomic reforms necessary for comprehensive national development."

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer offer a compelling and optimistic vision of poverty alleviation. By applying the insights of behavioral economics through Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), they demonstrate how human idiosyncrasies—procrastination, present bias, and sensitivity to minor inconveniences—can be harnessed for good. The empirical triumphs they cite, from removing user fees for school uniforms to offering bags of lentils to boost immunization rates, are undeniable. They have successfully proven that development policy need not always rely on massive, top-down capital injections; sometimes, small, well-designed adjustments can yield tremendous improvements in human welfare.

Yet, in our enthusiasm for these "small changes," we must be careful not to lose sight of the broader structural landscape. The risk of the behavioral revolution in development economics is that it threatens to reduce the monumental, systemic challenge of poverty to a series of technocratic tweaks. While human psychology may be relatively universal, the institutional and political environments in which people operate are not. Development is not merely about overcoming individual behavioral bottlenecks; it requires building the physical, economic, and institutional infrastructure that makes sustained prosperity possible.

Consider the authors’ reliance on the success of conditional cash transfers (CCTs), such as Mexico’s PROGRESA. The authors rightfully note that school enrollment rose when families received financial incentives. But PROGRESA succeeded not just because of the behavioral incentive it provided to families, but because Mexico had the necessary state capacity to identify beneficiaries, track daily attendance, and securely disburse funds on a massive scale. In many of the world's poorest nations, this administrative apparatus simply does not exist. A behavioral nudge is only as effective as the bureaucratic machinery required to deliver it.

Furthermore, Glennerster and Kremer focus heavily on the demand side of the equation—getting children into uniforms and mothers into clinics. But what happens when artificially stimulated demand collides with a broken supply side? It is a well-documented reality that public service delivery in developing countries is plagued by systemic failures. High rates of absenteeism among teachers and health workers mean that a child incentivized to walk to school may arrive to find an empty classroom. A mother who walks to a clinic for her bag of lentils may find that the vaccines are out of stock. If small changes increase demand without simultaneous, large-scale reforms to supply and accountability, the resulting frustration could undermine trust in public institutions entirely.

The macroeconomic context is equally vital. The authors note that rural Indian families began sending girls to school more frequently when recruiters for call centers arrived in their villages. But the arrival of those call centers was not the result of a micro-economic nudge; it was the product of massive, macro-economic liberalization, infrastructure investment, and global trade dynamics. If structural economic transformation does not create formal-sector jobs, the long-term returns to incentivized education will eventually crater.

This highlights a significant limitation of the RCT framework: the problem of external validity. As economists like Angus Deaton have recently argued, knowing that a specific intervention works in one village does not automatically tell us *why* it works or guarantee it will scale elsewhere. A chlorine dispenser next to a communal water source is a brilliant, cost-effective intervention, but it is ultimately a coping mechanism for the absence of a municipal water-treatment system. We must ensure that policymakers do not become so enamored with funding highly measurable, randomized micro-interventions that they neglect the messier, harder-to-measure necessities of development: building roads, securing property rights, expanding power grids, and fostering trade.

None of this is to say that the behavioral approach is without immense value. Glennerster and Kremer are entirely correct that the rational-agent model is insufficient for designing good policy. When governments or NGOs distribute health products, they absolutely should use RCTs to optimize delivery and make adoption as frictionless as possible.

However, we must view behavioral economics as a powerful diagnostic tool and a tactical asset, not a comprehensive theory of economic development. Small changes can indeed bring big results for specific health and education metrics, saving millions of lives in the process. But elevating entire nations out of poverty remains a macro-level challenge. We cannot nudge our way around the fundamental need for capable states and robust economic institutions.

  2. 02Focusing solely on stimulating demand through small incentives ignores systemic supply-side failures, such as teacher absenteeism and medical supply shortages, which can undermine the effectiveness of these programs.
  3. 03Long-term economic progress depends on macroeconomic factors like trade liberalization and infrastructure investment to create the jobs that make education and health investments worthwhile.
  4. 04The randomized controlled trial framework often lacks external validity and risks encouraging policymakers to favor easily measurable micro-interventions over essential but complex systemic improvements like power grids and property rights.
  5. 05Small-scale behavioral interventions often serve as mere coping mechanisms for the absence of robust public utilities and municipal systems.
Vanilla DeepSeek

"While behaviorally informed 'small changes' offer valuable and cost-effective tools for development, they should be viewed as complements to, rather than substitutes for, the deeper structural and institutional reforms necessary to transform societies."

The lead essay by Glennerster and Kremer offers a compelling narrative: that small, behaviorally informed tweaks to policy can yield large improvements in health and education outcomes for the world’s poor. Drawing on a growing body of randomized evaluations, they argue that modest incentives, free provision of preventive goods, and minor adjustments in timing and convenience can overcome procrastination and cognitive biases that prevent poor households from investing optimally in their own well-being. This is an important message, and the evidence they marshal is impressive. But as an agenda for development, the “small changes” approach risks elevating micro-level fixes over the deeper structural transformations that ultimately determine living standards.

The authors are right to celebrate the experimental turn in development economics. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have brought a welcome dose of empirical rigor to a field long dominated by grand theory and casual empiricism. The demonstration that a free school uniform can boost attendance by six percentage points, or that a kilogram of lentils can triple immunization rates, is both striking and actionable. These are precisely the kinds of concrete, cost-effective interventions that donors and governments can scale up with measurable impact. And by showing how seemingly trivial barriers—a bit of walking, a tiny fee—disproportionately deter beneficial behaviors, the research reveals a consistency in human psychology that cuts across cultures.

Yet there is a danger that this focus on the low-hanging fruit of individual behavior change distracts from the harder graft of institutional reform. The essay acknowledges that “the details of institutional circumstance can matter a lot,” but it largely sidesteps the question of why public health and education systems are so dysfunctional in the first place. In many countries, clinics lack drugs, teachers are chronically absent, and corruption diverts resources. A chlorine dispenser at a communal water point is a clever fix, but if the local water authority fails to maintain the borehole, the dispenser is of little use. Small incentives can get children into classrooms, but they do little to improve the quality of teaching once they arrive. The “small changes” paradigm risks seeking silver bullets that bypass, rather than repair, broken systems.

Moreover, the enthusiastic extrapolation of behavioral regularities across contexts may be premature. The essay’s claim that “strikingly similar patterns of behavior” hold “across different sectors, products, and continents” comes mainly from two countries, Kenya and India. Even within those settings, the studies are typically run in partnership with committed NGOs, under the watchful eye of researchers. Whether such effects survive national scale-up with weak government capacity is an open question. Scalability involves navigating political economy and bureaucratic incentives that small pilots can sidestep.

A related concern is that behavioral economics, for all its insights, can adopt a subtly paternalistic framing. The essay speaks of the poor as procrastinators who fail to make “the best decisions,” echoing the rational-agent model’s assumption that there is a single, knowable optimal choice. But what looks like procrastination may reflect a rational weighing of risks and constraints. A family that declines to buy a mosquito net at a subsidized price might have competing immediate needs (food, medical emergencies) that are invisible to researchers. Their apparent neglect of preventive health could stem from deep-seated mistrust of health providers, or from a realistic assessment that the net will not be properly maintained. The behavioral lens, if applied uncritically, risks treating poverty as a problem of faulty cognition rather than a problem of poverty itself.

These cautions are not an argument against the intelligent use of behavioral insights. The authors’ chlorine dispenser example is a model of how thoughtful design—reducing packaging costs, combining steps, providing visual cues—can align convenience with public health goals. And the broader message that we should take people as we find them, with all their cognitive quirks, is a vital corrective to the simplistic rational-actor model. But small changes should be seen as complements to, not substitutes for, larger efforts to strengthen public services, promote inclusive economic growth, and empower the poor to demand accountability. In the end, the biggest results—the kind that transform societies—are likely to come from structural change, not just from the clever calibration of carrots and lentils.

  3. 03The behavioral lens can adopt a paternalistic framing that treats poverty as a problem of faulty cognition rather than a rational response to severe resource constraints and systemic mistrust.
  4. 04Small-scale interventions like chlorine dispensers or school uniforms do not address the underlying quality of services, such as the standard of teaching or the maintenance of infrastructure.
