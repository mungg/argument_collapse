"While local police are essential partners in counterterrorism, the United States must prioritize a centralized, federally led intelligence system over a decentralized model that depends on the varying capacities and political preferences of local jurisdictions."

Daniel Richman’s essay is an elegant defense of federalism in counterterrorism, but it understates a hard fact: terrorism is a national and international threat, and the United States cannot afford a domestic intelligence system that depends too heavily on the varying preferences of thousands of local jurisdictions. Local knowledge matters. Community trust matters. But neither can substitute for national standards, centralized analysis, and clear federal command responsibility.

The failures before September 11 were not primarily failures of community policing. They were failures of national intelligence management. Information was dispersed across the CIA, FBI, INS, State Department, FAA, and other agencies. Watchlists were incomplete. Analysts lacked access to relevant data. Field offices did not always understand the significance of what they had. Headquarters did not always act. The “wall” between intelligence and law enforcement, whether overstated or not, contributed to hesitation. No local police department could have solved these problems. They required a federal response.

Richman acknowledges this but shifts attention to the indispensable role of state and local police in generating new information. He is correct that local officers are numerous and embedded in communities. Yet we must be candid about the unevenness of local capacity. Some departments, such as New York’s, have developed sophisticated counterterrorism programs. Many others are small agencies with limited training, poor technology, high turnover, and little experience with intelligence. There are roughly 18,000 law-enforcement agencies in the United States. A system that relies on all of them to mediate national-security threats will be as strong as its weakest links.

The problem is not only capacity. It is also parochialism. Local officials answer to local voters. That is usually a virtue in ordinary policing, but in counterterrorism the costs and benefits do not align locally. A police chief may avoid controversial intelligence work because it produces no visible local benefit and creates political headaches. A city council may restrict cooperation with federal authorities to satisfy local activists, while the risk prevented may lie in another state. Richman sees this divergence as a source of moderation. Sometimes it will be. But sometimes it will produce dangerous under-enforcement. The fact that a terrorist cell resides in one city does not mean it intends to attack that city. Local reluctance can impose national costs.

This is why the federal government must lead. It has responsibility for national defense, foreign intelligence, border control, and interstate threats. It alone can integrate information from overseas sources, financial investigations, immigration records, communications intercepts, and multi-jurisdictional criminal cases. It alone can see patterns that no locality can see. A traffic stop in Arizona, a mosque contact in Michigan, a wire transfer in New Jersey, and a visa record in Virginia may mean little separately. Their significance emerges only through centralized analysis.

The question, then, is not whether local police should participate. They must. The question is whether their participation should give them a veto over federal counterterrorism policy. Richman suggests that local departments can “exact a toll” from federal authorities in exchange for information. That may sound like healthy negotiation, but it is an odd way to run national security. The prevention of mass-casualty attacks should not depend on bargaining among agencies with different political incentives. We need cooperation, but we also need hierarchy.

A useful comparison is not simply the British “golden thread” between Special Branch and national agencies, but the broader lesson that domestic intelligence requires professional specialization. The FBI has historically combined criminal investigation and domestic intelligence, not always successfully. Some have proposed an American MI5: a domestic security service without arrest powers, focused on analysis and intelligence. The 9/11 Commission did not endorse that path, partly because of the FBI’s existing relationships with state and local law enforcement. But the argument for a more specialized domestic intelligence capacity remains strong. Criminal investigators are trained to build cases after crimes occur. Intelligence officers must identify threats before evidence is courtroom-ready. These are different crafts.

Civil libertarians worry that a centralized domestic intelligence agency would become a secret police. That worry is legitimate. American history contains too many examples of surveillance justified by national security and directed at dissent. But decentralization is not a sufficient safeguard. Local police Red Squads were not models of restraint. Nor is a fragmented system necessarily more accountable. Fragmentation can make it impossible to know who did what, who authorized it, and who should be blamed. A centralized agency with statutory limits, congressional oversight, inspector-general review, and clear public guidelines may be more controllable than a sprawling network of federal task forces and local intelligence units.

Richman’s criticism of using immigration enforcement broadly is persuasive but should be qualified. It would be foolish to turn every patrol officer into an immigration agent. It would be equally foolish to deny that immigration violations can be relevant to counterterrorism. Several of the September 11 hijackers violated immigration rules. Visa overstays, document fraud, and false statements may reveal networks and intentions. The answer is not a blanket local refusal to engage civil immigration issues, but a targeted system in which trained officers coordinate with federal authorities when immigration information bears on security threats or serious crime.

Similarly, community trust must be balanced against the risk of community intimidation by extremists. We often speak as if communities simply choose whether to cooperate with law enforcement. But some individuals may fear not only deportation or discrimination but retaliation from radicals or criminals. A strong federal presence can protect those who want to help. If local leaders discourage cooperation out of political opposition to federal policy, residents with valuable information may need channels that bypass local gatekeepers. Outreach should be broad, but intelligence cannot be hostage to the most vocal community representatives.

Funding is one area where Richman is plainly right. Homeland-security grants should be risk-based. The current distribution reflects Senate arithmetic more than threat assessment. High-risk targets—ports, transit systems, financial centers, chemical facilities, iconic landmarks—need resources. Cities should not be asked to bear enormous overtime and preparedness costs while money is spread thinly across low-risk jurisdictions. But risk-based funding should come with federal standards. If Washington pays for intelligence systems, communications equipment, and emergency preparedness, it should require interoperability, training, privacy rules, and performance measures.

A more centralized approach need not ignore local concerns. On the contrary, it should institutionalize them. Every major metropolitan area should have a formal intelligence and preparedness council including federal agencies, local police, fire officials, public health authorities, transportation officials, and relevant community liaison officers. But these councils should operate within a national framework, not invent policy city by city. There should be standardized classifications, reporting formats, privacy guidelines, and audit trails. Local agencies should know what to report and what not to report. Federal agencies should be required to provide feedback so that local tips do not vanish into a black hole.

The larger point is that counterterrorism is not ordinary policing with a new label. Ordinary policing is retrospective, local, and visible. Counterterrorism is preventive, networked, and often secret. Richman hopes the habits of community policing will tame domestic intelligence. That may help at the margins, but the more urgent challenge is competence. A system that is locally sensitive but analytically weak will fail. A system that collects thousands of low-quality tips without disciplined assessment will drown in noise. A system that allows local political controversies to determine participation will produce blind spots.

The United States must avoid both extremes: a federal security service indifferent to constitutional limits and local knowledge, and a loose confederation of police departments free to cooperate or not as they see fit. The proper model is federally led, professionally trained, legally bounded, and locally informed. Local police should be partners, not principals. They should supply information, receive alerts, and advise on community impact. But national authorities must set priorities, integrate intelligence, and be accountable for the result.

September 11 revealed the price of a system in which no one fully owned the prevention mission. We should not recreate that problem in the name of federalism. Democratic accountability is essential, but accountability begins with knowing who is in charge. For domestic counterterrorism, that must be the federal government.
