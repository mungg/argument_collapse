**On the Virtues of Plebiscitary Democracy**

Katznelson treats plebiscitary democracy as a third disfiguring alternative to legislative representation, alongside deliberative and populist temptations. California serves as his uncomfortable example: a state where "policy choices are offered directly for the people's approval" without much opportunity for deliberation. The implication is that referenda are blunt instruments that bypass the considered judgment that legislatures supposedly provide.

I want to push back. The case against plebiscitary instruments is much weaker than Katznelson suggests, and the case for them—as supplements to, not replacements for, representative institutions—is stronger than his framework allows.

Consider Switzerland, the country with the world's most extensive use of direct democratic instruments. Swiss citizens vote on roughly a dozen federal referenda per year, in addition to numerous cantonal and municipal ones. The Swiss confederation has used these instruments since the 19th century. It is among the world's most prosperous, peaceful, and well-governed states. The Swiss model has not produced authoritarian backsliding, not undermined parliamentary functioning, not led to demagoguery or to the substitution of mass passion for considered judgment. It has produced a political culture in which citizens are unusually engaged with public questions and unusually well-informed about policy details, because they know that their judgment may shortly be required.

Or consider Ireland's recent referenda on same-sex marriage and abortion. The 2015 marriage equality vote, which passed by 62 percent, was preceded by months of public discussion, civic engagement, family conversations, and personal testimony. It was, by all accounts, a deeply deliberative moment for Irish society—not despite being a plebiscite but because the plebiscitary form forced the question to be confronted by every citizen, not just by professional politicians. The result was both a clear policy outcome and a national experience of democratic self-determination.

Even California, Katznelson's cautionary case, looks more mixed than the standard liberal complaint allows. Yes, Proposition 13 has constrained fiscal policy for decades. Yes, the initiative process is sometimes captured by well-funded interests. But the same process has also produced significant progressive achievements: environmental protections, public health measures, criminal justice reforms. The complaint that voters lack the expertise to decide complex questions cuts in odd directions: it has been used to defeat conservative tax revolts and liberal climate measures alike, which suggests it functions more as elite skepticism of mass judgment than as a principled assessment of capacity.

The deeper point is that Katznelson's framework, in defending representative legislatures, takes for granted that legislative deliberation is more considered, better informed, and more responsive to the public interest than direct popular judgment. This is an empirical claim, and the evidence for it is much thinner than the rhetoric suggests. Legislators in many systems are insulated from direct accountability by gerrymandering and incumbency advantages. They are subject to intense lobbying by organized interests in ways the general public is not. They face perverse incentives toward short-termism (the next election) and toward symbolic position-taking rather than substantive lawmaking. None of this means they are useless, but it means the comparison with direct democratic instruments is not as one-sided as Katznelson implies.

There is also a constitutional argument worth making. Plebiscitary instruments can serve as a check on legislative capture by minoritarian interests. When legislatures are unresponsive to clear majoritarian preferences—as the U.S. Congress arguably is on issues like background checks for gun purchases, where polls show overwhelming support but legislation cannot pass—referenda offer a mechanism for the majority to override capture. This is not a slide toward Peronism. It is, in Madison's original framework, a way for "the people themselves" to act when their representatives have failed to represent them.

The serious question, then, is not whether plebiscitary instruments belong in democratic politics but what design choices make them work well. Threshold requirements that prevent narrow majorities from passing fundamental changes. Public funding for campaigns to ensure roughly equal advocacy resources. Provisions for legislative review and amendment. Adequate time for public deliberation before votes. Limits on the constitutional changes that can be made through initiative. These design choices distinguish well-functioning systems like Switzerland from more troubled ones.

Katznelson lumps deliberative, populist, and plebiscitary alternatives together as collectively threatening to representation. The more useful approach is to ask which combinations of representative, deliberative, and direct instruments produce the best democratic outcomes in particular contexts. Switzerland, Ireland, even California with appropriate reforms—these point toward hybrid arrangements that strengthen rather than weaken democratic legitimacy. The defense of representation against direct democracy is not, finally, a defense of democracy. It is a defense of one institutional form among several that democracies can use.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Successful models like Switzerland demonstrate that frequent direct democracy can foster a highly informed, engaged citizenry without leading to authoritarianism or legislative decay.
  2. 02Referenda can catalyze deep national deliberation and social consensus on fundamental issues, as evidenced by Ireland’s recent votes on marriage and abortion.
  3. 03The assumption that legislative deliberation is inherently more rational or public-spirited than popular judgment is an empirical claim undermined by issues like gerrymandering, lobbying, and incumbency bias.
  4. 04Direct democratic tools serve as a necessary constitutional check against legislative capture, allowing majorities to override minoritarian obstruction on popular policies.
  5. 05The effectiveness of direct democracy depends on specific institutional design choices—such as funding rules and threshold requirements—rather than the plebiscitary form itself.
  6. 06A healthy democracy is best served by hybrid arrangements that combine representative, deliberative, and direct instruments rather than relying solely on a centralized legislature.
