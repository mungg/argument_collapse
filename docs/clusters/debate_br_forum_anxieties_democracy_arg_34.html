"The essay argues that contemporary democratic anxiety stems less from internal institutional failures and more from the fundamental tension between national democratic self-determination and the rise of unaccountable transnational governance."

Katznelson's focus on the United States obscures an important global dimension of democratic anxiety: the tension between democratic self-determination and transnational governance. He mentions "transnational institutions and seemingly inexorable global processes" but quickly returns to domestic concerns. This misses the heart of contemporary democratic malaise.

Globalization has created a world where the most important decisions are made by institutions that lack democratic legitimacy. The European Union makes rules that affect 500 million people without meaningful European-wide democracy. The International Monetary Fund and World Bank impose conditions on borrowing countries. Trade agreements lock in rules that future legislatures cannot change. These developments have produced a democratic backlash—the very sovereigntism that Katznelson diagnoses as authoritarianism.

The tension isn't between democracy and authoritarianism but between different forms of democratic legitimacy. National democracy allows citizens to hold representatives accountable through elections. Supranational democracy, where it exists, relies on intergovernmental negotiation that insulates decisions from popular control. When citizens choose between these, they often prefer national democracy, even if it's less effective.

Katznelson treats Orbán's "illiberal democracy" as a threat to democracy. But Orbán's supporters might argue they're defending democratic self-determination against EU technocracy. The EU has imposed austerity on countries whose citizens didn't vote for those policies. It has restricted social provision through budget rules that no parliament adopted. It has extended market liberalization without democratic authorization. Perhaps Orbán's supporters are rejecting not democracy but external imposition.

This analysis doesn't excuse Orbán's attacks on press freedom and judicial independence. But it suggests that democratic anxiety has multiple sources, including legitimate concerns about lost sovereignty. Katznelson might have written a stronger essay engaging with these tensions rather than assuming national democracy is the only legitimate form.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Globalization has shifted decision-making power to supranational institutions like the EU and IMF that lack meaningful democratic legitimacy and insulate policy from popular control.
  2. 02The rise of 'illiberal' movements is often a reactionary defense of national sovereignty against external technocratic impositions rather than a simple rejection of democratic values.
  3. 03International trade agreements and budget rules increasingly constrain the ability of future domestic legislatures to enact the will of their voters.
  4. 04The conflict in modern politics is not a binary choice between democracy and authoritarianism, but a struggle between different and competing forms of democratic legitimacy.
