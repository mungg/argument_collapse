"Judith Jarvis Thomson’s cautious, minimalist defense of abortion rights is insufficient because it relies on the philosophical undecidability of fetal personhood rather than asserting a woman's absolute right to bodily autonomy."

In “Abortion: Whose Right?” Judith Jarvis Thomson trains her considerable philosophical talents on the weakest version of the anti-abortion position—the claim that even the fertilized egg has a right to life—and dispatches it with characteristic clarity. Yet as a feminist and a defender of abortion rights, I find her cautious minimalism deeply troubling. By focusing on early embryos and conceding that later fetuses might well have rights, she effectively cedes the moral high ground to the very forces that would control women’s lives. The right to abortion is not contingent on the philosophical undecidability of fetal personhood; it is grounded in the absolute right of every woman to bodily integrity and self-determination.

Thomson is, of course, the author of the most famous defense of this principle: the violinist thought experiment. It is a pity she did not employ it here. That analogy makes clear that even if we assume the fetus is a full person with a right to life, it does not follow that a woman must sustain its life with her body. No one—not a famous violinist, not a child, not a stranger—can claim such a use of another’s body without consent. The right to life is not a right to the use of another’s body, to claim what is needed to survive. This argument is far more powerful than Thomson’s current line, which depends on showing that the fetus “probably” has no right to life at all. The latter strategy invites endless and unwinnable metaphysical squabbles, and worse, it suggests that if it could somehow be proven that the fetus is a person from conception, then abortion might be murder. That is a dangerous concession. It plays directly into the hands of those who brandish ultrasound images and papal encyclicals, because we are forever on the defensive, arguing about heartbeats and neural development. The real moral fact is that women are persons, and the state may not compel them to gestate against their will, regardless of what we think about the entity inside them.

Thomson’s preoccupation with the encyclical’s claim about fertilized eggs is strategically misconceived. She devotes pages to demonstrating that reason does not compel belief in personhood at conception. But the anti-abortion movement in 1995 is not moved primarily by philosophical argumentation about fertilized eggs—it is moved by graphic images of late-term fetuses and by a deep-seated desire to reassert traditional gender roles. The encyclical itself is a political document aimed at reasserting male authority over women’s reproductive lives. When the Pope declares that abortion is murder and that those who support it are in moral crisis, he is not making a dispassionate philosophical claim; he is wielding religious power to enforce a particular vision of womanhood. Thomson’s gentle rebuke that the encyclical is “a thoroughly groundless assault on their moral integrity” is far too mild. It is a patriarchal edict, and we should say so.

Furthermore, Thomson’s legal argument relies on a slender liberal principle: that the state may not restrict a deeply valued liberty on grounds that the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting. This principle is appealing but oddly conservative. It suggests that if a majority of women someday came to accept fetal personhood, then restrictions might become legitimate. Rights should not depend on epistemological stalemates. The right to abortion is a fundamental right of bodily autonomy, akin to the right to refuse medical treatment or to defend oneself against physical invasion. It persists even if every single woman were to believe that the fetus is a person, because no moral community may use one person’s body as a life-support system for another.

To be sure, Thomson is on our side, and her essay offers invaluable ammunition against the most absolutist anti-abortion claims. But we must go further. We must assert that abortion is not merely permissible when the fetus’s moral status is questionable, but is a positive good—an exercise of women’s moral agency and a necessary condition of equality. The violence at clinics, the harassment of doctors, the slow erosion of access in rural areas—these are not primarily caused by philosophical confusion; they are caused by a political movement that has been allowed to set the terms of the debate. We need a feminist reclamation of the moral language, in which women’s lives and autonomy are the central values, not the hypothetical rights of zygotes. Thomson’s minimalism, however elegant, ends up reinforcing the idea that abortion is a regrettable exception to a general rule that life deserves protection. That is not a foundation for lasting freedom.
