**What the Encyclical Actually Says**

Thomson's essay deserves a response from someone who actually accepts the teaching of *Evangelium Vitae*, since most of what will be written about her piece will come from those who share her starting assumptions. I am a Catholic, and I believe what John Paul II writes: that the deliberate killing of an innocent human being from conception onward is gravely wrong. Let me try to say why Thomson's argument, for all its sophistication, does not move me—and why I think it should not move my fellow citizens either.

First, the matter of Dworkin. Thomson is right that Dworkin badly misreads opponents of abortion when he says we don't really mean what we say. We do mean it. The encyclical means it. When the Holy Father writes that procured abortion is "the deliberate killing of an innocent human being," he is not employing pious hyperbole that secretly amounts to "intrinsically regrettable." He is making a claim about what is happening on the operating table. I am grateful to Thomson for taking this seriously, even as she denies the claim. It is a more honest engagement than we usually receive.

Second, Thomson's argument about "rights presupposing interests" turns out, as she herself concedes, to be inconclusive. She knows of "no conclusive reason for denying that fertilized eggs have a right to life." Note what this concession costs her. She has built her entire political conclusion on the supposed reasonableness of denying fetal personhood, but her own analysis cannot establish that the denial is correct, only that it is not unreasonable. Yet she then claims that her opponents' position can be set aside in framing law. This is a remarkable maneuver: a view she cannot refute philosophically becomes one that may be disregarded politically.

Third, and most importantly, I want to challenge Thomson's central political principle: that "severe constraints on liberty may not be imposed in the name of considerations that the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting." Stated this way, the principle sounds modest. But consider what it implies. Slavery was once defended on grounds that abolitionists rejected; were abolitionists therefore forbidden from "imposing" emancipation? Child labor was permitted on grounds reformers rejected; were reformers forbidden from "imposing" labor laws? The protection of the weak against the powerful has always required law to act on contested moral premises. Indeed, the entire civil rights tradition that Thomson presumably honors required the federal government to impose, on Southern whites who genuinely disagreed, a moral vision they regarded as alien. The "constrained" can almost always claim that they are not unreasonable in rejecting the premise of the constraint.

What Thomson really needs is not the procedural principle she states, but a substantive one: that *when* a class of beings is contested as to its moral standing, the burden of proof falls on those who would protect it. But why? In every other domain we proceed precisely the opposite way. When in doubt about whether a creature can suffer, the humane person errs on the side of protection. When in doubt about whether a structure contains people, the demolition crew waits. When in doubt about whether a patient can hear us, we speak to her. The encyclical's principle—that "the mere probability that a human person is involved would suffice to justify" protection—is not some peculiarly Catholic dogma. It is the principle of caution that any decent moral agent applies whenever stakes are high and certainty is unavailable.

Fourth, Thomson dramatically understates the relevant biology. A fertilized egg is not "a piece of human tissue" like a thumb cell. A thumb cell will never become anything other than a thumb cell. A zygote, left to develop, becomes you and me. This is not a future-oriented fiction; it is a description of what the organism, *right now*, is doing. To be a developing human organism is to be a human organism at a particular stage of development. Thomson is too good a philosopher not to see this distinction, and her quick dismissal of the encyclical's continuity argument is the weakest passage in her essay.

Finally, on the violence. Of course the murders at Brookline were monstrous, and every serious opponent of abortion has condemned them. But Thomson's suggestion that strong pro-life rhetoric incites such violence is itself an argument that, applied consistently, would silence all serious moral discourse. If the truth is that abortion takes innocent human life on a scale of one and a half million per year in this country, then this is the most urgent moral fact of our generation, and saying so cannot be off-limits because some unbalanced person might respond criminally. The same logic would have silenced the abolitionists after John Brown.

Thomson's essay is generous in tone and admirably willing to engage opposing arguments on their merits. But its political conclusion does not follow from its philosophical premises. If we genuinely cannot rule out that the unborn child is one of us, then the burden of proof lies, as it always lies in such matters, with those who would proceed regardless.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Thomson correctly identifies that opponents of abortion literally believe the fetus is a human being with a right to life, rather than merely viewing its destruction as 'intrinsically bad' as Ronald Dworkin suggests.
  2. 02Thomson’s political conclusion is inconsistent because she admits she cannot philosophically refute the fetus's right to life yet argues that this view can be politically disregarded.
  3. 03The principle that laws cannot be based on moral premises that the 'constrained' reasonably reject is false, as evidenced by historical movements like abolition and civil rights which imposed contested moral visions to protect the weak.
  4. 04In cases of moral uncertainty regarding the status of a being, the burden of proof should fall on those who wish to deny protection rather than those who seek to provide it.
  5. 05Biological reality distinguishes a zygote from mere human tissue because the zygote is a distinct, developing human organism rather than a static cell.
  6. 06Strong moral rhetoric against abortion cannot be blamed for the actions of violent individuals, as the same logic would have unfairly silenced other urgent moral movements like abolitionism.
