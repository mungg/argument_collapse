"The Araki Yasusada hoax does not expose the hypocrisy of avant-garde 'Language poetry' as Marjorie Perloff suggests, but rather proves the necessity of its critique by demonstrating that mainstream literary institutions value the tragic biography of an author over the actual quality of the text."

In her reading of the Araki Yasusada hoax, Marjorie Perloff offers a compelling analysis of the American poetry world’s appetite for exoticized victimhood. However, she fundamentally mischaracterizes the relationship between the hoax and the avant-garde practices she groups under the banner of "Language poetry." By suggesting that Yasusada is somehow an extension of the "New Sentence" and that the hoax exposes the hypocrisy of Language writing, Perloff gets the institutional dynamics precisely backward. The Yasusada affair does not indict the avant-garde’s critique of the lyric subject; rather, it spectacularly proves its necessity.

Perloff points out that Yasusada's "translators" explicitly reference Ron Silliman's "New Sentence," claiming ancient Japanese renga as its precursor. She suggests that this flattering genealogy is part of what blinded progressive editors to the hoax. But let us look at *who* was actually fooled. The primary venues that embraced Yasusada—*American Poetry Review* (APR) and Jon Silkin’s *Stand*—are bastions of the mainstream, traditional, expressivist lyric. They are historically the very institutions that have most fiercely resisted Language poetry and its project of dismantling the unified, autobiographical "I."

Why did *APR* suddenly publish something that vaguely resembled disjunctive avant-garde writing? Because Kent Johnson provided them with the one thing they cannot live without: a tragic, heroic, romantic Author. The mainstream poetry establishment is addicted to the cult of personality. A poem is judged not by its linguistic construction, but by the suffering of the person who supposedly wrote it. Language poetry has spent twenty years arguing against this commodification of identity, insisting that meaning is produced in the text, not guaranteed by the biographical aura of the writer.

Johnson's genius was to take the formal techniques of the avant-garde (disjunction, parataxis, procedural composition) and graft them onto the ultimate authentic lyric subject: a Hiroshima survivor who lost his family. The editors at *APR* didn't publish Yasusada because they suddenly developed an appreciation for non-syllogistic movement. They published it because the avant-garde surface was safely anchored to a heartbreaking biography. The tears they shed were for the *hibakusha*, not for the torque of the sentences.

When the biological author was revealed to be a white American from Illinois, the editors demanded their money back and called it a "criminal act." Why? Because the poems hadn't changed. The text was identical. What vanished was the fetish object of the Author. The Yasusada hoax thus perfectly dramatizes the central claim of Foucault and the Language writers: the "author" is a regulatory fiction used by institutions to control and consume discourse. Perloff accuses the multiculturalist left of being Orientalists, which may be true, but she misses the deeper structural revelation. Yasusada shows us that the mainstream literary apparatus is incapable of reading poetry as poetry; it can only read biographies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary victims of the hoax were mainstream, expressivist journals like American Poetry Review and Stand, which have historically resisted the avant-garde project of dismantling the unified autobiographical subject.
  2. 02The mainstream poetry establishment is addicted to a cult of personality that judges poems based on the perceived suffering of the writer rather than their linguistic construction.
  3. 03Kent Johnson successfully deceived editors by using avant-garde formal techniques as a surface layer while anchoring them to the 'fetish object' of a tragic Hiroshima survivor.
  4. 04The outrage of the editors upon discovering the hoax confirms that they were consuming a regulatory fiction of authorship rather than reading the poetry itself, as the text remained unchanged while only the biography vanished.
  5. 05The hoax serves as a structural revelation that the mainstream literary apparatus is incapable of reading poetry as poetry, relying instead on the commodification of identity.
