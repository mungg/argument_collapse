"While pre-teens are not adults, the justice system should have the option to charge them as adults in rare, exceptionally grave cases provided there are strict judicial safeguards and youth-centered sentencing."

It should be possible, though exceedingly rare, to charge a pre-teen as an adult. The point is not to deny childhood or to satisfy a public appetite for vengeance. The point is that some crimes are so grave, so planned and so dangerous that the justice system must have a mechanism beyond the ordinary juvenile track.

A 12-year-old is not an adult. Neuroscience, common sense and constitutional law all say so. But “not an adult” should not automatically mean “incapable of serious culpability.” In the Milwaukee-area case, the allegations are not of a playground fight gone wrong. Prosecutors say two girls planned to kill a friend, lured her into the woods and stabbed her repeatedly. If those facts are proved, the law has to recognize more than childish misbehavior or even ordinary juvenile delinquency. Similarly, when a 9-year-old is dead, the community has a legitimate interest in a searching, formal process that can address both responsibility and danger.

The problem is that many transfer laws are crude. They allow prosecutors to decide, or they automatically move children into adult court based on the charge. That is backwards. If adult prosecution is ever allowed for a 12-year-old, it should follow a judicial hearing with a high burden of proof. The court should consider mental illness, competency, planning, prior history, amenability to treatment, risk to the public and the adequacy of juvenile jurisdiction. Adult imprisonment with grown offenders should be off the table for children this young. Mandatory sentences should be off the table as well. The Supreme Court’s decisions in Roper, Graham and Miller rightly insist that youth changes the punishment calculus.

Still, a categorical ban can produce its own injustice. Some juvenile systems lose jurisdiction at an age that may be far too soon for a child who committed an attempted murder or homicide and remains dangerous. A carefully supervised adult charge can preserve jurisdiction, protect the public and signal the seriousness of the act, while still permitting youth-centered sentencing.

The question is not whether a 12-year-old is “just like” an adult. He is not. The question is whether adult court should ever be available as a last resort. With strict safeguards, it should be.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Some crimes involve a level of planning and culpability that exceeds the scope of ordinary juvenile delinquency.
  2. 02Charging a child as an adult can be necessary to maintain jurisdiction over a dangerous offender beyond the age limits of the juvenile system.
  3. 03The community has a legitimate interest in a formal legal process that adequately addresses the responsibility and public danger associated with extreme violence.
  4. 04Adult prosecution for pre-teens should only occur after a rigorous judicial hearing that considers factors like mental health, competency, and risk to the public.
  5. 05Even when charged as adults, children should be protected from mandatory sentences and imprisonment with grown offenders.
