**The Numbers Behind the Slogan**

The Boston Review's framing essay asks whether we're entering a moment when the gap between our principles and our institutions has been "cast into especially sharp relief." For anyone who has been paying attention to the economic data over the past three decades, the answer is plainly yes—and Occupy deserves credit for finally forcing that data into political conversation.

Consider what Emmanuel Saez and Thomas Piketty's work on income concentration has shown. The share of national income accruing to the top one percent of American households has roughly doubled since the late 1970s, from around ten percent to over twenty percent. The top 0.1 percent has done even better. Meanwhile, real median wages have essentially stagnated since 1973, even as productivity has continued to climb. The Congressional Budget Office's October 2011 report, "Trends in the Distribution of Household Income Between 1979 and 2007," confirmed the picture from a different angle: after-tax income for the top one percent grew by 275 percent over that period; for the middle three quintiles, by under 40 percent.

These are not abstractions. They translate into concrete differences in life expectancy, educational attainment, civic participation, and—crucially—political voice. Martin Gilens's research on policy responsiveness suggests that when the preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the middle class and the poor, it is overwhelmingly the affluent whose preferences prevail in legislation. This is the empirical core of the "we are the 99%" slogan, and it is what makes the slogan more than a rhetorical flourish.

I would push the Boston Review's lead essay to be more specific, though. The language of "principles" and "practices" is appealing but also slightly evasive. The American story is not only one of principles slowly catching up to practice; it is also one of organized economic interests actively rewriting the rules to their advantage. The decline of unionization (from roughly a third of private-sector workers in the 1950s to under seven percent today), the deregulation of finance culminating in the repeal of Glass-Steagall, the shift to a tax code that treats carried interest as capital gains, the explosion of corporate money in elections following *Citizens United*—none of these are accidents of cultural drift. They are the result of decades of concerted effort by well-funded actors.

If Occupy is to translate moral energy into durable change, it needs to be at least as concrete about its diagnosis as about its grievances. The good news is that the policy menu is not mysterious. Restore meaningful progressivity to the tax code. Tax financial transactions. Break up institutions that are too big to fail. Reverse *Citizens United* by constitutional amendment if necessary. Strengthen the right to organize. Expand the Earned Income Tax Credit. Invest in infrastructure and public higher education at a scale appropriate to a wealthy nation. None of this is utopian; most of it was mainstream American policy within living memory.

The objection one hears constantly—that Occupy "has no demands"—is partly disingenuous (the demands are not hard to infer) and partly an artifact of the movement's deliberate horizontality. But I think it is also a real weakness. Social movements that have changed America—abolition, women's suffrage, the labor movement, civil rights—combined moral witness with specific legislative and institutional targets. The March on Washington was for "Jobs and Freedom," with concrete demands attached. The suffragists wanted the Nineteenth Amendment, not a vague reordering of gender relations. Occupy will eventually have to make analogous choices about what, exactly, it is asking the political system to do.

This is not a counsel of compromise so much as a counsel of leverage. Public attention is a scarce resource, and movements that fail to convert attention into institutional outcomes tend to dissipate. The Tea Party, whatever one thinks of its politics, understood this; it ran candidates, took over primaries, and reshaped a major political party in the span of two election cycles. Occupy has more diffuse goals and more skepticism about electoral politics, but the underlying logic is the same.

The most encouraging thing about the moment is that the diagnostic conversation has finally shifted. A year ago, the dominant frame in Washington was deficit hysteria; the threat to American prosperity was supposedly Social Security and Medicare, not concentrated wealth or unemployment. Today, polling shows that majorities across the political spectrum think inequality is too high and that the wealthy do not pay their fair share. That is a genuine political opening.

Whether the opening becomes a turning point depends on whether the energy in Zuccotti Park and a thousand smaller encampments can find institutional expression. The Boston Review's series will be valuable if it helps think through how. The principles are not really in dispute. The question is whether we have the political will and organizational creativity to make them operative again.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Empirical data on income concentration and stagnant wages confirms that American institutions are currently failing to uphold the principle of shared prosperity.
  2. 02Extreme economic inequality has translated into a disproportionate political voice for the affluent, undermining the democratic principle of equal representation.
  3. 03Current inequality is not an accidental drift but the result of organized economic interests actively rewriting policy rules regarding labor, finance, and taxation.
  4. 04To achieve durable change, the movement must adopt a specific policy menu, such as tax reform and strengthening labor rights, which were historically mainstream in American governance.
  5. 05Historical social movements succeeded by combining moral witness with specific legislative targets rather than relying on vague demands.
  6. 06The movement risks dissipating if it fails to convert its successful shift of the public narrative into tangible institutional outcomes and political leverage.

**Channel the Energy**

I want to offer a friendly but somewhat impatient response to the Boston Review's framing essay. I share its sympathies. I think Occupy has been an enormously valuable intervention in American political discourse, and I think the questions the editors pose are the right questions. But I have a worry, which I will state bluntly: movements that do not translate into electoral and legislative outcomes within a reasonable window of time tend to dissipate, and the window for Occupy is closing faster than its participants seem to realize.

A bit of context. I have spent most of my adult life working in and around Democratic politics, including on several campaigns and at advocacy organizations. From that vantage, the past three years have been a wrenching experience. The Obama administration came into office with the largest Democratic majorities since the 1970s, in the context of the deepest economic crisis since the 1930s, with a popular mandate that ought to have permitted ambitious reform. What we got, in policy terms, was the Recovery Act (too small, but real), the Affordable Care Act (a genuine if compromised achievement), and Dodd-Frank (deeply inadequate to the financial sector's structural problems). The midterms then produced a House Republican majority elected in part by mobilizing populist anger that, in a more functional political ecology, would have flowed toward the administration rather than against it.

That populist energy did not flow leftward, in 2009 and 2010, in significant part because there was no organized left to receive it. The Tea Party, well-funded by Koch-aligned donors and amplified by Fox News, captured the energy first. Occupy, two years later, represents a belated effort to do on the left what the Tea Party did on the right. The question is whether it will be more than belated—whether it will succeed in shaping the 2012 election cycle and the policy environment that follows it.

To do so, the movement has to make some choices that, so far, it has resisted making.

First, it needs to articulate demands. I understand the principled objections to doing so. Demands can be co-opted; specific asks can be granted symbolically while the underlying structure is preserved; the diversity of the movement makes consensus on any specific platform difficult. All true. But demands are also how movements connect to the legislative process and how they signal to potential allies what they actually want. The civil rights movement had demands—a Civil Rights Act, a Voting Rights Act, federal protection of voter registration drives, ending of school segregation. The labor movement had demands—the right to organize, minimum wages, the eight-hour day, unemployment insurance. Demands focus political conflict in productive ways.

What might Occupy's demands look like? I would suggest a relatively compact set: restoration of Glass-Steagall or equivalent structural separation in banking; a financial transactions tax; closure of the carried-interest loophole; restoration of pre-2001 top marginal rates and elimination of the preferential rate on capital gains; meaningful relief on student debt and underwater mortgages; a constitutional amendment to permit regulation of campaign expenditures; public financing of federal elections. None of this is utopian. All of it is broadly popular. Most of it has been within mainstream American policy debate within the past generation.

Second, the movement needs to engage with electoral politics, however reluctantly. This does not mean endorsing the Democratic Party, which has been a complicit partner in many of the developments the movement opposes. But it does mean recognizing that legislation is made by elected officials, that elected officials are chosen in elections, and that staying outside the electoral process means leaving its outcomes to others. Specific tactics: identifying primary challengers to incumbents most beholden to financial interests; running insurgent candidates in winnable districts; mobilizing turnout in the 2012 cycle around financial reform and inequality themes; engaging with state-level legislative agendas where some of the most important policymaking now happens.

Third, the movement needs durable organization. The encampments cannot last forever, and as winter sets in, several have already been cleared by local authorities. What remains, beyond the physical occupations? If the answer is "the inspiration," that is not enough. The movement needs working groups that survive the camps, websites and communication infrastructure that persist, funding mechanisms that can support full-time organizers, training programs for new activists, and—yes—designated spokespeople who can engage with media and legislators on behalf of the broader movement. Horizontalism is appealing as an ethos, but successful movements have always combined participatory practices with durable infrastructure.

Fourth, the movement needs to think about coalitions. Labor unions have been the most visible institutional allies, and that relationship should be deepened. But the broader coalition has to include faith communities, immigrants-rights groups, racial-justice organizations, environmental groups, and yes, sympathetic elements of the Democratic Party. Coalitions require compromise. They require listening to constituencies whose priorities differ. They require accepting partial victories rather than holding out for total transformation.

The Boston Review's editors are right that the disjuncture between principles and institutions has been cast into sharp relief. The opening is real. But openings close. The question for the next twelve months is whether Occupy will become a vehicle for reshaping American politics or a memorable but brief outburst that left things essentially as they were. I am rooting for the former. The choices that determine which it will be are being made now.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement must articulate a specific set of policy demands to focus political conflict and provide a clear roadmap for legislative action.
  2. 02Occupy needs to engage directly with electoral politics by running insurgent candidates and mobilizing voters to influence who holds legislative power.
  3. 03The movement must build durable organizational infrastructure and leadership that can survive beyond the temporary physical encampments.
  4. 04Participants must form broad coalitions with established interest groups and be willing to accept incremental compromises to achieve policy victories.

**A Generation's Ledger**

I read the Boston Review's lead essay sitting in a coffee shop in Brooklyn that I cannot really afford, between shifts at a job that pays $14 an hour and doesn't use my degree. I am 27 years old. I owe $58,000 in student loans. My partner owes more. We have decent educations from middling colleges, and we live in a country that we were raised to believe would reward our efforts. So far, it has not.

I write this not to elicit sympathy but to ground the abstractions. The editors ask whether the disjuncture between American principles and American institutions has been cast into especially sharp relief. For my generation, the answer is almost embarrassingly obvious. Whatever principles America may have once approximated, the institutions we have inherited do not deliver on them, and the responsible adults in charge of those institutions have spent the past three years acting as if the situation were normal.

Some numbers. Unemployment among 16-to-24-year-olds is around 17 percent, with much higher rates for young people of color. Underemployment is worse. Approximately two thirds of college graduates leave school with debt, averaging over $25,000 and rising. Total student debt has now passed $1 trillion, exceeding credit card debt for the first time in American history. Unlike almost any other consumer debt, student loans are nearly impossible to discharge in bankruptcy—a legal regime constructed deliberately, over the past two decades, at the urging of the financial industry that profits from servicing them. Wages for recent college graduates have been flat or declining in real terms for over a decade. The cost of the credentials we are told we need to compete has risen at roughly three times the rate of inflation.

This is the ledger my generation inherits. We were told that education was the path to prosperity, and we mortgaged our futures to buy it. We were told that hard work would be rewarded, and we have entered a labor market that has no jobs for us. We were told that politics was a contest of ideas, and we have watched institutions on which our parents and grandparents relied—affordable college, defined-benefit pensions, decent entry-level jobs, mortgages we could plausibly pay off—be hollowed out by a political class that does not seem to feel any obligation to explain itself.

I think this is part of why Occupy resonates. The movement's mood is not, despite occasional rhetoric, primarily revolutionary. It is more like the mood of a generation that has finally noticed that the deal it was offered does not exist. The encampments are full of people who did everything right by the rules they were given, and who are now standing in parks asking what happened. The older protesters—and there are many—are often people who did okay themselves but who watched the same hollowing-out happen to their children.

I have some impatience with the principles framing the Boston Review essay uses, not because the principles are wrong but because they are abstract. What we need is much more concrete. We need debt relief. Federal student loans should be modifiable through bankruptcy like other debts. The government should offer to refinance existing loans at the rates it gives banks—which is to say, at near zero. Income-based repayment should be the default. The for-profit college sector, which has captured an extraordinary share of federal student aid while delivering minimal educational value, should be aggressively regulated or shut down.

We need a federal jobs program at scale. Not a stimulus rounding error, but a serious commitment to direct hiring for infrastructure, energy retrofits, social services, and public goods that the country needs and that millions of unemployed people could provide. The 1930s precedent is right there. The capacity is right there. The only thing missing is political will.

We need housing relief. Millions of mortgages are underwater. The most direct remedy is principal write-downs, which would stabilize household balance sheets and free up consumer demand. The administration has resisted this in deference to the banks, which would take losses. Fine. Let them take losses. They were rescued; their creditors were rescued; their executives' bonuses were rescued. The mortgage holders were not. This needs to be corrected.

We need real financial reform. Glass-Steagall restored. The largest banks broken up. A transactions tax to discourage high-frequency speculation. Criminal prosecutions for the demonstrable frauds of the housing bubble years. The fact that essentially no senior financial executive has faced criminal liability for the largest financial collapse in three generations is a corruption of justice that the political class has so far refused to address.

We need a politics that takes the next generation seriously. We are the demographic majority among young adults; we are the future labor force; we are the future tax base. We are also the constituency most likely to be permanently scarred by the current crisis, in terms of lifetime earnings, family formation, and political socialization. A political system that does not respond to our situation will earn—is earning—our deepening alienation.

The Boston Review series will have my attention if it stays close to this kind of specificity. The principles are not really at issue. The arithmetic is at issue. The arithmetic is unforgiving, and my generation is the one currently being asked to balance the books on behalf of people who refuse to.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Young people have inherited a crushing burden of student debt and stagnant wages that make the promised path to prosperity impossible to achieve.
  2. 02The Occupy movement reflects the disillusionment of a generation that followed the rules only to find that the economic 'deal' they were offered no longer exists.
  3. 03The government must provide immediate debt relief by allowing student loans to be discharged in bankruptcy and refinancing them at lower rates.
  4. 04A large-scale federal jobs program is required to address high unemployment and underemployment among young people.
  5. 05The housing crisis should be addressed through mandatory mortgage principal write-downs, prioritizing homeowners over the financial institutions that were already bailed out.
  6. 06Meaningful financial reform must include breaking up large banks, implementing a transactions tax, and pursuing criminal prosecutions for financial fraud.
  7. 07The political system risks permanent alienation from the next generation if it continues to ignore the specific arithmetic of their economic displacement.

**A Cautionary Note from Civil Rights History**

The Boston Review's lead essay places Occupy in a lineage with abolition, women's suffrage, and the civil rights movement. As a historian of the last of these, I want to offer both encouragement and caution about that comparison.

The encouragement first. American social movements have repeatedly forced the country to confront the gap between its founding language—equality, liberty, consent of the governed—and the realities experienced by significant portions of its population. The movements that succeeded did so by combining moral clarity with organizational sophistication, and by sustaining their efforts over years and decades, not weeks and months. Occupy is showing the beginnings of moral clarity. Whether it will develop the other capacities remains to be seen.

The caution is this: nothing about the civil rights movement was as spontaneous, leaderless, or improvisational as it sometimes appears in retrospect. The Montgomery bus boycott, often remembered as a moment when an ordinary woman refused to move, was in fact carefully planned by an experienced network of activists who had been preparing for the right test case for years. Rosa Parks was a trained organizer and the secretary of the Montgomery NAACP. E. D. Nixon had been working on bus desegregation since the 1940s. Martin Luther King Jr. was selected as the boycott's spokesperson in part because, as a newcomer, he had not yet accumulated enemies. The boycott itself required a sophisticated alternative transportation system, sustained over more than a year, requiring fundraising, dispatching, mechanical maintenance, and constant negotiation.

Behind the public moments stood institutions. The black churches provided meeting space, communication networks, financial infrastructure, and moral authority. The NAACP provided legal expertise. The Highlander Folk School trained activists. The Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, the Congress of Racial Equality each brought distinct strategic capacities. Even when these organizations disagreed sharply—and they did, often bitterly—they constituted an ecology of mutually reinforcing institutions that could absorb setbacks, sustain morale, train new participants, and translate moral energy into legislative and judicial outcomes.

I emphasize this institutional reality because contemporary social movements, including Occupy, often define themselves in opposition to formal organization. The general assemblies, the consensus process, the refusal to designate leaders—these are presented as principled commitments to horizontal democracy. They have undeniable virtues: they make participation accessible, they distribute moral risk, they resist co-optation by ambitious individuals. But they also have costs. Movements without designated leaders cannot negotiate. Movements without institutional memory cannot learn from defeat. Movements without sustained organizational capacity cannot outlast the news cycle.

The civil rights movement was not afraid of leaders, and it was not afraid of institutions. It produced King, Bayard Rustin, Ella Baker, Fannie Lou Hamer, John Lewis, Diane Nash, James Lawson, Septima Clark, and many others. Each of them played distinct roles within a movement that had factions, debates, hierarchies, and chains of command. The movement also produced specific legislative demands—the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965—and organized methodically to achieve them, including by maintaining relationships with sympathetic figures inside the Democratic Party and the federal executive.

A second caution. The civil rights movement was successful in significant part because it was disciplined about violence, and disciplined about its public image. Nonviolence was not merely a tactic; it was a strategic commitment that required intensive training. SNCC's workshops drilled participants in how to absorb blows without responding, how to protect one another, how to maintain dignity under provocation. The result was a series of confrontations—Birmingham, Selma—in which the contrast between disciplined protesters and brutal repression created the moral conditions for federal intervention.

Occupy has not yet been tested in quite this way. There has been confrontation with police, sometimes brutal, and to its credit the movement has largely maintained nonviolent discipline. But it has not invested in the kind of systematic training that sustained the civil rights movement, and there are visible factions—particularly around tactics of property destruction in some West Coast actions—that could easily compromise the movement's broader appeal. Sustaining nonviolent discipline at scale is not automatic. It requires teaching, modeling, and sometimes the exclusion of those who refuse to abide by it.

Third, the civil rights movement was a long project. The Montgomery boycott was in 1955–56. The Civil Rights Act was in 1964, nine years later. The Voting Rights Act was in 1965. Real implementation of school desegregation, fair housing, and voting rights protections stretched well into the 1970s and beyond. The activists who started the movement could not know whether their efforts would yield results in five years or fifty. They committed anyway, building careers and lives around the work.

Occupy is two months old. It would be premature to render any verdict, positive or negative. What I would urge, from the vantage of the earlier movement, is patience, institution-building, and a willingness to learn from movements that came before. The principles invoked by the Boston Review's lead are correct. The hard work is in the institutions.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The civil rights movement's success relied on a sophisticated network of formal institutions and trained organizers rather than spontaneous or leaderless action.
  2. 02Occupy's commitment to horizontal democracy and lack of designated leadership hinders its ability to negotiate, learn from defeat, and sustain long-term efforts.
  3. 03Effective nonviolence requires systematic training and the enforcement of tactical discipline to maintain the moral high ground and public support.
  4. 04Meaningful social change is a decades-long project that requires building durable organizations capable of outlasting short-term media attention.
  5. 05The movement must be willing to translate moral energy into specific legislative demands and engage with existing political power structures.
