"The essay argues that while federalism and local police involvement are valuable, they cannot substitute for a robust statutory and legal framework designed to constrain executive discretion in domestic intelligence."

Daniel Richman concludes his essay by observing that "the primary sources of accountability and due moderation will have to come from institutional structure" because judicial and legislative oversight of executive discretion in this domain is so limited. I want to focus on that concession, because I think it is the most important sentence in the essay and also the most troubling.

What Richman is acknowledging — though he glides past it — is that the post-9/11 executive has constructed a domestic security regime that operates substantially outside the legal architecture the United States built up over the course of the twentieth century to constrain government investigation of its own citizens. Material witness warrants used as preventive detention. National Security Letters used to bypass grand jury process. The Attorney General's revised investigative guidelines, which authorize the FBI to attend public meetings and surveil religious and political gatherings without predication. Administrative subpoenas of records once protected by judicial process. The "enemy combatant" designation applied to U.S. citizens. The whole infrastructure of secret detentions, secret hearings, and secret evidence that the Inspector General's reports have begun to document.

Most of these moves have been judicially or congressionally untested, or tested only at the margins. The Supreme Court's decisions this past June in Hamdi and Rasul — which Richman briefly invokes — were narrow, hedged, and procedural; they will discipline a small subset of cases involving formal military detention but leave the broader administrative and intelligence apparatus essentially untouched. Congress, for its part, passed the PATRIOT Act in October 2001 with virtually no deliberation and has, three years on, largely declined to revisit its provisions, even as the four-year sunsets approach.

This is the institutional context for Richman's federalism argument. The federalism argument says: since law cannot constrain the executive in this domain, structure must. Local police, through their political accountability and community embeddedness, will provide a check that statute and court cannot. This is a creative move and I respect its ingenuity. But I think it is a mistake — perhaps a generous mistake, but a mistake nonetheless — to accept the premise that legal constraint is unavailable here.

Legal constraint is available. It is just not being constructed, because the political class has not insisted on it. The framework legislation for domestic intelligence that has long existed in the United Kingdom, in Germany, in Canada, in Australia could be constructed here. A statutory definition of "domestic intelligence collection," with predication requirements, minimization rules, retention limits, and audit obligations, is well within Congress's power. A FISA-like court with jurisdiction over a broader range of domestic intelligence activities — not just electronic surveillance — could be created. An inspector general for the intelligence community with cross-agency jurisdiction and broader public reporting authority is also doable. A statutory framework for watchlisting, including notice and challenge procedures for U.S. persons, has been proposed by serious commentators and is being ignored.

Richman's response to all this might be that none of it is going to happen, given current political realities. He may be right. But that is a forecast about politics, not a finding about the structure of the problem. The Church Committee reforms of the late 1970s were also unlikely to happen until they did. Political windows open. The work of advocates and scholars in the meantime is to articulate what should fill those windows when they open — and to insist that the right institutional response to a domestic intelligence emergency is law, not workarounds.

I worry that Richman's essay, by treating legal underdevelopment as a given and federalism as a substitute, reinforces the political pattern that produced the underdevelopment in the first place. If the message to the executive branch is "we will work around the absence of statutory constraint with creative federalism," the executive has even less reason to support the construction of statutory constraint. The federalism argument becomes, against its author's intent, a legitimation strategy for the unbounded administrative state in domestic security.

This is, I should say, a common pattern in post-9/11 commentary. Many thoughtful scholars have offered second-best solutions — better internal review, more transparency, sunsets, ombudsmen — that operate inside the basic frame of expansive executive authority. Each is reasonable on its own terms. Cumulatively, they normalize a constitutional posture that should not be normalized. The premise of the American security state used to be that domestic spying was sharply limited by the criminal-procedure framework and the First Amendment. That premise is now widely treated as quaint. It should not be.

What does the alternative look like, concretely? A 21st-century equivalent of the Church Committee reforms, embedded in statute, that would: (1) codify the line between criminal investigation and intelligence collection, including the predication required for each; (2) establish a statutory framework for domestic intelligence comparable to FISA but broader, with judicial oversight of non-content collection programs; (3) restructure watchlisting under clear legal standards with redress procedures; (4) require congressional reporting on domestic intelligence activities comparable to what is required of the foreign intelligence agencies; (5) provide remedies — including damages and exclusion — for unlawful collection that, unlike current doctrine, are actually available in practice.

These are not pipe dreams. They are the kinds of structural reforms that other democracies have implemented and that the United States itself implemented after the abuses of the 1960s and 1970s. They are also, I would argue, more durable than Richman's federalism solution, which depends on a continuing alignment of political interest between cities and immigrant communities that may not always hold.

I do not think Richman is wrong that local police should have more voice in the construction of the post-9/11 security apparatus. I think he is wrong that this voice can substitute for what is missing — namely, a serious legal architecture for domestic intelligence in a constitutional democracy. The right fight, in my view, is for that architecture. Federalism is at best a complement to it, not a replacement.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The post-9/11 executive branch has constructed a security regime that operates largely outside traditional legal architecture and judicial oversight.
  2. 02Accepting federalism as a workaround for the lack of legal constraints inadvertently legitimizes an unbounded administrative state and discourages the creation of necessary statutory limits.
  3. 03Meaningful legal constraints, such as codified predication requirements and expanded judicial oversight of intelligence collection, are politically achievable and have been successfully implemented in other democratic nations.
  4. 04A statutory architecture for domestic security is more durable and reliable than a federalism-based solution that depends on the shifting political interests of local municipalities.
  5. 05The focus of reform should be on establishing a 21st-century equivalent of the Church Committee reforms rather than normalizing expansive executive authority through second-best institutional solutions.
