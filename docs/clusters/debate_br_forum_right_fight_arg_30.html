"Integrating local police into the federal intelligence network violates the constitutional principle of federalism and creates a dangerous centralization of power that threatens civil liberties."

Daniel Richman’s essay “The Right Fight” advocates for a deep integration of state and local law enforcement into the federal domestic intelligence apparatus. He views this integration through the lens of political science, arguing that the competing interests of local and federal bureaucracies will result in a balanced system that protects civil liberties while preventing terrorism. However, Richman’s pragmatic approach ignores a foundational principle of the American republic: the constitutional separation of powers through federalism. By encouraging the blurring of lines between local police power and federal national security power, Richman advocates for the very centralization of authority that the Founders explicitly sought to prevent.

The United States Constitution establishes a system of dual sovereignty. The federal government is granted specific, enumerated powers—chief among them national defense, border security, and foreign intelligence. The states retain the broad, unenumerated "police power"—the authority to regulate behavior to protect the health, safety, and morals of their citizens. This division is not merely a bureaucratic convenience; it is a vital structural safeguard for liberty. The Founders were deeply suspicious of a standing national army and entirely opposed to the concept of a national police force.

When Richman proposes that local police departments should become the primary "eyes and ears" of the federal counterterrorism network, he is essentially proposing the creation of a national police force by proxy. If 700,000 local police officers are tasked with gathering intelligence for federal databases, enforcing federal immigration laws, and responding to federal threat directives, they cease to be local peacekeepers. They become the decentralized foot soldiers of the federal executive branch.

This federalization of local police subverts the Tenth Amendment and fundamentally alters the relationship between the citizen and the state. When a citizen interacts with a local police officer, they are interacting with an agent of their immediate community, constrained by state law, local ordinances, and the oversight of locally elected officials. If that same officer is simultaneously acting as an agent of the FBI or the Department of Homeland Security, the lines of authority and accountability are hopelessly blurred.

Richman attempts to assuage fears of this centralization by arguing that local police will push back against federal overreach to maintain community trust. He writes that "the chief whose only desire is to ensure local peace will be prone to give the feds too little help, not too much." This is a profound misunderstanding of how power and money flow in a federal system.

The federal government possesses the power of the purse. Through homeland security grants, equipment transfers, and the threat of withholding funds, the federal government can exert overwhelming coercive pressure on state and local governments. We have already seen this dynamic in the expansion of federal criminal law over the past three decades, where federal funding conditioned on state compliance has effectively nationalized large swaths of criminal justice policy, from drunk driving thresholds to drug sentencing. If the federal government demands that local police participate in intelligence gathering as a condition of receiving vital funding, local resistance will inevitably crumble.

Furthermore, Richman’s model invites the reverse problem: the localization of national security. National security is the paramount responsibility of the federal government, requiring a unified strategy, specialized expertise, and an overriding focus on foreign threats. Injecting local political concerns into this domain is highly dangerous. If a local mayor can dictate how federal intelligence is gathered in his city—perhaps demanding that federal agents ignore certain politically powerful groups, or conversely, demanding heavy-handed tactics against marginalized groups to appease voters—national security becomes subordinate to ward politics.

Richman praises the idea that "the contours of such local struggles will undoubtedly vary from place to place." But variation is the enemy of a coherent national defense. The rules governing counterterrorism intelligence, the protection of civil liberties, and the sharing of classified information cannot be allowed to vary wildly between Portland, Oregon, and Boise, Idaho, based on local political whims. A fragmented, locally negotiated approach to national security creates massive vulnerabilities that sophisticated terrorist networks will easily exploit.

The solution to the intelligence failures of 9/11 is not to mash local and federal law enforcement into an amorphous, entangled web. The solution is to strictly define and vigorously maintain their distinct constitutional roles. The federal government (FBI, CIA, NSA) must break down its own internal barriers and take full responsibility for domestic and international counterterrorism intelligence. Local police should focus on what they do best: responding to 911 calls, investigating local crimes, and maintaining public order.

If local police encounter evidence of a federal crime or a terror plot during their routine duties, they should, of course, pass that information to federal authorities. But they must not be tasked with proactively gathering intelligence on behalf of the federal government. To preserve the constitutional order and protect the liberties of the citizenry, we must resist the siren song of "integration" and vigorously defend the boundary between local police power and federal national security.
