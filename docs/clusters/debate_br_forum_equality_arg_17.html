"While the call for equal flourishing is a necessary corrective to the focus on equal opportunity, it is insufficient unless it explicitly incorporates reparative racial justice to address the structural legacies of white supremacy."

Christine Sypnowich’s call to move beyond equal opportunity toward equal flourishing is a timely and necessary corrective to the shallow egalitarianism that dominates contemporary politics. Yet her essay, for all its sophistication, suffers from a glaring omission: it says next to nothing about racial inequality and the specific demands of racial justice. By advancing a universalist socialism focused on class and community, Sypnowich reproduces a longstanding blind spot of the left—the assumption that race is a subset of class and that universal programs will automatically resolve racial disparities. The history of the United States shows otherwise. A truly egalitarian society must confront the legacy of slavery, Jim Crow, and ongoing structural racism not as an afterthought but as central to its project. That means reparations, not just universal flourishing.

Sypnowich critiques the focus on equal opportunity by pointing to its compatibility with persistent inequality. She notes that simply removing legal barriers does not undo the effects of entrenched disadvantage. She then argues for a socialist community that ensures everyone has the material and social bases for a flourishing life. But the inequalities that scar America are not simply class inequalities; they are deeply racialized. The median white family has roughly ten times the wealth of the median Black family, a gap that has barely budged in decades. This disparity is not primarily the result of differential “opportunity” in the abstract or even of class position: it is the direct legacy of centuries of state-sanctioned exploitation, from the theft of land and labor to redlining, racial terror, and mass incarceration. A universal program of wealth redistribution or equalization of resources, without race-specific measures, would leave this hierarchy intact.

Sypnowich’s appeal to “socialist community” and “egalitarian ethos” assumes a baseline of shared citizenship and mutual responsibility. But the American polity was founded on a racial contract that excluded Black people from full membership. The solidarity she invokes does not exist between Black and white Americans in the way she might hope. As Ta-Nehisi Coates powerfully argued in his 2014 essay “The Case for Reparations,” the oppression of African Americans has been a bipartisan project sustained over centuries, and any credible effort to address inequality must begin with a national reckoning and restitution. Simply instituting universal basic services, child allowances, and public works—all good policies in themselves—will not close the racial wealth gap when that gap is perpetuated by ongoing discrimination in housing, credit, and employment, and by the cumulative weight of inherited disadvantage.

Sypnowich’s framework of equal flourishing also risks erasing the distinctive cultural and political dimensions of Black liberation. She rightly insists that flourishing includes “non-material aspects” like relationships, recognition, and self-respect. For African Americans, self-respect has been bound up with the struggle against white supremacy and the demand for acknowledgment of historical wrongs. Reparations are not just a material transfer; they are a moral act of repair that signifies the polity’s commitment to atonement. A colorblind socialism that merely promises equal resources going forward can look like yet another evasion of this demand. The socialist tradition has its own history of marginalizing Black voices, from the labor movement’s exclusion of Black workers to the complicity of some left groups in the racial order. Sypnowich’s essay, by drawing almost exclusively on classical European socialist thinkers like Marx, Tawney, and Morris, and on contemporary white philosophers, inadvertently perpetuates that erasure.

There is a rich Black radical tradition that links racial justice with economic equality. W.E.B. Du Bois’s “Black Reconstruction” showed how the fleeting experiment of Reconstruction combined land redistribution, political participation, and the formation of cooperative institutions—an early vision of what Sypnowich might call socialism. But it was undone by racist terrorism and the withdrawal of federal commitment. Martin Luther King, Jr., in his final years, championed a “Bill of Rights for the Disadvantaged” that would have functioned as a kind of domestic Marshall Plan for Black Americans, explicitly targeting the legacy of slavery. Today, scholars like William Darity Jr. and A. Kirsten Mullen have made the comprehensive case that reparations are the only means to achieve true equality of outcomes for Black Americans. This is not a call for opportunity but for a specific, targeted outcome: the elimination of the racial wealth gap through direct payments and systemic overhaul.

Sypnowich might reply that her emphasis on outcomes implicitly includes racial disparities, and that a socialist government would give everyone what they need to flourish, which would disproportionately benefit those historically behind. But this is wishful. Universal policies, even if they compress overall inequality, can actually widen relative racial gaps. For example, general increases in the minimum wage help all low-wage workers, but if Black workers remain overrepresented in the most precarious jobs and face discrimination in promotion, the racial gap in earnings and wealth may persist or even grow. Only race-conscious policies—affirmative action in asset building, targeted investment in Black communities, direct reparations payments to descendants of enslaved people—can ensure that equal outcomes are actually equal across racial groups.

An egalitarian society worthy of the name must therefore embrace both universal social provision and reparative justice. The two are not mutually exclusive, but they are distinct. Sypnowich’s “socialism” could encompass this, but only if it explicitly centers racial justice from the start. That means listening to Black organizers and theorists who have long insisted that class struggle must be anti-racist struggle. It means acknowledging that the ideal of equal flourishing cannot be achieved until the specifically racial dimensions of American inequality are directly addressed. Opportunity talk may be insufficient, but so too is a class-first universalism that fails to name and dismantle white supremacy. The path forward is a socialist politics that is unapologetically reparative and anti-racist.
