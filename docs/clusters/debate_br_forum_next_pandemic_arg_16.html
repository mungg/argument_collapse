"While Jasanoff’s call for humility correctly identifies the failures of elite preparedness, it overlooks the fact that the pandemic's greatest failures were rooted in material global inequalities and vaccine nationalism rather than mere epistemic overconfidence."

Sheila Jasanoff's call for humility resonates differently depending on where you sit. For American readers, her essay offers a critique of elite overreach from someone who is herself a Harvard professor and member of the policy establishment. For much of the world, however, the pandemic revealed a different hierarchy of failures: not primarily a crisis of overconfident preparedness but a crisis of global inequality in which wealthy nations hoarded vaccines, blocked patent waivers, and treated the virus as solved at home while it devastated countries with no access to doses.

Jasanoff's analysis is strongest when examining domestic policy but thins considerably when the scope widens. She notes that the GHSI placed the United States and United Kingdom at the top and small, war-torn states at the bottom. But this ranking was not simply wrong about preparedness; it reflected a worldview that equates health security with the capacity for coordinated state action—a metric designed by American institutions that implicitly assumed their own model as the standard. Countries that scored poorly often lacked not humility but resources, colonial legacies that disrupted indigenous governance, or populations too young and mobile to implement shelter-in-place orders. Their failures were not epistemic but material, and no amount of humility in Washington would have changed the fact that they had no vaccines.

The essay mentions that "the virus that should have been conquered" continued to mutate and spread, but it does not adequately address why. The rise of variants was not simply a consequence of insufficient humility in wealthy nations. It was a predictable consequence of vast inequities in vaccination. As long as large populations remain unvaccinated, the virus has opportunities to evolve. The Omicron variant, which emerged in southern Africa after months of low vaccination coverage, made this dynamic tragically visible. Jasanoff's technologies of humility would have asked who was left out of planning—but the answer was not that their perspectives were ignored. It was that they could not afford to participate in a system designed to enrich pharmaceutical companies in donor nations.

The patent waiver debate that roiled international bodies in 2021 illustrated the limits of humility as a policy principle. India and South Africa proposed suspending intellectual property protections for COVID vaccines to enable generic production in lower-income countries. The proposal was framed in terms of global solidarity and shared sacrifice—in the language of humility. But wealthy nations, representing populations that had already been vaccinated, blocked the waiver for months, citing the need to protect innovation incentives. This was not a failure of humility. It was a failure of interest alignment in a global system that distributes vulnerability based on wealth. No amount of institutional redesign in Washington would have produced different outcomes unless the underlying incentive structure changed.

Jasanoff is right that preparedness frameworks assumed a world that did not exist—a world of unified national responses, compliant populations, and sufficient state capacity. But the world that does exist is not simply a matter of attitudes or institutions. It is structured by material inequalities that shape who can afford to wait, who can work from home, who has access to healthcare, and who lives in multigenerational households where isolation is impossible. Humility in the face of uncertainty is a privilege of the secure. For those facing immediate survival pressures, the luxury of epistemic modesty is irrelevant.

The essay quotes Alexandre White saying that pandemic failings "are rooted in the West's self-styled superiority to the rest of the world, especially when it comes to disease." This is closer to the mark than Jasanoff's framing suggests. The failures were not primarily failures of humility but failures of solidarity—of treating global health as a market opportunity rather than a common good, of building vaccine nationalism rather than vaccine equity, of assuming that the solutions developed in Boston and Berlin would work in Botswana and Bangladesh. Genuine humility would require not just better institutional mechanisms but a fundamental reorientation of global health governance away from charity and toward justice. That is a political project, not an epistemological one, and Jasanoff's essay, for all its sophistication, underestimates the distance between acknowledging uncertainty and changing the structures that produce it.

  5. 05Addressing global health crises requires a political reorientation toward justice and resource equity rather than just an epistemological shift in how experts handle uncertainty.
