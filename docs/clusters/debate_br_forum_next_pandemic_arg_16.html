"The failures of pandemic policy were not caused by a lack of intellectual humility, but rather by the inherent limitations of centralized public health authority in a pluralistic, liberal democracy."

Sheila Jasanoff's essay is a sophisticated account of pandemic policy failures, but it errs in attributing those failures to insufficient humility when they more accurately reflect the predictable consequences of expansive public health authority. Her framework assumes that the problem was too much confidence in the wrong direction; I would argue the problem was too much confidence in the right direction—confidence that experts knew best how to manage individual behavior for collective benefit.

Jasanoff critiques preparedness as a "heroic, can-do posture" that "bravely denies the possibility of defeat." But this framing conflates two different things: preparedness as a general disposition and preparedness as a justification for specific interventions. The latter is what generated the controversies of 2020 and 2021. Mask mandates, business closures, travel restrictions, vaccine passports—these were not failures of having a playbook. They were consequences of treating a respiratory virus as a problem that could be managed through the coordinated exercise of state power. That approach failed not because policymakers lacked humility but because the public, or significant portions of it, rejected the premise that such power was legitimate.

Jasanoff quotes Jay S. Kaufman saying that "the U.S. Achilles's heel is not lack of humility; it is a toxic social ideology rooted in a blind obsession with individual freedom." I would go further: that "obsession" is not a pathology but a feature of a constitutional order designed to limit centralized authority. The founders of the republic did not trust unified command. They separated powers, protected federalism, and enumerated individual rights precisely because they feared the consequences of concentrating decision-making in expert hands. The pandemic revealed not a failure of humility but a conflict between two visions of legitimate governance: one that prioritizes collective outcomes and accepts the costs of centralized direction, and one that prioritizes individual liberty and accepts the costs of decentralized response.

The GHSI's top rankings for the United States and United Kingdom, which Jasanoff rightly notes proved tragically inaccurate, were not simply wrong about national capabilities. They were measuring a particular kind of capacity—the ability to implement coordinated responses—that may be inherently limited in societies where such coordination is contested. Countries that scored lower but performed better did so not because they were humbler but because they had different political structures, different relationships between state and citizen, and different cultural expectations about compliance with public health guidance.

Jasanoff argues that humility "permits steps to be taken here and now in order to forestall worst-case scenarios later" through "technologies of humility" that incorporate diverse perspectives. But the history of such attempts suggests they tend to legitimate decisions made on other grounds. The Schmidt Futures Forum she describes featured distinguished experts reflecting on their own failures—a process of introspection that changed nothing about the power structures that produced those failures. Genuine humility, in her sense, would require those with authority to share it with those who lack it. But the essay never explains how this is supposed to happen within existing institutional frameworks.

The mask wars, the vaccine resistance, the school board conflicts—these were not failures of humility. They were expressions of a pluralistic society in which different communities held different values, trusted different sources of authority, and made different calculations about acceptable risk. Jasanoff sees this as a problem to be solved by better policy. I see it as a condition to be navigated, with imperfect tools, through the slow work of democratic persuasion rather than the swift exercise of expert judgment. The pandemic taught us that "following the science" was always a political choice, not a neutral one—because science could not resolve questions about what risks were acceptable, whose welfare should be prioritized, and how to balance present harms against future ones.

Humility might have softened the edges of pandemic politics, but it would not have resolved its fundamental tensions. Those tensions are built into the structure of liberal democracy, and they will persist regardless of how much we introspect about our epistemic limitations. The better lesson of the pandemic may be this: prepare for the next crisis by building resilient systems that can function even when unified command fails—by strengthening local capacity, diversifying supply chains, and maintaining redundancy in critical infrastructure. These are not humble aspirations, but they are realistic ones.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The conflict over pandemic measures like mandates and closures reflects a fundamental disagreement over the legitimacy of state power rather than a failure of expert playbooks.
  2. 02The American constitutional order is intentionally designed to prioritize individual liberty and decentralized response over the unified command structures that public health experts favor.
  3. 03The inaccuracy of global preparedness rankings stems from measuring technical capacity while ignoring the political and cultural resistance inherent in societies that contest coordinated state action.
  4. 04The 'technologies of humility' proposed by Jasanoff often serve to legitimate existing power structures rather than genuinely redistributing authority to marginalized voices.
  5. 05Democratic persuasion and the building of resilient, redundant local systems are more realistic solutions than attempting to solve value-based political conflicts through improved expert judgment.
  6. 06Science is incapable of resolving the essential political questions of risk tolerance and the balancing of competing social harms.
