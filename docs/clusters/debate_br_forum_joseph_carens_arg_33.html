"The debate over irregular migration should unbundle legal residency from the path to citizenship, as granting durable residence without immediate citizenship addresses urgent humanitarian concerns while potentially overcoming political deadlock."

I want to register a worry about the unstated assumption underlying not just Carens' essay but the entire amnesty debate: that the choice is between deportation and permanent residence with a path to citizenship. There is a third possibility that almost no one discusses in the American context but that has substantial European precedent: long-term legal residence without a path to citizenship.

Carens' framework essentially equates legalization with eventual citizenship. He writes that irregular migrants who have crossed the threshold of belonging "should acquire a legal right of permanent residence and all the rights that go with that, including eventual access to citizenship." But these are separable. There is a long tradition, particularly in continental Europe, of granting durable legal residence rights without offering citizenship, or with offering citizenship only after additional waiting periods and requirements. Germany did this with its Turkish guest workers for decades. The model has costs, particularly the long-term creation of resident populations without political voice, but it is not without merits.

What if the right policy response to the eleven million is to grant durable residence — protection from deportation, work authorization, access to services, freedom from the daily fear that Miguel Sanchez and his family live with — without immediately granting a path to citizenship? This would address the central concerns Carens raises: the disproportion between removal and the original wrong, the harm of family separation, the cruelty of deporting long-settled residents. It would not address the additional concerns about democratic membership and political voice, but it would address the most urgent humanitarian concerns. And it might be politically achievable in a way that full legalization-plus-citizenship is not.

I raise this because I think the conflation of legalization and citizenship is doing some of the work that makes the amnesty debate so politically deadlocked. The two are not the same. Legalization is, in the first instance, about protection from removal and the ability to participate openly in the economy and civic life. Citizenship is about political membership: the right to vote, the right to run for office, the right to call upon the state for diplomatic protection abroad, the right to bring family members into the country. These are different goods, and they can be unbundled.

The opposition to amnesty draws much of its force from the citizenship dimension. The image that animates opposition is not the long-resident migrant continuing to live and work in her current community. It is the long-resident migrant eventually voting, eventually participating in deciding what kind of country the United States will be. The first image is sympathetic to most Americans; the second is more contested. By bundling them, the standard amnesty proposal makes the politically harder one a condition of the politically easier one.

There are several reasons to consider this kind of unbundling. First, there is a question of consent. Carens argues that long-resident migrants have become members of the political community and should be recognized as such. But the political community itself has not consented to this recognition. In a democracy, the consent of the existing political community to the addition of new members has some weight. A policy that grants durable residence without citizenship is one that has, in effect, the political community's tacit consent (it can be enacted by ordinary legislation, and the costs to existing citizens are limited). A policy that grants citizenship requires a stronger affirmative endorsement from the political community, which has often been hard to obtain.

Second, there is a question of integration. Citizenship is, in part, a recognition that integration has occurred. Granting citizenship before integration has fully occurred can be premature. Long-resident migrants are at varying stages of integration: some are fully integrated, others less so. A policy that grants durable residence first and citizenship later, on standard naturalization terms, allows for a fuller integration process before the political dimension of membership is conferred.

Third, there is a question of reversibility. Durable residence is in principle reversible — it can be revoked for cause, it can be allowed to lapse, it can be conditioned on continuing requirements. Citizenship, once granted, is essentially irrevocable. A policy that grants durable residence preserves more flexibility for the political community to respond to subsequent developments. This is not a knock-down argument — Carens would say the irrevocability of citizenship is a feature, not a bug — but it is a consideration.

Against this, there are serious arguments for the standard package. Durable residence without political voice creates the very "underclass" problem that Plyler v. Doe warned against. The lesson of the German Turkish guest workers is that durable-residence-without-citizenship can produce multi-generational populations who are integrated economically but excluded politically, with consequences that are bad for everyone. The political dimension of membership is not separable from the economic and social dimensions in the long run. A policy that grants durable residence but withholds citizenship indefinitely is, in effect, creating a permanent second-class status.

These arguments have force. I do not want to come down too firmly on the unbundling side. What I want to argue is that the discussion should be more nuanced than Carens' essay suggests. The choice is not between full legalization-with-citizenship and continued deportation. There is a range of intermediate options — durable residence, conditional residence, time-limited legalization with renewal mechanisms, distinct paths to citizenship for different categories of long-resident migrants — and these deserve more attention than they typically receive.

In practical terms, I would propose something like the following: a durable residence grant for the existing eleven million, on terms similar to what Carens proposes, but with naturalization following a substantial additional waiting period (say, ten years from the date of legalization) and on terms similar to those that apply to other lawful permanent residents. This would address the immediate humanitarian concerns; it would preserve the citizenship dimension as a matter requiring further integration and consent; it might be more politically achievable than the standard proposal; and it would preserve the long-term ideal of full membership for those who continue to live in the country.

Carens' moral framework can accommodate this. His central concern is the harm of removal and the disproportion between that harm and the original wrong. Durable residence addresses these directly. The citizenship dimension is additional, important, but separable. The amnesty debate would be better, I think, if we treated these dimensions as distinct rather than fused. We might find more agreement than we currently can.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Granting durable residence alone directly addresses the moral concerns regarding the cruelty of deportation and the disproportionate harm of uprooting long-settled residents.
  2. 02Separating residency from citizenship respects the political community's right to consent to new members, as citizenship involves a higher level of political integration and affirmative endorsement.
  3. 03A policy of durable residence allows for a more gradual integration process, ensuring that the political dimension of membership is conferred only after integration has fully occurred.
  4. 04Unbundling these rights provides the state with more flexibility and reversibility compared to the irrevocable nature of granted citizenship.
  5. 05A compromise model involving durable residence followed by a substantial waiting period for naturalization preserves the long-term ideal of full membership while being more politically achievable.
