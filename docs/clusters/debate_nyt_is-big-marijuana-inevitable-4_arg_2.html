"The federal government must resolve the conflict between federal and state law by rescheduling marijuana and establishing a clear regulatory framework that allows for state-level experimentation."

The real obstacle to sensible marijuana policy isn't whether Big Marijuana is inevitable—it's that federal law remains completely out of step with state reality. Until we resolve this fundamental tension, every debate about regulatory frameworks misses the larger point.

Federal law still classifies marijuana as a Schedule I controlled substance with no accepted medical use and high abuse potential. This creates a chaotic situation where states can legalize, businesses can operate, but banks can't serve them without risking federal prosecution. Patients in medical marijuana states face legal uncertainty. Researchers face barriers to studying the drug's effects. And law enforcement agencies receive mixed signals about when to enforce federal law.

The Obama administration's recent guidance suggesting it won't interfere with state legalization schemes has helped, but this is a policy position, not law. It could change tomorrow with a new administration. We need Congress to act—to reschedule marijuana, establish a clear federal-state framework, and let the experimentation begin in earnest.

Beyond federalism, there's a deeper question about what legalization should actually mean. Pure commercialization creates markets that drive consumption, as we've seen with alcohol and tobacco. Pure decriminalization—allowing possession but no legal commerce—creates ambiguity and limits consumer protections. Neither extreme satisfies.

The best path forward combines federal reform with state flexibility. Remove marijuana from Schedule I, establish baseline protections for public health and safety, and then let states design systems that reflect their values. Some states will choose robust commercial regulation like Oregon's; others may prefer more restrictive models. The diversity of approaches isn't a bug—it's how we learn what works.

We have an opportunity to craft marijuana policy that avoids the extremes of both prohibition and commercialized legalization. Let's not squander it by fighting the last war instead of preparing for the future.
