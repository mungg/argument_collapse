"The news media should identify mass shooters for the sake of public accountability and accuracy, but must exercise voluntary restraint to avoid sensationalizing the killer or encouraging copycat crimes."

The press should not pretend a mass murderer is anonymous in a public event of enormous consequence. Names matter for accuracy, for investigating warning signs, for examining failures by institutions, and for preventing rumors that can wrongly implicate others. But naming is not the same as branding. The media can report identity once, when confirmed, without turning the killer into the protagonist of the story.

There is enough evidence to justify restraint. Suicide researchers have long warned that sensational coverage can encourage imitation; guidelines urge avoiding romanticized descriptions, repeated images and step-by-step accounts. Mass shootings are not identical to suicides, but many perpetrators are suicidal, grievance-driven and hungry for recognition. Columbine has become a template for some later attackers. The Virginia Tech gunman mailed photos and video to NBC precisely because he wanted a stage. When news organizations loop such images, publish manifestoes at length, or reduce the crime to a killer’s “dark genius,” they may be helping deliver the reward.

A responsible standard would be: identify the shooter after official confirmation; use no glamour photographs; avoid publishing manifestoes except for brief excerpts needed to establish motive; do not dwell on weaponry, tactics or body counts in a competitive scoreboard style; and stop using the killer’s name in headlines unless there is a clear news reason. Profiles should be careful and unsentimental, emphasizing patterns that help the public understand risk without offering mythology.

This restraint should be voluntary, not imposed by sheriffs or legislators. Government officials have their own incentives to suppress facts, and the public needs independent reporting on police response, gun access, mental health systems, campus security and missed warnings. The answer is not silence but proportion.

The victims, survivors and community should receive more attention than the killer. Journalism cannot eliminate copycats, but it can refuse to make infamy the most visible prize.

"The news media should report the identity of mass shooters sparingly for the sake of public accountability while avoiding the sensationalized coverage that grants them the notoriety they seek."

Sheriff Hanlin's instinct is understandable, and the contagion concern is not fanciful. Researchers like Sherry Towers have produced statistical evidence suggesting mass shootings cluster in time, much as suicides do after high-profile coverage—the so-called Werther effect that has long shaped responsible reporting guidelines on suicide. Several recent killers, from Newtown to Isla Vista to Roseburg, left manifestos or online trails indicating they studied their predecessors and craved the notoriety they saw conferred. If fame is part of the motive, the press is part of the incentive structure.

But a blanket "Don't Name Them" rule asks journalism to do something it cannot honestly do: pretend a central fact of a public event does not exist. Names matter for accountability—for understanding how a killer obtained weapons, slipped past mental health systems, or signaled intent online. Suppression also breeds rumor and conspiracy, and it sets a precedent that police can dictate which facts citizens are allowed to know. That is a precedent worth resisting even when the sheriff's motives are sympathetic.

The workable path lies between saturation and silence. Outlets can name the perpetrator once, sparingly, and refuse to run the trophy photos, GoPro footage, or manifesto excerpts that killers curate for posterity. They can decline the wall-to-wall biographical treatment—the yearbook montages, the bedroom tours—that converts a murderer into a dark celebrity. They can foreground victims, first responders, and the policy questions the shooting raises. Anderson Cooper's stated practice of avoiding the Newtown shooter's name, and the AP's tightened guidelines on suicide coverage, show such restraint is feasible without censorship.

In short: report the who, but stop auditioning killers for the antihero role they are seeking.

The sheriff’s plea not to name the Oregon shooter taps into a growing unease: does our ritualized coverage of mass murderers transform them into anti-heroes for the next disturbed individual? By 2015, research makes that fear hard to dismiss. Studies show that many rampage shooters explicitly seek notoriety, and criminologists warn of a contagion effect — saturation coverage of one attack significantly raises the probability of another within weeks. The “Don’t Name Them” campaign is built on a straightforward premise: deny the killer the posthumous celebrity he craved, and you remove a lethal incentive.

Yet a blanket ban on naming a perpetrator oversimplifies both journalism and public safety. Journalism’s core duty is to provide an accurate account, and that includes the “who.” Without the name, we cannot reliably investigate warning signs, radicalization patterns, or the pathways to weapons — knowledge crucial for prevention. Additionally, suppressing a name can fuel conspiracy theories and corrode trust in reporting precisely when communities need clarity.

The challenge, then, is not to erase the shooter but to de-center him. A trauma-informed media would make a deliberate shift in framing. Rather than leading broadcasts with the killer’s self-styled manifesto photos or repeating his name dozens of times, outlets should prioritize the faces and stories of the victims, the heroism of first responders, and the community’s recovery. The public does need to learn the name, but they do not need it emblazoned in headlines or isolated from the devastation it caused. Coverage should quickly pivot to systemic context — mental health failures, domestic violence red flags, access to firearms — without mythologizing the perpetrator as an enigmatic monster. Language matters: terms like “evil genius” not only mislead but inadvertently glorify.

Ethical coverage treats the shooter as a footnote in a much larger story about human loss and societal failure. He gets named, factually, several paragraphs down — a bureaucratic detail, not a brand. The spotlight stays on the lives we lost and the urgent changes we owe them. That balance honors the public’s right to know while refusing to provide a script for the next attack.

It should not recite his manifesto as though it were a sacred text. It should not produce breathless packages about his favorite songs, wardrobe, dating life or supposed genius. It should not rank him in a league table of mass murderers. But it should tell the truth about who he was, what he did, what he believed and how he was able to do it.
