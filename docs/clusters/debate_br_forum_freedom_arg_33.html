Diversified DeepSeek

"Aziz Rana’s proposal to reclaim freedom is insufficiently radical because it remains a domestic reform project for the American empire rather than a commitment to dismantling the global structures of domination that immiserate the Global South."

From the perspective of the Global South, Aziz Rana’s intervention feels at once familiar and deeply frustrating. Familiar, because the critique of U.S. freedom as imperial, racialized, and exclusionary is the air we have breathed for generations. Frustrating, because even as Rana exposes the deep roots of American freedom in settler colonialism and global dominance, his proposed solution remains stubbornly inward-looking, aimed at American renewal rather than at the profound transformations that the global majority requires. The essay performs a critical analysis of empire, yet its political horizon is still essentially a domestic reform program for the empire’s core. If freedom is to be genuinely reclaimed, it must be on terms set not by a reformist American left but by the peoples and movements of the Global South who have endured the sharp end of that empire and who have, in many cases, developed far richer emancipatory traditions.

Rana’s historical reconstruction rightly notes that the material basis of white American freedom was built on Indigenous dispossession and enslaved labor. He also observes that twentieth-century American primacy continued to extract “rents” from the global arena, subsidizing a particular middle-class experience. However, he then draws the lesson that because fewer Americans now benefit from empire, a space has opened for a solidaristic left politics at home. This framing effectively treats the rest of the world as a backdrop—a source of rents that financed American freedom, but now a declining factor in domestic calculations. It misses the reality that the empire is still very much active in immiserating billions, and that any progressive freedom project must center not American renewal but a direct and immediate undoing of the structures of global domination. The “we” in Rana’s essay is consistently “we Americans,” and the stakes are framed as American cultural narratives and American democratic deficits. The phrase “genuine global well-being and equality” appears toward the end as a kind of afterthought, a side benefit of reallocating the U.S. defense budget.

But the U.S. defense budget is not just a misallocation of domestic wealth; it is an instrument of ongoing violence that daily forecloses the freedom of others. The trillions poured into military power have sustained client regimes from Egypt to Honduras, enforced sanctions that amount to collective punishment, and provided the hardware for atrocities. Rana’s call to redirect those funds is welcome, but it does not address the fact that the international economic architecture—the IMF, the World Bank, the dollar system—continues to operate as a mechanism of extraction, regardless of who sits in the White House. The New International Economic Order that he invokes in passing was not a request for nicer American policy; it was a demand for a thorough restructuring of the global economy that the United States used every tool, including covert wars, to crush. Reclaiming freedom for the Global South means resurrecting that demand, not simply hoping that a reformed America will be more amenable.

Furthermore, Rana’s emphasis on institution-building in the American workplace and political system, while laudable in itself, reveals a kind of provincialism. The most vibrant experiments in democratic self-rule today are not happening in the United States; they are happening in the Zapatista caracoles in Chiapas, in the Kurdish cantons of Rojava, in the municipalist movements across Spain, in the landless workers’ cooperatives of Brazil. These are not “theoretical and abstract” invocations of freedom; they are lived practices that have emerged precisely in resistance to the neoliberal order enforced by American power. They offer models of a freedom that is deeply anti-imperial, intersectional, and ecologically rooted. An emancipatory politics that takes them seriously would ask the American left to stop looking inward and start seeking solidarity on terms of humility and listening, recognizing that its primary task is not to fix its own narrative but to impede its own government’s capacity to strangle alternatives elsewhere.

This is not an argument for ignoring the struggles inside the United States. The labor revival, the Movement for Black Lives, and the immigrant rights movement are all vital, and they have profound global implications. But the logic of Rana’s own analysis should lead to the conclusion that no domestic reform can be secure or fully realized without a break from the imperial system that has always structured American freedom. That break cannot be led by U.S. progressives who frame it as a way to “make solidaristic freedom as culturally resonant” at home. It must be driven by international solidarity that accepts the leadership of those on the receiving end of empire. This means demanding the closure of U.S. military bases from Okinawa to Djibouti, supporting the right of postcolonial nations to default on odious debts, and treating migrants not as a domestic constituency to be incorporated into a “shared class solidarity” but as bearers of a just demand for mobility that the global economic system has made both necessary and criminalized. The goal is not to decriminalize entry so that America can be a better version of itself; it is to contest the very system that divides the world into zones of citizenship and zones of disposability.

Rana closes by acknowledging that any domestic vision of freedom rests on global conditions. But his translation of this insight into a set of national policy reforms—reallocating security spending, a more welcoming immigration regime—still treats the “global” as a condition to be managed for domestic ends. A truly anti-imperial freedom project would invert this: it would subordinate domestic policy to the demands of global justice. It would ask not what the U.S. can do to improve its own freedom, but what the U.S. must stop doing to allow others to be free. It would recognize that the word “freedom” has often been a weapon of the powerful in the Global North, and that the task is not to reclaim it for a new set of domestic powerholders, but to let it be redefined by those who have consistently made it meaningful through struggle—the enslaved who freed themselves, the colonized who overthrew empires, the women who built collective care economies in the ruins of neoliberal adjustment.

From where I sit, in a country whose economic “independence” was shattered by structural adjustment and whose debt service payments outstrip health and education spending, freedom means the right to a sovereign development path, the cancellation of illegitimate debt, and the restoration of stolen land and resources. I do not need an American left to build institutions for me to experience solidaristic self-rule; I need it to dismantle the institutions that currently prevent it. Rana has provided a powerful diagnosis of the American disease. But the cure must be prescribed by the patients the empire has afflicted, not by the physicians within its walls.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rana’s framing of freedom is stubbornly inward-looking, treating the Global South as a backdrop for American renewal rather than centering the immediate undoing of imperial violence.
  2. 02The American left must move beyond domestic institution-building to actively dismantle the international economic and military architectures, such as the IMF and military bases, that foreclose the freedom of other nations.
  3. 03True emancipatory models and vibrant practices of self-rule are already being led by movements in the Global South, and the American left should look to these for leadership rather than seeking to fix its own national narrative.
  4. 04A genuine anti-imperial freedom project must subordinate U.S. domestic policy to the demands of global justice, prioritizing the restoration of sovereign development and the cancellation of illegitimate debt over American cultural resonance.
  5. 05The definition of freedom should be set by those who have endured the empire's oppression rather than by reformists operating within the empire's core.
