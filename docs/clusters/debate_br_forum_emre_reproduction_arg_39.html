"While the lead essay correctly identifies that all reproduction is technologically mediated, it fails to critique how assisted reproductive technologies (ART) reinforce ableist hierarchies by treating embryo selection and the elimination of disability as neutral, optimized goods."

The galvanizing premise of “All Reproduction Is Assisted”—that no birth is unmediated and that the “natural” is a mystification—offers a valuable corrective to complacent pro‑natalism. But there is an underside to the essay’s celebration of assisted reproductive technologies (ART) that goes largely unexamined: its persistent, if quiet, investment in a hierarchy of viable life. For all its narrative sensitivity, the essay reproduces the very logic of optimization that it elsewhere critiques, and it does so in ways that threaten to reinforce some of the most noxious ableist assumptions of our era. If we are going to have a feminism that treats assisted reproduction as the norm, we had better be clear about which lives the assistance is meant to produce—and which ones it selects against.

Notice how the stories of B, K, and N are structured around the language of attrition and screening. B’s twenty‑two retrieved eggs are fertilized into eleven embryos, only six of which make it through preimplantation genetic screening, with a final tally of three “free of any chromosomal abnormalities.” The phrasing is neutral, almost clinical, but it carries a heavy normative load: the abnormal embryos are not named as lost potential children, but as defective entities rightly winnowed out. The genetic screen is presented as an uncomplicated good, a way to guard against the sudden and singular loss of a miscarriage. B’s doctor lists the results, and B feels pride when she reports: “I have two girls and a boy.” There is no pause to ask whether a child with Down syndrome, with a chromosomal translocation, or with a condition like cystic fibrosis would have been worth gestating, nor whether the desire to avoid disability might be entangled with wider social failures—lack of support services, inadequate healthcare, pervasive stigma—that make raising a disabled child feel impossible. The technology is treated as a neutral filter, but it is in fact a cultural sieve, shaped by a medical system that has a long history of pathologizing and eliminating disabled people.

This is not an abstract concern. In Iceland and elsewhere, the widespread use of prenatal screening has led to a near‑total elimination of births of people with Down syndrome, a development that many disability rights activists view as a form of soft eugenics. The lead essay never mentions this debate, even as it describes, in another section, the artificial womb of the Royal Caroline Hospital that “brought back to life babies born dead” and, more horrifyingly, “babies legally aborted from their mothers.” That history is recounted with an appropriately critical tone, but the contemporary practices of embryo selection are allowed to slip past without equivalent scrutiny. The effect is to suggest that the problem with the Swedish wombs was their coercive potential, not the underlying value judgment about which lives are worth living. Yet that value judgment is exactly what congenital condition screenings operationalize today, millions of times a year, in fertility clinics around the world.

The essay’s core thesis—that all reproduction is assisted—could have been a fruitful entry point for challenging ableism. When we acknowledge that every pregnancy already involves technological mediation, we can begin to see that the line between “therapeutic” and “enhancement” technologies is a political construction. An incubator for a premature baby is uncontroversially lifesaving, but what about a cochlear implant for a Deaf child? What about growth hormone therapy for a child who is simply very short? The answers depend not on the technology but on the social meaning of disability in a given context. The essay gestures toward this kind of analysis when it critiques the natural/culture binary, but it stops short of applying the same deconstructive energy to the healthy/diseased binary. Firestone and Haraway, the essay’s intellectual heroes, both understood that the body is a site of political contest, not a given biological fact. Yet in the stories they inspire, healthy and normal are left standing as unmarked ideals.

I do not mean to dismiss the real suffering of infertility, miscarriage, or the stress of raising a child with high support needs. Those are real, and they deserve policy responses that fund care, not eugenic shortcuts. The tragedy is that the same society that makes genetic screening seem like a “responsible” choice is the one that has systematically refused to build a comprehensive infrastructure of care for disabled people and their families. In the United States, home‑ and community‑based services for people with disabilities remain chronically underfunded; paid family leave is a patchwork; and the medical model of disability often pushes parents toward a tragic narrative in which a diagnosis is a disaster. In that environment, selecting against disability can seem like an act of love. But it is an act shaped by a coercive background, and it contributes to a world in which the very existence of disabled bodies is treated as a mistake that technology ought to prevent.

A truly liberatory reproductive politics would refuse the optimization imperatives that the Silicon Valley fertility industry enthusiastically embraces. Rather than asking how many genetically normal embryos we can bank, it would ask how we can create the material and cultural conditions in which any child, regardless of ability, can be welcomed and supported. That means demanding universal, single‑payer healthcare with robust long‑term supports; housing and transportation that are truly accessible; inclusive education; and a cultural shift away from measuring human worth by productivity or IQ. It would mean listening to disabled adults when they say that their lives are not tragedies, and that the push for ever‑more precise screening often rests on ignorance and prejudice rather than on a genuine respect for human dignity.

The Boston Review essay does many things well. It gives voice to longing and loss, it demolishes the sanctimony of natural childbirth, and it exposes the structural inequalities that lurk in the reproductive clinic. But it fails to ask the one question that every assisted reproduction narrative should force us to confront: What kinds of people do we want to assist into being, and why? Until feminism integrates disability justice into its core commitments, our visions of reproductive freedom will remain profoundly impoverished.
