"While Merve Emre’s use of narrative effectively grounds the lived realities of reproductive technology, narrative alone cannot resolve the political debates it raises because the same stories can support conflicting ideological conclusions."

What Narrative Can and Cannot Do

Merve Emre opens her essay with a methodological claim: "To appreciate all this — and to figure out what to do about it — we need narrative." The four stories that follow — S, B, N and K, micha cárdenas — are the essay's argumentative substance, and they are exquisitely told. I want to spend a response taking that methodological claim seriously, which means asking what narrative can do in reproductive politics, and what it cannot.

What narrative can do, demonstrably, is render the texture of reproductive technology as it is actually lived. Until you have read or written something like Emre's account of egg freezing, you do not understand the daily rhythm of the procedure: the 9 p.m. injections, the every-other-day ultrasounds, the prohibition on sex and exercise, the trigger shot scheduled exactly seventy-two hours before retrieval, the fear of the 1–2 p.m. phone call. The clinical literature does not convey this; the policy literature does not convey this; the manifesto literature, in its hortatory pitch, does not convey this. Narrative does. And once you have the texture, certain arguments become available that were not available before. You can see, for example, that the choice to freeze eggs is not a one-time decision but a month-long bodily project; that the OHSS risk B nearly fell into is not a footnote but a serious adverse event; that the indignities N and K encountered from the resident who could not find a cervix are not exceptional but structural; that the "DIY" creativity of cárdenas's bioart was made necessary by the failures of the medical literature she was given.

These are real gains. The argumentative work narrative does here is the work of grounding — taking abstractions like "assisted reproduction" or "reproductive choice" and showing what they consist of, hour by hour, in particular bodies and particular clinical encounters. The feminist tradition that Emre is working in — from Adrienne Rich's Of Woman Born through Rayna Rapp's ethnographies of amniocentesis through more recent work by Sarah Franklin and Charis Thompson — has long understood that the grounding work is essential. Reproductive politics conducted in pure manifesto register, without ethnographic substance, tends to misfire. The manifesto knows what it wants; it does not know what the thing it wants is actually like.

Now the harder question: what can narrative not do? It cannot, by itself, generate the political conclusions Emre draws in her closing pages. The conclusions — that we should make assisted reproduction the "preferred strategy" of feminist struggle, that we should close the gap between political and technological, that we should move from a politics of "the natural" to a politics of acknowledged assistance — do not, in fact, follow from the four narratives. They float above them. The narratives are compatible with those conclusions, but they are also compatible with several other conclusions, including some quite different ones.

Consider what other arguments S, B, N and K, and cárdenas could each support. S could equally well anchor a critique of corporate egg freezing as a privatization of social reproduction, in which a tech firm extracts ovarian labor in lieu of providing real parental support. B could equally well anchor a defense of "natural" pacing — her hyperfertile family, her healthy follicle count at forty, the obstetric establishment's insistence that she should accept IVF rather than continue with IUI — and a critique of the genetic-screening cascade that ended with her ranking three "viable" embryos by sex. N and K could anchor a defense of community-based, non-medical knowledge networks — the queer TTC Facebook group, the lesbian-specific subgroup — as the actual technology that made their parenthood possible, with the clinic as a frequently hostile environment they had to endure rather than as the site of liberation. cárdenas could anchor a critique of the entire fertility industry, since the central fact of her story is that she had to build her own knowledge with a fifty-dollar microscope because the medical establishment had lied to her and the Facebook network of trans women was the only source of accurate information she could find.

Each of these alternative readings is consistent with the narrative material. Emre selects one reading and frames the others, when she frames them at all, as partial views to be integrated into hers. This is not a critique of her method but a recognition that narrative under-determines its uses. The same story can be told in several political registers. The selection of register is an argumentative choice, and it should be defended argumentatively, not just narratively.

The choice Emre makes is, broadly, the assimilationist one: the technologies are here, the women are using them, the appropriate political program is to universalize access and dethrone the residual ideology of the natural. This is not an unreasonable choice. But it is a choice. The alternatives — defending the natural as anti-medicalization critique; defending community knowledge against clinic capture; suspecting the technology as a privatization of social reproduction; questioning the trajectory of selective reproduction — are also live, and they are also supportable from the same material.

I want to flag one specific place where I think the narrative work and the argumentative work part company. The essay's final paragraphs invoke Dorothy Roberts's reproductive justice framework and use it to call for "a non-discriminatory system of universal healthcare, paid parental leave, and protections for LGBT people and people with disabilities." Good. But the essay then concludes that "acknowledging the positive right to reproduce" could "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight." This is a non-sequitur. The positive right to reproduce, in Roberts's framework, is principally a right of women of color and poor women to have and raise children — children who are being taken from them by the foster system, killed by police, deported, denied healthcare, and shaped by environmental racism. Assisted reproduction, in the technological sense, is not the central instrument for vindicating that right. The central instruments are: childcare, housing, healthcare, immigration protection, police reform, environmental justice. Emre's conclusion fuses two different things — the technological "assisted" of IVF and gamete preservation, and the social "assisted" of community and state support — under one word and treats the political weight of the second as if it transferred to the first. It does not.

What I am suggesting is that the essay is two essays. One essay is a beautiful piece of reproductive ethnography that does what narrative does best: grounds abstractions in bodies and clinics. That essay is a real contribution. The other essay is a manifesto-in-disguise that draws conclusions narrative cannot fully support and that, on examination, smuggle in commitments the narrative material would not unanimously endorse. The two essays are intertwined, and the second is supported by the first in ways that warrant pulling them apart.

This is not a rejection of Emre's project. It is a reminder that the form of "narrative" she rightly champions does not, by itself, resolve the political questions it raises. To resolve them, we still need argument, and the argument is still being conducted — among feminists who agree that the natural is contested, the technology is here, and the politics of reproduction is unfinished, but who do not agree about which way the unfinishedness should be finished. Emre has made one contribution to that conversation. The conversation continues.
