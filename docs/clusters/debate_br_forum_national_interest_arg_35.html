"While Stephen Walt’s 'offshore balancing' framework is useful, it fails to provide a viable path forward because it underestimates the inherent dysfunction of international institutions and ignores the powerful domestic political constraints that make his specific policy recommendations unrealistic."

Stephen Walt's analysis, while insightful on many points, fails to grapple with the fundamental challenge facing American foreign policy: the limitations of both multilateralism and unilateralism as frameworks for action. What the United States needs is not a choice between these extremes but a sophisticated approach that combines multilateral legitimacy-seeking with decisive unilateral capacity.

Walt correctly identifies the failure of Bush administration unilateralism in Iraq, but his proposed remedy—greater reliance on international institutions—understates the pathologies of those institutions. The United Nations proved incapable of disarming Iraq over twelve years; the Security Council was paralyzed by fundamental disagreements among great powers; and the nuclear nonproliferation regime has manifestly failed to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons. International institutions reflect the interests and biases of their member states, and those interests often diverge from American interests. Walt's suggestion that the United States should seek Security Council authorization for military action ignores the political realities of that body.

This does not mean multilateralism is useless. The international system does provide mechanisms for legitimizing American action, and American policymakers should exploit those mechanisms when possible. But legitimacy-seeking must not become legitimacy-dependence. The United States must retain the capacity to act without international authorization when vital interests demand it, while simultaneously working to build international support for American positions.

The more serious problem with Walt's analysis is its neglect of domestic politics. American foreign policy is not made by dispassionate strategists weighing national interests; it is made by politicians responding to domestic constituencies, interest groups, and electoral incentives. Walt's recommendations—pressure on Israel, nuclear reductions, rejection of preventive war—face fierce opposition from powerful domestic constituencies. The Israel lobby is not the only organized interest shaping foreign policy, but it is among the most effective. Walt's proposal to condition aid on Israeli policy changes is not merely politically unrealistic; it is politically impossible in the current environment.

Similarly, Walt's nuclear proposals would face resistance from the defense establishment, which has strong interests in maintaining robust nuclear capabilities. The Air Force and Navy depend on nuclear forces for mission relevance, and arms control advocates lack the organized constituency that hawks possess. Walt may be right that smaller nuclear arsenals would enhance American credibility, but he offers no realistic pathway for achieving that goal.

The essay's most valuable contribution is its emphasis on long-term strategic thinking. American policymakers have a tendency to focus on immediate crises and neglect the cumulative effect of their actions on American standing and influence. The Iraq war was not merely a tactical error; it reflected a strategic culture that values military action over diplomatic engagement and treats American power as unlimited. Rebuilding that culture will require not only policy changes but also institutional reforms—the creation of mechanisms for independent strategic analysis, enhanced diplomatic capacity, and greater continuity across administrations.

Walt's offshore balancing offers a useful framework for thinking about American strategy, but it needs to be embedded in a more realistic understanding of both international institutions and domestic politics. The United States cannot simply choose a grand strategy and implement it; it must work within the constraints that both the international system and American democracy impose.
