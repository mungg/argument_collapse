"To defeat authoritarianism, the Democratic Party must move beyond traditional left-right policy debates and adopt an internationalist strategy that focuses on changing the axis of conflict toward a broad anti-corruption and pro-democracy front."

Bonica and Grumbach are right to look beyond the United States. American liberals too often treat Trumpism as an indigenous aberration, a product of one man’s personality or of peculiar constitutional defects. But democratic backsliding is international. Authoritarian movements have learned from one another: attack courts, delegitimize elections, capture media, politicize bureaucracy, demonize minorities, reward loyal oligarchs, and describe all accountability as persecution. The opposition must learn internationally as well.

The lesson from abroad is not that moderation never works. It is that defeating authoritarianism usually requires changing the axis of conflict. When opposition parties fight authoritarians on ordinary left-right terms, they often lose because the authoritarian has already redefined politics around loyalty, nation, resentment, and fear. A tax plan does not answer a myth of national betrayal. A careful policy paper does not defeat a leader who promises revenge. Successful opposition movements find a broader frame: democracy versus corruption, Europe versus isolation, law versus impunity, dignity versus humiliation, the people versus the clique.

In Brazil, the anti-Bolsonaro coalition depended not only on left mobilization but also on broad democratic alignment, institutional resistance, and a candidate with deep working-class credibility. In Poland, opposition to Law and Justice required cooperation among ideologically diverse parties and a defense of European democratic identity against nationalist capture. In Turkey, Hungary, India, Israel, and other cases, opposition forces have struggled when fragmented, when overly elite, or when unable to penetrate the governing party’s media ecosystem. These examples differ enormously, but they share a warning: authoritarian politics cannot be defeated by assuming normal alternation of power will continue.

The United States is not Hungary, Turkey, or India. Its federal system, civil society, courts, media pluralism, and party structure differ. But the authoritarian playbook is recognizable. The Republican Party’s transformation under Trump has included election denial, intimidation of officials, contempt for independent law enforcement when it investigates allies, threats against political opponents, attacks on the press, and an increasingly explicit demand that state institutions serve the leader’s movement. In this context, “moderation” can become a refusal to understand the regime conflict.

The lead essay’s anti-corruption proposal fits the international pattern. Corruption is often the authoritarian’s weakest flank because strongmen promise to purify the nation while surrounding themselves with cronies. They attack “elites” while enriching loyal elites. They rail against globalists while courting oligarchs. They claim to represent the people while weakening the institutions through which people can hold power accountable. Anti-corruption politics exposes the contradiction.

But anti-corruption succeeds only when it becomes organizational, not merely rhetorical. Around the world, protest movements can fill streets and still fail if they lack parties, local structures, legal strategies, labor alliances, and credible governing alternatives. Outrage opens a door; organization walks through it. Democrats have a tendency to mistake national messaging for organization. They raise money, produce ads, hire consultants, and hope national mood shifts. Anti-authoritarian politics requires more durable infrastructure: civic groups, unions, professional associations, local parties, election monitors, independent media, litigation networks, and cross-partisan democracy coalitions.

International experience also cautions against purity. In moments of democratic crisis, opposition forces often need popular-front politics. That means alliances with people who are wrong about many issues but right about the necessity of preserving competitive elections and the rule of law. Bonica and Grumbach criticize Harris’s courtship of Liz Cheney as part of a failed moderation strategy. They are right that celebrity endorsements from anti-Trump Republicans cannot substitute for mobilizing disaffected voters. But the broader instinct—showing that the defense of democracy crosses ideological lines—is not foolish. The problem was not that Cheney was included. It was that the campaign sometimes seemed more eager to reassure elite Republicans than to empower ordinary citizens.

A successful anti-authoritarian coalition must operate on two levels at once. At the elite level, it should split the authoritarian bloc where possible: former officials, military leaders, business figures, religious leaders, conservative lawyers, and local Republicans who refuse election subversion should be welcomed into a constitutional front. At the mass level, it must offer material and moral reasons for nonelite voters to join: lower costs, accountable government, labor power, clean courts, safe communities, and freedom from billionaire rule. Elite permission without mass mobilization looks like restoration. Mass mobilization without elite coordination may lack institutional leverage. Both are needed.

Another lesson from abroad concerns media. Authoritarian movements thrive in information environments where their supporters inhabit a separate reality. Hungary’s ruling party benefited from media capture; India’s BJP from a vast sympathetic media and digital ecosystem; Bolsonaro from social media networks; Trump from a hybrid of partisan media, influencers, and algorithmic amplification. Opposition parties often respond by trying to fact-check their way out. Facts matter, but they rarely travel far enough without trusted messengers. Democrats must build channels, not just messages: local media, Spanish-language and Black media, labor communication, religious networks, veterans’ networks, podcasts, streamers, and community institutions.

Bonica and Grumbach’s critique of popularism is apt here. Poll-tested messages are weak if they have no trusted carrier. A voter may agree in principle that congressional stock trading is wrong, but the issue becomes politically meaningful only when embedded in a story told by someone they trust. International movements that succeed often create symbols and rituals of solidarity: marches, songs, colors, local assemblies, strike actions, legal defense campaigns. American Democrats have often outsourced emotion to presidential campaigns and then wondered why the right dominates belonging.

Still, one international lesson complicates the lead essay’s anti-establishment emphasis. In many countries, attacks on “the establishment” have been the authoritarians’ own weapon. They use anti-corruption language to purge independent institutions and replace them with loyalists. Therefore democratic anti-corruption must be institution-building, not institution-smashing. It should distinguish between captured institutions that need reform and independent institutions that need defense. It should attack impunity, not expertise; oligarchy, not public service; judicial corruption, not the idea of law.

The United States also needs a federal strategy. Democratic backsliding often advances subnationally before it becomes national: through state election laws, gerrymanders, control of prosecutors, school boards, police powers, and local media deserts. Bonica and Grumbach note nationalization, but international comparisons remind us that authoritarians consolidate through every level of government. Democrats and democracy advocates must contest state legislatures, secretary of state races, county commissions, sheriffs, and judgeships. The defense of democracy cannot appear only in presidential years.

What should Democrats learn, then? First, stop treating authoritarianism as a messaging challenge and treat it as a regime challenge. Second, build the broadest possible constitutional coalition without allowing elite reassurance to replace popular mobilization. Third, use anti-corruption as a frame that connects democracy to daily life. Fourth, invest in organizations that can survive defeat and govern after victory. Fifth, defend institutions by reforming them, so that “democracy” means equal power rather than nostalgia for a failing status quo.

The lead essay’s title is well chosen. One way not to defeat authoritarianism is to assume that normal politics can defeat abnormal politics. But another way not to defeat authoritarianism is to imitate the authoritarian’s contempt for institutions, expertise, and pluralism. The democratic opposition must be more determined, as Bonica and Grumbach say, but also more disciplined and more capacious.

History abroad offers no formula. It offers patterns. Authoritarians are strongest when opposition is fragmented, demoralized, overly elite, and trapped in old issue dimensions. They are weakest when citizens see them as corrupt predators, when institutions retain defenders, when opposition groups coordinate across difference, and when democratic politics promises not mere restoration but renewal. That is the path Democrats should study—not because America is just like other backsliding democracies, but because it is no longer exempt from their lessons.
