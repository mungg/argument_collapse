"The debate over Democratic strategy should shift from a narrow focus on electoral mechanics and winning to a deeper consideration of what the party substantively stands for and its core values."

The lead essay represents a sophisticated engagement with recent political science research. I want to offer a different kind of critique: one that questions the framework within which the debate is conducted.

Bonica and Grumbach frame the question as: how can Democrats win elections? This is a reasonable question given that winning elections is necessary for governance. But it is not the only reasonable question.

Alternative framings include: What should Democrats stand for? What policies would actually improve the lives of Americans? What vision of society should the party promote? These questions are prior to the question of winning, or at least distinct from it.

The authors implicitly assume that the goal of political strategy should be electoral victory. This assumption is so common that it often goes unexamined. But politics is not only about winning; it is about what you do when you win and what you stand for whether or not you win.

A party that wins elections by abandoning its values is not obviously better than a party that loses while maintaining its commitments. The Clinton Democratic approach—pragmatic, triangulating, focused on winning—produced electoral success but also produced a party that many voters saw as out of touch with their concerns.

The authors are right that the moderation strategy has failed. But they may not fully appreciate why: the party failed to offer a vision that inspired loyalty. Moderation as a strategy failed because it was paired with a substantive emptiness.

The answer to this failure is not a new messaging strategy. It is a renewed commitment to substantive politics: policies that would actually change the lives of working Americans, a vision of society that offers hope rather than mere competence, and a willingness to fight for things that powerful interests oppose.

The authors offer useful analysis of electoral mechanics. But they do not engage with the deeper question of what Democrats should stand for. That question cannot be answered by political science alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The assumption that electoral victory is the sole or primary goal of political strategy ignores the fundamental importance of a party's vision and policy commitments.
  2. 02Winning elections by abandoning core values can lead to a sense of substantive emptiness that ultimately alienates voters.
  3. 03The failure of the moderation strategy is rooted in its inability to offer an inspiring vision rather than just a failure of messaging or polling.
  4. 04A successful political movement requires a commitment to policies that tangibly improve lives and a willingness to challenge powerful interests, regardless of immediate electoral calculations.
  5. 05Political science research into electoral mechanics cannot answer the prior and more essential question of what a political party's moral and social purpose should be.
