"The Democratic Party's turnout crisis is not a technical messaging failure but a result of political estrangement caused by a strategy that treats marginalized voters as disposable tools for mobilization rather than citizens whose specific grievances deserve moral clarity and policy action."

Bonica and Grumbach are right to criticize the ritual call for Democratic moderation, but their essay risks treating turnout as a technical problem rather than a problem of political respect. The decline in Black turnout, youth turnout, and participation among irregular Democratic-leaning voters in 2024 cannot be explained simply by insufficiently exciting messaging. It reflected a deeper estrangement. Many voters who are asked every two or four years to “save democracy” have good reason to wonder whether democracy, as practiced by either party, has saved them.

This is especially true for Black voters. For decades, Black Americans have been the most loyal constituency in the Democratic coalition and among the most consistent defenders of democratic rights. Yet the party’s strategic conversation often treats them as a base to be mobilized, not as citizens to be followed. After losses, elite debate tends to focus on white working-class voters, suburban moderates, or the latest movement among Latino men. Black turnout is discussed as a number that failed to reach its benchmark. Less often do Democrats ask what obligations flow from relying so heavily on Black political labor.

The language of moderation has often been used to discipline racial justice demands. When activists protested police violence, many Democratic strategists quickly concluded that the party needed distance from “defund the police.” Some of that concern was understandable; the slogan was unpopular and easily weaponized. But the subsequent retreat frequently went beyond rejecting a slogan. It became an excuse to avoid confronting police abuse, mass incarceration, surveillance, municipal fines and fees, racialized poverty, and the everyday experience of state power in Black communities. Voters notice when their pain becomes a messaging problem.

The same pattern appears on immigration. Democrats oscillate between humane rhetoric and enforcement theater, depending on the perceived mood of swing voters. Immigrant communities are told they are valued, then watched as leaders compete to sound tough at the border. Again, there are real governance challenges. A democratic state must manage migration, process asylum claims, and maintain public confidence. But treating immigrants primarily as a vulnerability in campaign ads corrodes trust. Authoritarianism thrives when groups can be marked as disposable.

The lead essay’s strongest argument is that Democrats need moral clarity. But moral clarity cannot be selective. If anti-authoritarian politics means only that Trump and his allies threaten elections, courts, and civil service protections, it will be too narrow. Authoritarianism is also family separation, political violence against protesters, threats against journalists, Muslim bans, attacks on birthright citizenship, voter suppression, criminalization of poverty, and the use of state power to designate some people as less fully American. A democracy movement that soft-pedals these realities in search of moderation may win a few approving editorials, but it will lose the people who know most intimately what authoritarian power feels like.

This does not mean every activist demand is wise or every slogan should be defended. Movements make mistakes. Language can become insular. Organizations can confuse online approval with mass politics. But the solution is not to move “to the center,” as if the center were a neutral place. On many issues, the center has been shaped by decades of fearmongering. The “center” on crime has often meant tolerating brutality until a video makes it impossible to ignore. The “center” on immigration has often meant accepting cruelty as proof of seriousness. The “center” on welfare has often meant suspicion toward poor mothers. Democrats should not accept those baselines as morally innocent.

Bonica and Grumbach propose anti-corruption as a way to reframe politics. That frame has promise for racial justice, but only if corruption is understood broadly. It is corrupt when wealthy donors can shape courts. It is also corrupt when majority-Black cities are denied self-government by state officials. It is corrupt when polling places are closed in minority neighborhoods, when felony disenfranchisement dilutes Black political power, when private probation companies profit from the poor, when police unions block accountability, when disaster aid follows political influence rather than need. Anti-corruption should expose the racialized distribution of power, not cover it over.

The authors also call for recruiting working-class candidates—nurses, teachers, veterans. Good. But Democrats should be careful not to use “working class” as code for white, male, rural, or culturally conservative. The working class is disproportionately female, multiracial, service-sector, immigrant, and urban or suburban. A home health aide is working class. A childcare worker is working class. A warehouse worker with an ankle monitor is working class. A public school paraprofessional is working class. If the party wants authenticity, it must broaden its imagination of who counts as an authentic American.

The turnout crisis among young voters also deserves a more serious diagnosis. Many young people did not sit out because no one found the right TikTok message. They are facing unaffordable housing, precarious work, debt, climate fear, loneliness, and distrust of institutions that seem incapable of acting at scale. Some were alienated by U.S. policy in Gaza and by the sense that moral outrage abroad was treated as a nuisance by party leaders. Others saw Democrats defend democracy while appearing unable or unwilling to deliver consequences for elite lawbreaking. Still others simply did not believe either party was speaking to their future. Moderation does not answer that despair. But neither does scolding.

A democracy strategy must therefore be participatory, not merely mobilizational. Voters should not be treated as reservoirs of turnout to be tapped. They need institutions through which they can deliberate, organize, and influence the agenda. This means investing in local party structures, youth organizations, HBCU organizing, unions, community colleges, immigrant associations, tenant unions, and civic groups that build leadership outside campaign season. It means sharing power within the party, not only asking for votes after decisions have been made.

At the same time, Democrats must resist the temptation to frame every internal disagreement as betrayal. A multiracial coalition will contain genuine differences on policing, schools, religion, gender, foreign policy, and economic priorities. Black voters, Latino voters, Asian American voters, Native voters, and young voters are not ideological monoliths. Respect means persuasion and negotiation, not assuming that the loudest nonprofit or influencer speaks for everyone. But negotiation must begin from the premise that marginalized communities are not expendable in the pursuit of an imagined median voter.

The lead essay usefully rejects the false choice between moderate and progressive. I would restate the real choice differently: Democrats can either build a democracy movement rooted in the experiences of those most vulnerable to domination, or they can build a campaign strategy that remembers those voters mainly when turnout drops. The former may actually persuade more people because it speaks with conviction. The latter will continue to generate cynicism.

Authoritarianism is defeated not only by defending institutions but by democratizing them. That means voting rights, fair maps, court reform, ethics rules, and limits on executive abuse. It also means accountable policing, immigrant due process, labor power, reproductive freedom, environmental justice, and public investment in neglected communities. These are not special interests. They are democracy in lived form.

If Democrats want people to believe democracy is on the ballot, they must show that democracy is also on the block, in the workplace, at the border, in the courtroom, in the school, and in the hospital. They should stop asking which rights must be muted to reassure swing voters and start asking how to make the fight for equal citizenship feel like a fight everyone has a stake in. Moderation has too often meant asking the most loyal voters to wait. A democracy worth saving cannot be built on waiting.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rhetoric of moderation is frequently used to discipline and dismiss racial justice demands, signaling to Black voters that their lived experiences of state abuse are merely messaging problems to be avoided.
  2. 02Oscillating between humane rhetoric and enforcement theater on immigration corrodes trust and validates authoritarian frameworks that treat certain groups as disposable.
  3. 03A truly effective anti-corruption frame must be expanded to include the racialized distribution of power, such as voter suppression and the denial of self-government to minority-majority cities.
  4. 04The party's definition of 'working class' must be broadened beyond white, culturally conservative archetypes to authentically represent the multiracial, service-sector reality of the modern labor force.
  5. 05Youth voter apathy is a rational response to institutional failure on issues like housing, climate, and foreign policy, which cannot be solved by moderation or scolding.
  6. 06To defeat authoritarianism, the party must move from a top-down mobilizational strategy to a participatory one that shares power with local civic institutions and marginalized communities.
