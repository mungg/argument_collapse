"The influence of drug companies on medical treatments can be reduced by implementing structural reforms that separate commercial marketing from medical education and diagnostic standards."

Yes, drug companies’ influence can be reduced, but only if we stop pretending that marketing is just another form of medical education. The problem is not that A.D.H.D. is imaginary. It is not. Many children and adults are impaired by inattention, impulsivity and disorganization, and stimulant medications can be remarkably helpful. The problem is that the companies that profit from expanding the market have been allowed to help define the market.

That influence operates at several points. Companies fund studies, pay prominent physicians to speak, underwrite educational materials, send sales representatives into offices, advertise directly to consumers and sometimes support patient advocacy groups. Each activity can be defended in isolation. Together they create an environment in which more people see ordinary struggles as symptoms, more doctors reach first for a prescription pad and more families hear about benefits before they hear about uncertainty, side effects or alternatives.

The rapid rise in A.D.H.D. diagnosis should make us cautious. Recent federal survey data suggest that roughly 11 percent of school-age children have received an A.D.H.D. diagnosis, with especially high rates among boys. That does not prove that every added diagnosis is wrong. But it does show that the boundary between illness and normal variation has moved quickly. When the beneficiaries of that movement are also financing much of the conversation, skepticism is warranted.

The most direct reform would be to sharply limit direct-to-consumer advertising of prescription drugs, especially controlled substances used by children. The United States is one of the few countries that permits such advertising, and the public has not benefited from turning diagnoses into brand identities. At minimum, ads should be required to present absolute benefits, common harms and non-drug alternatives in plain language.

Second, continuing medical education should be separated from industry money. Physicians need education, not salesmanship with slides. If that means higher licensing fees or public funding for independent education, it would be money well spent. Third, guideline panels should be largely free of financial conflicts. Experts who have taken significant money from manufacturers may still have knowledge, but they should not be writing the rules that expand prescribing.

Finally, transparency is necessary but not sufficient. The Sunshine Act’s payment disclosures are a start, but patients should not need to investigate their doctors to know whether advice is independent. Medical journals should require access to trial data, not just company-written summaries. Insurers should pay for careful diagnosis, behavioral therapy and parent training, not only for pills.

Drug companies have a legitimate role in making and testing medicines. They do not have a legitimate role in defining the limits of normal childhood or turning every classroom difficulty into a lifelong pharmaceutical customer. We cannot remove commerce from medicine entirely, but we can build walls high enough that medical judgment is not just marketing by another name.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Direct-to-consumer advertising of prescription drugs should be sharply limited or strictly regulated to prevent the transformation of medical diagnoses into brand identities.
  2. 02Continuing medical education must be funded independently of industry money to ensure physicians receive objective information rather than sales pitches.
  3. 03Clinical guideline panels should be composed of experts free from financial conflicts of interest to prevent the expansion of prescribing rules for profit.
  4. 04Medical journals and insurers should prioritize transparency in trial data and provide better coverage for non-pharmaceutical interventions like behavioral therapy.
  5. 05The current environment created by industry funding leads to the over-medicalization of normal human struggles and a lack of focus on drug side effects and alternatives.
