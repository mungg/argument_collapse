"The presidential primary schedule should be restructured so that each party's earliest contests reflect the specific demographics and core coalitions of their respective electorates."

If the question is which states should vote first, my answer is: the states that best test each party’s actual coalition. The Democratic and Republican parties do not have identical electorates, and their calendars need not be justified in exactly the same way.

For Democrats, beginning in Iowa and New Hampshire is especially hard to defend. African-American voters are central to the Democratic coalition, as are Latinos, union households, younger urban voters and socially liberal professionals. Yet the first two contests give disproportionate influence to white rural caucus-goers and New Hampshire independents. South Carolina and Nevada are much better early tests for Democrats. A Democratic candidate who cannot appeal to black voters in South Carolina or Latino and labor voters in Nevada has not really demonstrated national strength.

For Republicans, Iowa and New Hampshire make somewhat more sense, but still only partially. Iowa tests evangelical organization and conservative activism. New Hampshire tests fiscal conservatism, independents and a more libertarian Republican strain. But the Republican Party is also Southern, suburban, military-connected and increasingly shaped by debates over immigration and trade. South Carolina belongs early for Republicans too, as do states such as Arizona, Georgia or North Carolina in rotating cycles.

The parties should stop pretending that the same inherited schedule serves both equally. They should design early windows that reflect their voters and their governing challenges. Democrats might begin with Nevada, South Carolina, New Hampshire and a Midwestern state with labor strength. Republicans might begin with Iowa, New Hampshire, South Carolina and a growing Sun Belt state. Both parties should include at least one state with a significant nonwhite electorate before the press declares winners and losers.

This would not be undemocratic. Parties are not neutral election agencies; they are associations choosing nominees. Their rules should encourage nominees who can unite their own voters and compete nationally. What is undemocratic is allowing two states to retain power by custom long after the country and the parties have changed.

The first contests should not be museum pieces. They should be diagnostic tests. Different parties may need different tests, but both should be broader than Iowa and New Hampshire alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Democratic primary calendar should prioritize states like South Carolina and Nevada to ensure candidates can appeal to essential Black, Latino, and labor voters.
  2. 02The Republican primary calendar should include Southern and Sun Belt states to better represent the party's military-connected, suburban, and immigration-focused constituencies.
  3. 03Both parties must include states with significant nonwhite populations early in the process to prevent the media from declaring winners based on unrepresentative data.
  4. 04Parties are associations that should design their rules to produce nominees capable of uniting their specific voters and competing on a national scale.
  5. 05Maintaining the current dominance of Iowa and New Hampshire is undemocratic because it relies on outdated customs that no longer reflect the modern composition of the country or the parties.

"The primary calendar should be customized by each political party to reflect their specific voter coalitions rather than forcing both parties to follow the same sequence of states."

There is no single answer because the parties are not trying to represent the same electorates. A Democratic primary and a Republican primary are different institutions with different coalitions. The early calendar should acknowledge that instead of pretending one set of states can fairly test both parties.

For Democrats, Iowa and New Hampshire are plainly inadequate as the opening act. The Democratic coalition depends heavily on black voters, Latinos, Asian-Americans, union households, urban professionals and younger voters. A calendar that begins in two overwhelmingly white states gives disproportionate influence to a slice of the party that is not its backbone. Democrats should begin with Nevada and South Carolina, then move quickly to a larger industrial or Midwestern state such as Michigan or Wisconsin. That sequence would test candidates with immigrants, service workers, African-American voters, organized labor, college towns and rural whites.

For Republicans, Iowa and New Hampshire are more defensible, though still incomplete. The Republican electorate is whiter and more rural than the country as a whole, and Iowa’s evangelical conservatives and New Hampshire’s anti-tax independents are real components of the party. But the GOP is also a Sun Belt party, a suburban party, a military party and a party with important constituencies in the South and West. South Carolina has earned its early place. Florida or Arizona could also test whether Republican candidates can speak to seniors, Latinos, veterans and fast-growing metropolitan areas.

Let each party design an early calendar that reflects its voters and its governing aspirations. That would be more honest than using “America” as an abstraction while allowing two small states to dominate both contests. Parties are private associations performing a public function; they owe fairness not only to tradition, but to the people who actually make up their ranks.

The first states should not be chosen by nostalgia. They should be chosen by coalition.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Iowa and New Hampshire are unrepresentative of the Democratic coalition, which relies on a diverse mix of minority, urban, and union voters.
  2. 02Democrats should prioritize states like Nevada, South Carolina, and Michigan to better test candidates against the party's actual demographic backbone.
  3. 03While Iowa and New Hampshire align better with Republican demographics, the GOP calendar still needs to incorporate Sun Belt and suburban states to represent its full constituency.
  4. 04Selecting early states based on party coalition rather than tradition ensures that the primary process is fair to the people who actually make up each party's ranks.
