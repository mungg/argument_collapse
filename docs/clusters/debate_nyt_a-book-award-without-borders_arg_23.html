"The decision to expand the Man Booker Prize to all English-language writers is unwise because it replaces a historically significant arena for post-colonial dialogue with a system governed by market logic and commercial dominance."

The phrase “without borders” is too easy. The old Booker border was not innocent: it was drawn along lines left by empire. Britain, the Commonwealth, Ireland and Zimbabwe were not a natural literary family. They were a historical one. Yet that history was precisely why the prize mattered. It created an uneasy arena in which writers from formerly colonized societies could remake English in public and be read, uncomfortably, at the old imperial center.

That is no small thing. One of the Booker’s great achievements has been to make British readers encounter English as a language with many owners. “Midnight’s Children,” “The Famished Road,” “The God of Small Things,” “Disgrace” and “The White Tiger” are not merely successful novels that happened to qualify under an old rule. They are part of an argument about history, power, class, migration and the afterlife of empire. The prize’s odd eligibility kept that argument visible.

Opening the prize to all English-language writers may look like liberation from colonial categories. But it may also replace history with market logic. In practice, the new “borderless” field will still have gatekeepers: British publication, literary agents, review attention, submission rules and the budgets of large publishing houses. A novel from a major American imprint and a novel from a small African or Caribbean press do not enter the race equally. To pretend otherwise is to confuse formal eligibility with genuine access.

If the Booker wished to escape its imperial inheritance, it had other choices. It could have strengthened pathways for writers from the global South. It could have given more power to small presses. It could have created a prize that brought translated fiction into equal competition with English-language work. Instead it has moved from the map of empire to the map of Anglophone commerce.

Still, the old rule did deserve questioning. No one should be sentimental about Commonwealth labels. But abolishing a compromised boundary is not the same as creating a just literary space. What may be lost is the Booker’s historical memory: its role as a place where English was contested, not merely celebrated. A better reform would have admitted the arbitrariness of the old system while preserving the prize’s responsibility to the literatures it helped bring to wider notice.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The prize's original eligibility criteria created a vital space where writers from formerly colonized societies could challenge the imperial center and reclaim the English language.
  2. 02The old rules kept important arguments regarding history, power, and the afterlife of empire visible to the British public.
  3. 03Expanding the prize to all English-language writers ignores the reality that American imprints and large publishing houses possess unfair market advantages over smaller presses from the global South.
  4. 04The expansion confuses formal eligibility with genuine access, failing to address the systemic barriers that prevent diverse voices from competing on equal footing.
  5. 05Instead of moving toward a purely commercial model, the prize should have reformed by strengthening pathways for writers from the global South or including translated fiction.
