"While technology can be a tool for development, its impact is determined by the social power dynamics and political structures it inhabits rather than just individual intent or capacity."

Kentaro Toyama’s essay should be welcomed for puncturing the fantasy that machines can rescue the poor from politics. Yet his “magnifier” metaphor needs a sharper account of power. Intent and capacity do not float freely inside individuals, waiting to be enlarged by a tool. They are produced, constrained, and punished by social relations. A woman’s “motivation” to use a mobile phone, a Dalit child’s “capacity” to enter a telecenter, or a farmer’s “intent” to challenge a corrupt official cannot be understood apart from the risks attached to those acts.

Toyama is careful to say that he is not blaming the victim. But phrases such as “learned helplessness,” “frivolous desires,” and “limited capacity” can easily slide into a familiar development story in which poor people’s choices are treated as the obstacle to their liberation. If young men dominate telecenters, why is that? Because technology magnifies generic human desire for entertainment? Partly. But also because village public space is gendered. Because girls may be discouraged from loitering near a shop. Because caste and class determine who feels entitled to touch a keyboard. Because parents may fear reputational harm if daughters communicate with outsiders. Because the telecenter operator may belong to one faction and not another. These are not incidental “context” variables. They are the development problem.

The same is true of mobile phones. Toyama mentions Jenna Burrell’s work on gender politics and phones, and rightly notes that phones can become tools of sexual exchange or male control. But this should lead not to skepticism about phones as such, but to more political questions: Who owns the handset? Who controls airtime? Can a woman charge the phone safely? Can she keep numbers private? Are there women’s groups that can make phone use legitimate? Are there services designed around shared access, voice interfaces, or confidential counseling? A phone in the hands of a patriarch may strengthen patriarchy; a phone in the hands of a woman linked to a savings group, health worker, or labor organizer may alter the terms of dependence.

Technology is not neutral in these struggles. Nor is it all-powerful. It is a site of contestation. Consider the difference between a telecenter placed in the shop of a dominant-caste male entrepreneur and one governed by a women’s self-help group. The hardware may be identical, but the social meaning is not. In one case, the computer says, “Modernity belongs to those who already command the village.” In the other, it may say, “Women can manage accounts, information, and public dealings.” That symbolic shift will not abolish poverty, but neither is it trivial.

Toyama’s essay is strongest when he criticizes the “myth of scale.” Scale is often the enemy of justice because it strips away the very negotiations that make an intervention inclusive. A million laptops can be announced from a capital city. A million equitable learning relationships cannot. A national telecenter program can count kiosks; it cannot easily count whether widows, low-caste laborers, adolescent girls, or linguistic minorities feel welcome inside them. The pressure to scale favors standardized procurement over local accountability.

But there is also a danger in romanticizing “human capacity” as the slow alternative to technology. Capacity-building programs can be just as top-down, patronizing, and captured as technology programs. Teacher training can be useless. Community participation can mean meetings dominated by local elites. Microfinance can discipline poor women into repayment obligations without increasing their power. Agricultural extension can serve large farmers first. The issue is not technology versus human development. It is whether either is organized democratically.

Toyama’s examples suggest that he knows this. Digital Green succeeded not simply because it had good nonprofit staff but because it used local farmers in videos and relied on social persuasion. Its genius was not the camera alone; it was the reconfiguration of expertise. Farmers were not passive recipients of distant scientific instruction. They saw people like themselves experimenting and explaining. That is a technology-mediated shift in authority. It complicates the magnifier thesis because the technology did not merely amplify existing extension; it helped change who could be seen as a teacher.

This is why the design of ICT4D should begin not with “What device?” but with “Whose agency?” If the goal is girls’ education, technology must be judged by whether it gives girls more time, safety, confidence, and recognized achievement—not by the number of machines distributed. If the goal is health, it must be judged by whether patients can act on advice, not merely receive messages. If the goal is accountable government, it must be judged by whether complainants are protected from retaliation and whether officials face consequences. If the goal is livelihoods, it must be judged by whether weaker market actors gain bargaining power, not merely information.

Opportunity costs, too, must be analyzed politically. Toyama’s contrast between laptops and deworming is compelling, but budgets are not neutral containers. A minister may prefer laptops not only because he believes in technology, but because procurement contracts produce patronage, ribbon-cuttings, and international applause. A donor may prefer a mobile-health pilot because it promises measurable outputs and partnership with a technology firm. A corporation may prefer “shared value” projects that open new markets. The problem is not simply mistaken theory; it is institutional self-interest dressed as innovation.

A feminist and anti-caste ICT4D agenda would therefore be modest about gadgets and ambitious about power. It would fund women’s collectives to decide what communication tools they need. It would support local-language content created by marginalized groups. It would measure who uses facilities by gender, caste, age, and class. It would place maintenance money in the budget, not in a footnote. It would train intermediaries from the communities most often excluded. It would assume that conflict is normal: if technology gives some people voice, others may try to silence them.

The question “Can technology end poverty?” is too blunt. Poverty is not an absence of devices but a structure of subordination. Technology can reinforce that structure, decorate it, or sometimes help people crack it open. Toyama is right that no machine substitutes for human intent and capacity. But we should ask whose intent counts, whose capacity is recognized, and whose use of technology is feared. The poor do not need us to choose between laptops and liberation. They need tools embedded in struggles that are already theirs.

"Technology cannot end poverty because poverty is a product of unequal power relationships rather than a lack of information, meaning technology only facilitates progress when it is used as a tool within political struggles to contest those relationships."

The question “Can technology end poverty?” has an oddly apolitical sound, as if poverty were a locked door and technology one possible key. Poverty is not a door. It is a relationship among people: employers and workers, landlords and tenants, creditors and debtors, officials and citizens, men and women, dominant castes and subordinated castes, rich countries and poor ones. A technology that leaves those relationships untouched may make life more convenient without making it more just.

Kentaro Toyama’s essay is valuable because it reintroduces human beings into a debate enchanted by devices. But I would go further. The central issue is not just “human intent and capacity.” It is power. Some people benefit from the poverty of others. Some officials profit from opacity. Some employers prefer workers who cannot coordinate. Some husbands prefer wives without private channels of communication. Some local elites prefer villagers who do not know their entitlements. In these settings, technology is not merely adopted or ignored; it is fought over.

That is why transparency projects so often disappoint. Put government records online and nothing happens if citizens cannot read them, courts will not act, journalists are intimidated, and officials face no sanctions. Create a corruption hotline and it may become a list of complainants for corrupt authorities to punish. Digitize welfare payments and leakage may fall, or new intermediaries may arise to extract fees. The technology changes the terrain, but not necessarily the balance of power.

Yet technology can matter precisely when it enters political struggle. A mobile phone can help workers coordinate a protest. A cheap camera can document police abuse. A community radio station can broadcast land-rights information. A database can help an advocacy group identify patterns of exclusion. SMS can mobilize voters or warn residents of violence. These uses do not “end poverty” in the technocratic sense; they help people contest the arrangements that keep them poor.

Toyama’s examples of entertainment and ringtones may be accurate, but they are incomplete. The same networks that carry gossip carry rumors, organizing, news, and dissent. The same phones that reinforce gender control may allow women to maintain kinship ties or seek help. The same Internet that young men use for films may expose them to jobs, politics, and worlds beyond the village. None of this guarantees emancipation. But politics has never offered guarantees.

The development industry often prefers apolitical technology because it is safer. A donor can fund laptops without confronting land distribution. A corporation can support mobile health without discussing drug pricing or public budgets. A government can inaugurate telecenters while avoiding teacher absenteeism or police brutality. “Innovation” becomes a language for avoiding conflict. Toyama’s opportunity-cost critique should therefore be read not only financially but politically: What hard reform is being postponed because a gadget provides the appearance of action?

At the same time, anti-technology rhetoric can serve power too. Elites have always found reasons why the poor are not ready for new tools: not literate enough for books, not responsible enough for votes, not trained enough for computers, not sophisticated enough for the Internet. Toyama does not make that argument, but his emphasis on prior capacity could be misused by those who would indefinitely defer access. The answer is not to wait until citizens are fully empowered before giving them means of communication. Empowerment often begins with imperfect access.

A political approach would ask three questions of any ICT4D proposal. First, who gains leverage? If the answer is mainly vendors, ministers, consultants, or already privileged users, beware. Second, who might lose control? If no entrenched interest is even mildly inconvenienced, the project may be less transformative than advertised. Third, what institutions convert information into consequences? Without courts, unions, parties, social movements, journalists, cooperatives, or accountable agencies, information often evaporates.

Technology will not end poverty because poverty is not simply a lack of information. But struggles against poverty will use technology, just as they use meetings, pamphlets, songs, strikes, lawsuits, and elections. The poor need schools and clinics, yes. They also need channels through which to demand them. The task is not to worship the channel. It is to put it in the hands of those willing to speak.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary obstacle to development is not just a lack of human capacity, but the active resistance of powerful elites who benefit from the poverty and opacity that technology might otherwise disrupt.
  2. 02Technological interventions like transparency projects often fail because they do not change the underlying balance of power or the institutional lack of accountability.
  3. 03Technology matters most when it provides marginalized groups with the means to coordinate, document abuses, and mobilize for political reform.
  4. 04The development industry often promotes apolitical technological solutions as a way to avoid the conflict inherent in necessary structural reforms like land distribution or government accountability.
  5. 05While human capacity is important, emphasizing it as a prerequisite for technology access can be misused by elites to indefinitely defer the empowerment of the poor.
  6. 06The effectiveness of any technology project depends on the existence of robust institutions, such as courts and unions, that can convert information into tangible social consequences.
