"To realize the benefits of the Internet of Things while minimizing privacy risks, security must be integrated into the product design through mandatory baselines, limited connectivity, and independent certification."

I am less worried about the “Internet of Things” than about the Internet of Cheap Things. The worst privacy failures will not come from some science-fiction supercomputer. They will come from inexpensive cameras, routers, toys and appliances rushed to market by firms that have never thought seriously about security. A webcam with a factory password, an unpatched operating system and no easy update mechanism is not a miracle of innovation. It is a peephole.

The possibilities are nevertheless impressive. Smart irrigation systems could save water in drought-prone regions. Networked medical monitors could allow doctors to spot trouble before a patient lands in the emergency room. Industrial sensors can make bridges, factories and power grids safer. Even mundane applications — a package that reports its temperature during shipping, a parking space that reports availability — can reduce waste.

But minimizing risk requires us to be blunt: these objects are computers, and computer security is hard. We cannot rely on consumers to fix problems they cannot see. Most people will not change default passwords, install firmware updates or read technical warnings. Nor should the safety of a baby monitor depend on whether exhausted new parents understand router configuration.

Manufacturers need a security baseline. Devices should not use universal default credentials. They should authenticate users properly. They should encrypt sensitive communications. They should receive patches for a clearly stated support period. They should fail safely. If a company cannot maintain the software in a connected device, it should think twice before connecting it.

There is also a design lesson: not everything needs to connect to the public Internet. Some devices can work on a local network. Some can transmit only aggregated data. Some can have physical switches that cut off cameras and microphones. Engineers often ask, “Can we connect this?” The better question is, “What is the least connectivity needed to deliver the benefit?”

Finally, we need independent testing. We have safety standards for toasters because bad toasters can burn down houses. Insecure devices can also cause real harm: stalking, burglary, identity theft, sabotage. A trusted certification mark for basic security would help consumers and reward responsible companies.

The Internet of Things can be made safer, but only if we stop treating security as a patch to be added after the product launch. It must be part of the product itself.

  1. 01The greatest security threats arise from inexpensive, poorly designed devices that lack basic protections like unique passwords and update mechanisms.
  2. 02Smart devices offer significant societal benefits, including resource conservation, improved medical monitoring, and increased industrial safety.
  3. 03Security cannot be the responsibility of the consumer, as most users lack the technical expertise to manage firmware updates or complex configurations.
  4. 04Manufacturers must adhere to a security baseline that includes encrypted communications, proper user authentication, and a commitment to software maintenance.
  5. 05Engineers should minimize risk by limiting device connectivity to the public internet and incorporating physical privacy switches.
  6. 06Independent testing and safety certifications are necessary to protect consumers from the physical and digital harms caused by insecure devices.
