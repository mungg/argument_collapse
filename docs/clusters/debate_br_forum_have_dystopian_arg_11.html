"To ensure a humane AI future, we must redirect technology to support and value the essential caregiving and relational work that sustains society rather than using it as a cheap substitute for human attention."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay rightly asks whether AI will be used to replace human beings or to make them more capable. But the answer depends partly on what kinds of human work we value. If we measure productivity mainly through markets, wages, and labor shares, we risk missing the work that sustains society: caregiving, teaching, healing, listening, feeding, cleaning, mentoring, and maintaining relationships. Much of this work is performed by women, often underpaid or unpaid. Any humane AI future must begin there.

The pandemic has made this impossible to ignore. COVID-19 did not merely disrupt the economy; it revealed its hidden foundation. When schools closed, parents—especially mothers—absorbed the shock. When hospitals filled, nurses, aides, cleaners, and home care workers carried enormous risk. When elder care facilities became dangerous, families faced impossible choices. When service workers were called “essential,” many discovered that essential work often came with low pay, little protection, and limited voice. The crisis exposed a contradiction: the work most necessary for human life is frequently treated as least worthy of investment.

AI could worsen that contradiction. In education, health care, elder care, and social services, budget pressures create incentives to use technology as a substitute for human attention. Automated grading, online modules, symptom checkers, remote monitoring, and chatbot counseling can be useful tools. But if they become excuses to reduce staffing, enlarge caseloads, or replace relationships with screens, they will degrade care. A child does not need only information; she needs encouragement, interpretation, discipline, and trust. An elderly person does not need only medication reminders; he needs companionship and dignity. A patient does not need only a risk score; she needs someone who can understand fear, family, pain, and context.

Acemoglu sees this clearly when he imagines AI that empowers teachers, nurses, technicians, and doctors rather than automating them. That should be the center of the agenda. But to make it real, we need to treat care work as skilled work. Too often “human judgment” is celebrated in abstract while the workers who exercise it are paid poorly. Home health aides notice subtle changes in mood, appetite, mobility, and cognition. Child care workers manage conflict, language development, safety, and emotional regulation. Teachers read confusion and curiosity in a classroom. Nurses coordinate complex care across bodies, families, and institutions. These are not soft extras around the real technical work; they are forms of expertise.

AI systems built without this understanding will fail. They will optimize what is easy to measure: test scores, visit times, medication adherence, billing codes, patient throughput, response rates. But care cannot be reduced to measurable outputs without losing something essential. The danger is not only job loss. It is the Taylorization of care: breaking relational work into standardized tasks, timing each task, monitoring compliance, and using data to discipline workers. We already see this in home care systems that allocate minutes for bathing or feeding, in schools obsessed with metrics, and in hospitals where documentation can overwhelm bedside attention. AI could intensify this logic.

A feminist AI agenda would ask different questions. Does the system give caregivers more time with people, or less? Does it increase worker autonomy, or impose scripts? Does it help identify unmet needs, or merely ration services? Does it reduce racial and gender disparities, or encode them? Does it make unpaid labor visible, or exploit it further? Does it support collective provision, or shift responsibility back onto families?

Consider education. AI tutors may help students practice math or reading. They may provide teachers with real-time information about where students struggle. But if school districts adopt them primarily to cut costs, wealthier children will continue receiving human mentorship while poorer children receive software. That would reproduce inequality under the banner of personalization. The priority should be smaller classes, better-paid teachers, special education support, counselors, and culturally responsive curricula, with AI as an aid. A tool that helps a teacher see patterns across students is valuable. A tool that replaces teachers in underfunded schools is not.

In health care, AI can assist diagnosis, monitor drug interactions, predict deterioration, and reduce paperwork. These are real benefits. But health care is already distorted by billing systems, administrative burden, and unequal access. If AI is developed mainly for profitable specialties, hospital efficiency, and insurer cost control, it may not improve health for those most in need. We need AI for community clinics, maternal health, disability support, mental health, and elder care. We need tools designed with nurses, aides, patients, and families, not only with physicians and administrators.

Elder care deserves special attention. Aging societies will face growing demand for support. Some technologists imagine robots and sensors filling the gap. There is a role for devices that prevent falls, detect emergencies, assist mobility, or reduce isolation. But the “gap” is not simply a shortage of gadgets; it is a shortage of decent care jobs. We have chosen to pay care workers badly and to organize care through fragmented, underfunded systems. AI should not become a way to avoid that political choice. If technology helps one caregiver safely support more people while improving quality, good. If it allows agencies to cut visits and leave elders alone with monitoring devices, that is abandonment disguised as innovation.

The labor-share metric Acemoglu proposes may help distinguish worker-enhancing technologies from labor-displacing ones, but care work also requires attention to unpaid labor. If an AI health system reduces hospital staffing by pushing monitoring responsibilities onto family members, market data may miss the transfer. If educational software requires parents to supervise children at home, mothers may pay the price in lost work time and stress. If elder care technologies assume a family caregiver will respond to alerts, the system may externalize labor onto women. AI policy should measure these burdens.

Democratic oversight must therefore include caregivers and care recipients. Too often technology is designed by people far removed from the environments where it will operate. Engineers imagine an average user; care systems contain vulnerable, diverse, and dependent people. Children, elders, disabled people, patients with limited English, and overworked staff all experience technology differently. Participatory design is not a luxury. It is a safeguard against harm.

There is also a privacy dimension specific to care. Health, education, and home life generate intimate data. Children’s learning patterns, mental health histories, disability status, genetic risks, reproductive health, household routines—all can be valuable to companies and insurers. AI systems in care settings must follow strict data minimization and consent rules. The fact that a person needs care should not make them a data mine. Nor should workers be subjected to constant surveillance in the name of quality. Trust is essential to care, and trust cannot flourish under omnipresent monitoring.

Acemoglu’s broader political economy argument is correct: markets alone will not produce socially desirable AI. Care work proves the point. Markets undervalue care because much of its benefit is diffuse, long-term, and relational. A well-cared-for child becomes a healthier adult; a supported elder avoids suffering; a patient who trusts a nurse follows treatment; a disabled person with assistance participates in community life. These gains do not accrue neatly to the firm that invests in them. Public funding and social norms must fill the gap.

The best AI future is not one in which human beings are freed from dependence on one another. Dependence is not a defect to be engineered away; it is a condition of life. We are born dependent, many of us become dependent through illness or age, and all of us rely on networks of care. The question is whether AI will strengthen those networks or replace them with cheaper simulations.

Acemoglu is right that AI need not be dystopian. But avoiding dystopia requires expanding our idea of productivity. A society is not productive merely because it produces more goods with fewer workers. It is productive when it enables people to live, learn, heal, age, and care with dignity. AI should be judged by that standard.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Standard economic metrics like labor share and market wages fail to capture the immense social value of unpaid or underpaid care work predominantly performed by women.
  2. 02AI applications in education and healthcare risk 'Taylorizing' care by reducing complex human relationships into standardized, measurable tasks that degrade the quality of service.
  3. 03The development of AI should prioritize increasing the autonomy and capacity of frontline workers like teachers and nurses rather than automating their roles to cut costs.
  4. 04AI policy must account for the 'externalization' of labor, ensuring that technological efficiency in the market doesn't simply shift the burden of work onto unpaid family caregivers.
  5. 05Democratic oversight of AI must include the voices of caregivers and vulnerable recipients through participatory design to prevent the exploitation of intimate data and the erosion of trust.
  6. 06True productivity should be redefined to measure a society's ability to provide dignity in learning, healing, and aging rather than just the maximization of industrial output.
