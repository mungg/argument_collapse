Diversified MiniMax

"Mie Inouye’s theory of solidarity is limited because it fails to account for how immigration status and the threat of state violence create unique vulnerabilities that cannot be resolved through organizational endurance or epistemic humility alone."

As a longtime activist in immigrant rights organizing, I read Mie Inouye's essay with both appreciation and frustration. The essay captures something true about the challenges of multiracial coalition building and the limitations of deference politics. But it also reveals a troubling blindness to how immigration status structures political possibility in ways that pure analysis of race and class cannot fully address.

Inouye writes about "difference" as if it were a general category applicable across contexts. She discusses the Rainbow Coalition of the 1960s and 1970s without adequately addressing how the historical presence of undocumented workers in the United States differed from today. She critiques deference politics without considering how deferring to people without legal status might operate differently from deferring to documented people who are racialized or poor. In short, her essay treats solidarity as a problem of consciousness and organization without adequately engaging with how citizenship and immigration status structure the field of political possibility.

This matters because immigration status creates a political condition unlike race or class. You cannot educate your way out of being undocumented. You cannot organize your way out of your immigration status (though organizing can change the legal framework). You cannot transform your way out of the precarious position that undocumented people occupy at the intersection of multiple systems of domination. When an undocumented person "shows up" to an organizing meeting, they are showing up under conditions of extreme vulnerability that no amount of epistemic humility or conflict tolerance can dissolve.

This does not mean that solidarity across immigration status is impossible. It means that it requires different theoretical frameworks and organizational practices than solidarity across race or class. In my own organizing, we have learned that the question "what do you want?" must be asked differently of people with different immigration statuses. For documented allies, the question might be about policy demands or ideological education. For undocumented members, the question is often about survival itself: How do we stay safe? How do we avoid deportation? How do we access healthcare without exposing ourselves to ICE?

Inouye's invocation of Bob Lee's organizing tactics—the physical proximity, the insistence that people speak, the use of touch to establish authority and care—is compelling. But it assumes a political context in which the people being organized are not under immediate threat of deportation or criminalization. When the young white woman finally speaks about her brother being stabbed by a cop, her disclosure does not immediately expose her to state violence. For undocumented people, the calculation is different. Every disclosure is a risk. Every visible participation in collective action is a potential target.

This is not to say that undocumented people cannot or should not organize. The Immigrant Workers' Freedom Ride, the movement for sanctuary cities, the campaigns for the DREAM Act and DACA have all demonstrated that undocumented people are among the most courageous and effective organizers in the country. It is to say that theories of solidarity developed without adequate attention to immigration status may not translate cleanly into contexts where status matters.

I want to propose that the framework of social endurance needs to be supplemented by what we might call "radical hospitality"—organizational practices that specifically address the vulnerabilities created by immigration status. This includes knowing your rights, having legal support available, creating physical spaces that are genuinely sanctuary, and developing relationships of mutual accountability that do not require undocumented people to expose themselves to risk. It also includes acknowledging that documented people have specific obligations to undocumented people that cannot be reduced to reciprocal solidarity.

Inouye is right that solidarity requires us to "bump up against other people" and allow ourselves to be changed. But "bumping up" is not possible for people who cannot afford to be visible. The theory of solidarity that emerges from her essay must grapple with this constraint. Otherwise, it risks universalizing the experience of people who can afford to show up to meetings and treating it as the model for all coalition building.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Immigration status creates a distinct political condition that differs from race or class because it involves a level of legal precarity that cannot be transformed through shifts in consciousness or ideological education.
  2. 02The physical and vocal organizing tactics Inouye celebrates, such as public disclosure and visibility, carry disproportionate risks for undocumented individuals that documented participants do not face.
  3. 03The question of 'what do you want' in an organizing context must be framed differently for undocumented people, as their primary needs often center on immediate survival and protection from deportation rather than general policy demands.
  4. 04A robust theory of solidarity must supplement 'social endurance' with 'radical hospitality,' which involves specific organizational practices designed to mitigate the risks and vulnerabilities inherent to being undocumented.
  5. 05Documented allies have specific, non-reciprocal obligations to protect undocumented comrades that are not captured by a model of solidarity based on mutual transformation through conflict.
