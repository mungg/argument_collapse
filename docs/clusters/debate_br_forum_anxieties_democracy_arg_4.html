"While representative institutions are necessary, the health and legitimacy of democracy depend primarily on the disruptive, extra-institutional movements that force legislatures to address injustice and expand inclusion."

I share Ira Katznelson’s anxiety about democracy, but I worry that his account gives too much honor to representative institutions and too little to the unruly democratic forces that have made those institutions tolerable. The legislature is not simply the house of democratic reason. It has also been the place where democratic claims go to die, where polite procedures have dignified exclusion, and where “compromise” has often meant sacrificing those with the least power.

The American Congress whose reconstruction Katznelson urges was for much of its history a profoundly undemocratic body. The Senate overrepresented slave states and then segregationist states; committees were ruled by seniority systems that empowered southern conservatives; labor rights, anti-lynching bills, voting rights, and fair housing were delayed or defeated by exactly the procedures now celebrated as legislative dignity. If democracy has advanced in the United States, it is because people outside the formal channels forced themselves into view.

That history complicates the essay’s suspicion of populism and extra-institutional participation. The abolitionists were often described as fanatics who would not accept the ordinary compromises of representative politics. Suffragists were disruptive. Industrial unionists engaged in strikes, sit-downs, and mass pressure that respectable opinion considered coercive. Civil rights activists broke laws, filled jails, boycotted buses, and embarrassed presidents. ACT UP later made health bureaucracies and politicians respond to AIDS with urgency. These movements did not merely “invigorate” representative democracy from the outside; they altered what representation meant.

Katznelson is right that mobilization cannot substitute for legislation. The Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act mattered because they became law. But they became law because non-legislative democracy changed the moral and political terrain. When we say that legislatures “settle” conflicts, we should ask who had the power to place the conflict on the agenda in the first place. For many Americans, the answer has rarely been their representative. It has been the picket line, the lawsuit, the church meeting, the student organization, the riot, the boycott, or the community campaign.

This matters in 2015 because many of today’s most important democratic energies are similarly extra-institutional. The protests after the killings of Michael Brown, Eric Garner, Walter Scott, Freddie Gray, and others have exposed a gap between formal citizenship and lived subordination. Young activists using social media, street demonstrations, and disruptive tactics have forced local and national officials to speak about policing in ways they would have avoided. It would be a mistake to treat this as a lesser form of democracy because it is not legislative. It is a demand that legislative democracy become real.

The same is true, though in different ideological directions, of the Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street. Katznelson pairs them as populist efforts to sidestep representation. There is truth in that, but it is incomplete. Both movements expressed a belief that ordinary channels had been captured—by federal overreach and debt, in the Tea Party’s account; by banks and the wealthy, in Occupy’s. One need not endorse either movement’s full analysis to recognize the democratic significance of their grievance: citizens looked at governing institutions after the financial crisis and concluded that accountability had failed.

The danger is not participation outside institutions. The danger is the lack of a credible path from participation to power. Occupy dramatized inequality but struggled to convert protest into organization. The Tea Party proved much more capable of entering primaries and disciplining officeholders, but often intensified anti-legislative obstruction. Black Lives Matter, still emerging, faces the challenge of translating moral exposure into local, state, and federal change without being absorbed or neutralized. These are organizational problems, not reasons to retreat to a legislature-centered account of democracy.

Indeed, a legislature-centered account can obscure the ways representation itself must be democratized. Who gets represented? Through what districts? With what barriers to voting? Under what campaign finance system? In a country where voter ID laws, felony disenfranchisement, gerrymandering, and the weakening of the Voting Rights Act shape the electorate, the legislature cannot be presumed to embody the people. It embodies a filtered people. Sometimes the most democratic act is to challenge the filter.

Katznelson’s warning about Peronism and illiberal populism is serious. A politics that imagines one authentic people embodied by one leader is hostile to pluralism and criticism. But not all populism is Peronist. There is also a democratic populism that insists institutions answer to those they exclude. Its rhetoric can be overheated, its diagnoses partial, its tactics uncomfortable. But without it, liberal democracy becomes a constitutional museum: elegant forms, guarded procedures, and ever fewer citizens who believe they matter.

The task, then, is not to choose between representation and mobilization. It is to build stronger conduits between them. Parties should be more open to social movements. Cities should experiment with participatory budgeting and civilian oversight. Labor law should make worker organization possible again. Voting rights should be expanded, not merely protected. Campaigns should be financed in ways that amplify small donors. Legislatures should be pressured, occupied, petitioned, and sometimes shamed.

Democracy’s anxiety is not that people are too impatient with representation. It is that representation has too often been patient with injustice. We need legislatures, yes. But we also need the noisy, impolite, morally urgent democratic action that makes legislatures worth saving.

  5. 05The primary danger to democracy is not populist participation, but the lack of credible conduits to translate social mobilization into institutional power.

"Populist mobilization should be viewed as a necessary corrective to a captured and unresponsive representative system rather than a threat to democratic stability."

Ira Katznelson worries that populism may become a substitute for representative democracy. I worry about the opposite: that fear of populism will become an excuse to protect a representative system that no longer represents very well. In a time of concentrated wealth, professionalized politics, and institutional self-dealing, populist anger is not the disease. It is a symptom, and sometimes a cure.

The word “populism” is often used by elites as a warning label for any politics that is too loud, too angry, or too distrustful of established authority. Certainly there are dangerous populisms. A movement that imagines a single pure people, treats opponents as traitors, or longs for a strongman is hostile to liberal democracy. But there is also a populism that begins from a democratic insight: institutions can become captured, and ordinary citizens may need to mobilize against them.

The financial crisis made that insight unavoidable. Millions lost homes, jobs, savings, and security. The largest banks were rescued; few powerful executives were held accountable; the language of necessity wrapped decisions that seemed to favor insiders. Experts explained why bailouts were required to prevent catastrophe, and perhaps they were right. But democracy is not sustained by technocratic correctness alone. When citizens see failure rewarded at the top and austerity prescribed below, they conclude that the system is rigged.

Occupy Wall Street named that reality with unusual force. Its “99 percent” slogan was imprecise but politically clarifying. It changed public language about inequality, debt, and plutocracy. It did not produce a conventional legislative program, and its horizontal style limited its staying power. But it accomplished something representative institutions had failed to do: it made economic inequality a central democratic issue.

The Tea Party did something similar from the right. I disagree with much of its analysis, especially its tendency to treat social insurance and health reform as tyranny while underplaying private power. But it was not simply irrational rage. It reflected a belief that both parties had expanded government, debt, and bailouts without consent. Tea Party activists attended meetings, challenged incumbents, and used primaries to impose accountability as they understood it. That is not outside democracy. It is democracy, even when inconvenient.

Katznelson writes as though mobilized citizens risk displacing legislatures. Yet in the United States, the more common problem is that legislatures displace citizens. Congressional districts are gerrymandered. Senate representation gives vastly unequal weight to voters depending on state size. Campaigns depend on donors. Lobbyists write details. Committee procedures are opaque. Party leaders control agendas. Voters are invited to choose between packages assembled elsewhere. Under these conditions, periodic eruptions of popular pressure are not threats to representation; they are reminders that representation is conditional.

The fear of Peronism is instructive but overdrawn. Juan Perón used patronage, nationalism, and executive power to restrict pluralism. That danger exists wherever a leader claims unmediated union with the people. But American populist movements today are fragmented, decentralized, and suspicious of leaders. Occupy could barely tolerate spokespeople. The Tea Party pressured politicians more than it worshiped one. The danger in America is less a charismatic populist regime than an entrenched oligarchic one wearing democratic clothing.

If populism is to renew rather than corrode democracy, it needs organization. Anger must become durable power. That means primaries, parties, unions, local associations, ballot campaigns, litigation, and legislative agendas. It means learning the boring arts of politics without losing the moral clarity that made mobilization possible. The problem with Occupy was not that it was too populist, but that it was not institutional enough. The problem with the Tea Party was not that it pressured representatives, but that its pressure often rewarded obstruction over governing.

Still, we should not ask populist movements to become polite before we hear them. Politeness is often a demand made by those comfortable with existing arrangements. The abolitionists were impolite. Labor organizers were impolite. Civil rights sit-ins were impolite. The current protests against police violence are impolite because polite requests have failed for decades. Democracy needs moments when citizens refuse the terms of debate offered by official politics.

Katznelson is right that lawmaking matters. Movements that cannot translate protest into policy will disappoint their followers. But translation should not mean domestication. The best representative democracy is one in which officeholders know that citizens are watching, organizing, and capable of removing them. Populism supplies fear, and democratic representatives sometimes need to be afraid.

There is also a moral danger in too much reverence for procedure. Procedures are not neutral when access is unequal. A legislative compromise between the well organized and the barely heard may be procedurally proper and substantively corrupt. To say this is not to reject liberal democracy; it is to insist that liberal democracy be judged by whether it empowers the many against the few.

The task, then, is to distinguish democratic populism from authoritarian populism. Democratic populism expands participation, challenges concentrated power, protects criticism, and seeks institutional reform. Authoritarian populism narrows the people, attacks minorities, and concentrates power in a leader. The first may save representation from decay. The second may destroy it.

In 2015 the greater threat is not that citizens believe too much in their own power. It is that many no longer believe they have any. Populist insurgency is democracy’s alarm bell. We should not smash the alarm because the sound is unpleasant.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary danger to American democracy is not charismatic authoritarianism but an entrenched oligarchic system that protects elite interests over ordinary citizens.
  2. 02Populist movements like Occupy Wall Street and the Tea Party perform a vital democratic function by forcing neglected issues like economic inequality and institutional accountability into the public discourse.
  3. 03Legislative procedures and political politeness are often used by elites as tools to domesticate dissent and maintain unequal power structures.
  4. 04Democratic populism strengthens representation by reminding officeholders that their authority is conditional and that they must remain accountable to a mobilized public.
  5. 05The failure of populist movements often stems from a lack of institutional organization rather than an inherent incompatibility with representative democracy.
  6. 06A healthy liberal democracy must be judged by its ability to empower the many against the few, rather than by its adherence to neutral-seeming procedures that favor the well-connected.
