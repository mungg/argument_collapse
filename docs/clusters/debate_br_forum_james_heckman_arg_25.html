"James Heckman’s proposal for early childhood intervention is insufficient because it ignores how structural racism and racial discrimination—rather than just parenting quality—shape the disadvantages faced by minority children."

James Heckman’s essay is a masterful synthesis of developmental science and economic evaluation, but it suffers from a stunning omission: the role of racial discrimination in shaping the diverging destinies he documents. He observes that gaps in cognitive and non-cognitive skills open up early and persist, and that a growing fraction of American children are born into disadvantage. Yet he never seriously considers that the mechanisms of disadvantage include not just parenting quality, but also the structural racism that saturates housing, policing, employment, and schooling. The result is a colorblind policy prescription that risks reinforcing the very inequalities it seeks to remedy.

Heckman writes that “birth is becoming fate,” but for black children in America, fate has long been tied to skin color. The high school dropout rates he recalculates with such care—showing that roughly 65 percent of black and Hispanic students leave school without a diploma—are presented as a consequence of early family environments and non-cognitive deficits. He does not ask why those families are disadvantaged in the first place. The residential segregation that concentrates black families in neighborhoods with underfunded schools, environmental toxins, and violent policing is no accident; it is the legacy of redlining, restrictive covenants, and ongoing housing discrimination. This context shapes parenting in ways that go far beyond a mother’s word count.

Heckman cites the Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE) study as evidence that early adversity leads to poor adult outcomes. He does not mention that black children are far more likely to experience such adversities, not because of inherent family pathology but because of generations of unequal treatment. The ACE framework risks pathologizing individuals while ignoring the political and economic structures that generate those childhood experiences. A child living in a neighborhood where asthma rates are elevated due to diesel pollution from nearby highways, where her father is more likely to be incarcerated for a nonviolent drug offense because of racialized policing, and where her mother faces hiring discrimination is experiencing adversity that no dose of character-building preschool can fully overcome.

Even Heckman’s treatment of cognitive and non-cognitive skills raises concerns. The “non-cognitive” concept, as scholars like Annette Lareau have shown, is deeply cultural. The qualities labeled as perseverance, self-regulation, and motivation are often judged through middle-class white norms that may not be recognized or valued in the same way in African American communities. Programs that seek to instill these skills without attending to cultural difference risk imposing a hidden curriculum of assimilation. Heckman is sensitive to this, asking “Will the programs achieve high levels of compliance?” and noting potential tension between the values of the child and parents. But his faith in culturally diverse programs assumes that those programs can be designed and delivered without reproducing the very biases that lead to disproportionately harsh discipline of black boys in school. The school-to-prison pipeline is not merely a product of cognitive deficits; it is fueled by teachers and administrators who perceive black children’s behavior as threatening.

Moreover, Heckman’s central policy solution—early childhood programs—would, if implemented in a race-neutral manner, likely leave untouched the deep racial wealth gap. Even with sliding fee scales, universal programs do not address the fundamental economic inequality between black and white families. The average white family has nearly six times the net worth of the average black family, a disparity that no amount of early enrichment can erase. That wealth gap affects housing stability, parental stress, and access to the full range of opportunities that middle-class children take for granted. Any serious predistribution policy must therefore include race-specific transfers: baby bonds, reparations, or targeted investments in black communities that directly address historical injustices.

Heckman’s call to avoid the mistakes of the War on Poverty is particularly troubling. The War on Poverty was undermined not by its generosity but by the political backlash that racialized poverty and undermined support for broad-based social programs. By framing his intervention as predistribution, Heckman hopes to escape the equity-efficiency tradeoff and build political support. But if the programs are perceived as benefiting primarily minority communities—and the data he presents about minority disadvantage guarantee they will be—they will face the same political headwinds that welfare did. Without explicitly naming and confronting racism, the predistribution agenda is as fragile as the old redistribution one.

A true agenda for social mobility must put racial justice at its center, not as an afterthought tacked onto a portfolio of early childhood investments. That means acknowledging that the accident of birth includes the accident of race, and that the two are inextricably linked in America.
