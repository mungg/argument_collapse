"The lead essay's appeal to shared American principles is flawed because it treats these ideals as settled consensus rather than as deeply contested concepts with ambiguous meanings."

The Boston Review's lead essay invokes American principles as if they were settled and self-evident. But what are these principles, and where do they come from? A critical examination of the language of principles reveals tensions and ambiguities that the essay glosses over.

The essay claims that "everyone, not just the rich, should have the opportunity to get ahead." This sounds straightforward, but "getting ahead" is an ambiguous standard. Does it mean equal outcomes? Equal opportunity? Or merely the absence of formal barriers? Americans disagree sharply on what equality requires. Some believe that unequal results are acceptable so long as the rules are fair and individuals have freedom to pursue their goals. Others believe that genuine equality requires affirmative measures to address historical and structural barriers.

Similarly, the essay claims that "everyone, not just the rich, should have a right to be heard." But what does "heard" mean in a democracy? Does it require equal influence over political decisions? Or merely the formal right to vote and speak? The history of American democracy is largely a history of battles over these questions. The definition of "everyone" has expanded over time—from white male property owners to all white men, to Black men after Reconstruction, to women after 1920. Each expansion was contested, not because opponents rejected democracy in the abstract, but because they disagreed about who belonged to the political community.

The essay's invocation of "cherished principles" suggests a shared consensus that may be more mythical than real. The American founding wasshot through with contradictions: principles of liberty articulated by slaveholders, equality proclaimed in a society of hierarchy, consent celebrated in a world of dependency. These contradictions did not invalidate the principles, but they did mean that the meaning of principles was always contested and would remain so.

What the Occupy movement has done is to highlight one interpretation of American principles—the interpretation that emphasizes economic democracy, political equality, and community solidarity—and to contrast it with an alternative interpretation that emphasizes individual liberty, market freedom, and limited government. This is a genuine debate about values, not simply a question of whether we are "living up to" clearly defined principles.

I do not mean to suggest that all positions are equally valid or that the movement's concerns are merely rhetorical. The concentration of wealth and the decline of democratic participation are real phenomena that can be documented and measured. But the language of principles can obscure as much as it illuminates. Political conflict is not simply about applying universal principles to concrete situations; it is about defining what principles mean and which principles should take priority when they conflict.

The essays in this series should engage these deeper questions, not just the surface appeal to shared ideals.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The standard of 'getting ahead' is ambiguous because Americans disagree on whether equality requires equal outcomes, equal opportunity, or merely the absence of formal barriers.
  2. 02The right to be 'heard' in a democracy is historically contested, with ongoing debates over whether it implies equal political influence or merely formal voting rights.
  3. 03The American founding was characterized by inherent contradictions rather than a unified consensus, meaning principles have always been subject to conflicting interpretations.
  4. 04The Occupy movement represents a specific interpretation of principles—emphasizing economic democracy and solidarity—rather than a simple effort to uphold universally accepted ones.
  5. 05Political conflict is fundamentally about defining the meaning and priority of principles when they inevitably clash, rather than just applying universal ideals to reality.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Occupy movement's primary challenge is not a gap between shared principles and practice, but rather a fundamental lack of consensus regarding which principles should guide American society."

The Boston Review's lead essay describes the Occupy movement's attention to societal problems and its potential to re-democratize American politics. But I want to push back on one aspect of the essay's framing: the assumption that the problem is primarily about the gap between principles and practices rather than about the principles themselves.

The essay assumes that Americans share a commitment to equality of opportunity, political voice, and meaningful participation. It then asks why we are not living up to these principles. But this framing ignores the substantial segment of the American public that either rejects these principles or interprets them very differently than the Occupy movement does.

Consider the many Americans who embrace free-market ideology and view inequality not as a problem to be solved but as a natural and desirable consequence of liberty. For them, the market is just, and attempts to use the state to redistribute income or regulate business are themselves violations of principle—violations of property rights, contract freedom, and limited government. From this perspective, the Occupy movement represents not a struggle to realize shared ideals but a clash between incompatible value systems.

Or consider the Americans who prioritize national security, cultural tradition, or religious faith over democratic participation and economic equality. For them, the Occupy movement's focus on material concerns misses deeper questions about meaning, purpose, and social cohesion.

The essay's language of "cherished principles" implies a consensus that does not exist. American society is divided not only over policies but over the foundational values that should guide collective life. This is not necessarily a bad thing; democratic societies require ongoing debate about fundamental questions. But it means that the Occupy movement cannot simply appeal to shared principles and expect to build a broad coalition.

What does this imply for the movement's strategy? I think it suggests that Occupy needs to do more than articulate critiques; it needs to build bridges to constituencies with different values and priorities. The great reform movements of American history succeeded not by insisting on their own righteousness but by building diverse coalitions that could win elections and govern.

The movement has shown that it can capture attention and generate enthusiasm among its core supporters. Now it must show that it can also persuade those who do not share its premises. This will require engaging seriously with competing perspectives, not just denouncing the "1 percent" as villains.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay incorrectly assumes a national consensus on ideals like equality of opportunity, ignoring those who view inequality as a natural byproduct of liberty and free-market justice.
  2. 02Many Americans prioritize values such as national security, tradition, or faith over the economic and democratic participation goals emphasized by the movement.
  3. 03The movement's strategy of appealing to supposedly 'shared' principles is insufficient for building the broad, diverse coalitions necessary for lasting political reform.
  4. 04To succeed, the movement must move beyond denouncing the elite and engage seriously with competing value systems to persuade those who do not share its initial premises.
