"Instead of a broad wealth tax, the United States should implement a comprehensive system of taxes on economic rent—specifically land value, natural resources, and carbon—to more efficiently and securely curb the power of the superrich."

It is an odd but revealing feature of Saez and Zucman’s long and learned essay that they never mention the word “land.” They speak of wealth, of equity and bonds, of listed and private companies. Yet the original American property tax, the tax that funded local schools and roads in the nineteenth century, was above all a tax on land. And the greatest fortunes of the Gilded Age robber barons were built not only on oil and steel but on the ownership of land and the natural monopolies it made possible. By leaving land out of the conversation, the authors miss the most straightforward, most economically efficient, and most constitutionally secure way to tax the unearned wealth of the superrich: a broad-based land value tax, combined with a tax on other natural rents and a steep carbon tax.

My argument is not that the wealth tax is misguided. It is that a different tax base—economic rent in all its forms—would achieve the same democratic and distributive goals with fewer of the problems that have sunk wealth taxes elsewhere. And it would do so in a way that aligns with the gravest emergency we face, which is not only inequality but the climate crisis that the superrich have done so much to exacerbate.

Begin with the core insight of nineteenth-century political economist Henry George. Land is not created by human effort; its value arises from the community that surrounds it. When a plot of Manhattan becomes immensely valuable, that value is not generated by the owner but by the millions of people who work, live, and pass through the city, and by the public investments in subways, sewers, and safety. To tax that land value heavily—to capture at least a large fraction of the ground rent—is not to punish industry or thrift. It is to return to the community what the community creates. And because land cannot be moved to a tax haven or hidden in a shell corporation in Panama, a land value tax is uniquely hard to evade. Its valuation is easier than valuing a private equity stake because land is physically fixed, its boundaries are recorded, and even when transactions are scarce, comparable sales in the same city or county provide a reliable basis for assessment.

The authors’ billionaire problem can be partly solved through the lens of rent. When Jeff Bezos’s fortune soars, a substantial component of Amazon’s value lies in its control over land—warehouses, data centers, distribution nodes—and in its use of publicly built roads and delivery infrastructure, a form of land-based network externality. A federal land value tax, perhaps levied on commercial and industrial land at a higher rate than residential land, would reach that untaxed accretion of value without requiring the IRS to parse the labyrinthine structure of Bezos’s shareholdings. It would also be progressive in its incidence because land ownership is even more concentrated than financial wealth: the richest one percent of households own a vastly disproportionate share of real estate equity.

But land is only the most visible form of unearned rent. The royalties from mining and drilling on public lands, the spectrum licenses that telecom giants use, the landings slots at congested airports, and, most critically, the right to dump carbon into the atmosphere without charge—all of these are gifts from nature or from public policy that have been privatized at almost no cost. A properly designed system of resource taxation would cap the incomes of the superrich at their source, before they can accumulate into the dynastic fortunes that a wealth tax must later chase.

A carbon tax, for example, would drain rent from fossil fuel assets while generating hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue. So would a tax on financial transactions, which would recapture some of the rent extracted by high-frequency traders and investment banks from the liquidity of public markets. These taxes are not alternatives to the wealth tax in the sense of being mutually exclusive; they are complementary. But I would argue that they deserve priority because they attack the most socially harmful forms of profit-making. Saez and Zucman are right that extreme wealth functions as a negative externality. The best response to a negative externality is a Pigouvian tax calibrated to the damage, and the most damaging activities of the rich—pollution, resource extraction, land speculation—are far more directly and cheaply taxed at the point of the damage than through an annual levy on total net worth.

There is another reason to favor rent taxes now. As we face the economic fallout from the pandemic that is already shutting down cities and cratering employment, asset values are plunging. A wealth tax assessed on a snapshot of net worth at the end of this year would produce bizarre and unfair outcomes: a restaurant chain owner whose business is temporarily worthless might pay almost nothing, while a technology baron whose assets cratered in March but recovered by December could face a confusing liability. A land value tax, by contrast, is assessed on the underlying, long-term value of the site, not the volatile day-to-day price of a share of stock. It would provide a stable source of revenue in exactly the kind of crisis when governments need it most.

The wealth tax has undoubted rhetorical power; “taxing billionaires out of their billions” is a slogan that energizes voters. But rhetoric is not the same as governance. The authors acknowledge that Europe’s wealth taxes largely failed, but they underestimate the extent to which the failure was due to the inherent difficulty of taxing a broad, ill-defined asset base rather than to bad design choices. In the United States, a land value tax and its siblings have a long and honorable tradition. The progressive-era reformer Louis Brandeis argued that in the extraction of natural resources, “we must tax and tax and tax until we make it unprofitable to hold land for speculative purposes.” That was the spirit of the original property tax, before it was eroded by carve-outs for financial instruments.

So what would a comprehensive rent-tax strategy look like? At the federal level, a tax on the unimproved value of all land, with a higher surcharge on large commercial holdings, could be introduced alongside a carbon tax and a strengthened system of royalties for mineral extraction on public lands. The revenue would be distributed, at least in part, as a citizen’s dividend—a universal basic income or a dedicated fund for green infrastructure—making the tax progressive in its overall effect. Billionaires would still pay more, because they own more land and benefit disproportionately from the untaxed rents of the fossil fuel economy. But the tax would be levied on the income-generating capacity of their assets, not on a notional total net worth that may not reflect their actual ability to pay in cash.

I do not dismiss the work of Saez and Zucman. Their arguments have shifted the Overton window and made radical taxation thinkable again. But as the window opens, we should look beyond the single instrument of a wealth tax to the broader arsenal of rent taxation that is available. The superrich accumulate wealth not despite society but because of it—because of the legal privileges, public investments, and unpriced environmental sinks that society provides. Taxing those privileges directly is more efficient, more just, and more likely to endure. The robber barons of the twenty-first century must be tamed, but the best time to do it is when they reach into the common pot, not after they have already filled their vaults. Let us tax the taking, not the having.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Land value taxation is superior to a wealth tax because land is a community-created asset that cannot be hidden in tax havens or moved to avoid assessment.
  2. 02Taxing economic rent at its source, such as through carbon taxes or mineral royalties, prevents the accumulation of dynastic fortunes before they require the complex enforcement of a wealth tax.
  3. 03A land value tax provides a more stable and predictable revenue stream than a wealth tax because it is based on long-term site value rather than volatile, day-to-day market fluctuations of financial assets.
  4. 04Focusing on rent taxation aligns distributive goals with the urgent need to address the climate crisis by penalizing the negative externalities generated by the wealthy.
  5. 05Taxing the 'taking' of public resources and social privileges is more economically efficient and constitutionally secure than taxing the 'having' of accumulated net worth.
