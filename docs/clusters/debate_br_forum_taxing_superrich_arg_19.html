"While the diagnosis of extreme wealth inequality is correct, a radical wealth tax is premature and prone to failure; instead, the U.S. should pursue a sequenced strategy of moderate wealth taxation combined with robust reforms to the existing income and estate tax systems."

The urgency with which Saez and Zucman make their case is impossible to fault. Having tracked wealth concentration for years, they understand better than almost anyone how the American tax system became a machine for entrenching inequality. And they are right that a properly constructed wealth tax could, in theory, fill the yawning gap between what the ultra-rich can pay and what they actually do. But between the theoretical elegance of their proposal and the messy realities of governance, legislation, and global capital lies a chasm that their essay, for all its sophistication, does not fully bridge. My view is that the wealth tax, in the radical form they ultimately endorse, would be premature and prone to failure. A more moderate approach, combined with complementary reforms to the existing tax base, offers a better chance of achieving their goals without the enormous risks.

Let me start where there is common ground. The current tax system is a scandal. The effective tax rate on the Forbes 400 is lower than the rate paid by a typical household, and the primary reason is the sharp distinction between economic income and taxable income. Saez and Zucman’s diagnosis of the Buffett problem—unrealized capital gains sitting untouched for decades inside holding companies—is irrefutable. Something must be done to reach that accumulated value. Their solution, an annual tax on net worth, is one way to do it. But there are other, less disruptive paths to the same destination that deserve as much, if not more, attention.

First, we could dramatically strengthen the taxation of capital when it is actually realized, while also deeming realization at certain life events. One such reform is to tax capital gains at death, treating the transfer of assets to heirs as a realization event. This would end the “angel of death” loophole that allows unrealized gains to pass untaxed forever. A complementary measure is to end the practice of stepped-up basis at death, so that heirs cannot reset the cost basis of inherited assets. Together, these changes would subject the bulk of dynastic wealth to the income tax at least once per generation. They would not capture the year-by-year accumulation during a billionaire’s lifetime, but they would prevent the truly endless deferral that now exists.

Second, we could impose a mark-to-market taxation system for publicly traded securities, requiring taxpayers above a high threshold to pay tax annually on the change in value of their stock portfolios, regardless of whether they sell. This would close the loophole that allows billionaires like Bezos and Buffett to take no salary and receive no dividends while their wealth compounds. Because publicly traded stocks have objective daily prices, the valuation problem simply evaporates. For privately held business assets, a deemed-interest or deferral-charge approach could apply, where the tax is deferred until sale but accrues interest at a market rate to prevent the benefit of deferral. The authors’ own in-kind payment idea could still be used to handle true illiquidity. Mark-to-market taxation would not require a new tax base; it would operate within the familiar framework of the income tax, avoiding the constitutional quagmire of a direct property tax.

The constitutional objection cannot be waved away. The apportionment requirement for direct taxes is not a dead letter; it is the very reason the Sixteenth Amendment was adopted in 1913 to allow income taxation without apportionment. A wealth tax is not an income tax, and no amendment authorizes a general federal property tax. While some scholars argue that an unapportioned wealth tax would be upheld, the current Supreme Court has a conservative majority that has shown great skepticism toward novel interpretations of federal taxing power. If the Court were to strike down a wealth tax, the political damage would be staggering: the major progressive achievement of a generation would be nullified, and the precedent could jeopardize other forms of taxation on accumulated property, including a strengthened estate tax. It is safer, and more honest, to build on the constitutionally firm ground of the income tax.

Then there is the matter of international experience. Saez and Zucman note that European wealth taxes failed partly because their thresholds were too low. That is true, but only part of the story. The deeper lesson is that capital is mobile, and financial engineering is more creative than legislators. Once a wealth tax exists, the wealthy hire legions of lawyers to reclassify assets as trusts that fall below the exemption, to shift ownership to offshore entities, or to invest in art and other hard-to-value objects. Even with the best enforcement, a tax that depends on accurate annual valuations of every major asset is a tax that invites endless litigation and drains productive energy into tax avoidance. The IRS has been systematically underfunded for years; it is not the crack enforcement agency the authors imagine. Handing it the responsibility of valuing tens of thousands of private businesses and intangible assets would overwhelm it, leading to arbitrary assessments and widespread noncompliance.

A moderate wealth tax, say the Warren proposal of two percent above fifty million dollars and three percent above one billion, might be worth attempting as a complement to income tax reform. It would raise significant revenue, begin to curb the largest fortunes, and provide a laboratory to see whether the valuation and enforcement problems can be managed at the very top without spilling over onto the merely affluent. But the radical ten percent rate that Saez and Zucman dangle is a leap into the unknown. At that level, the incentive to expend resources on avoidance becomes immense, and the tax would almost certainly generate less revenue than projected as the largest fortunes shrink—exactly as they predict. That might be an acceptable outcome if the only goal is to reduce billionaires’ wealth. But in a time of immense fiscal need—and as of March 2020, with the coronavirus pandemic likely to require enormous public spending—we need revenue, not symbolic degrowth of the Forbes list. A radical wealth tax that deliberately reduces its own base is a luxury we cannot yet afford.

My preferred path is a sequenced strategy. First, pass the Sanders or Warren wealth tax at moderate rates, and simultaneously strengthen the IRS with dedicated funding for enforcement and a mandate to build the valuation infrastructure. Second, implement mark-to-market taxation for traded securities of taxpayers above the wealth tax threshold, ensuring that the bulk of listed equity is captured within the income tax system. Third, increase the top marginal income tax rate, tax capital gains at ordinary rates for ultra-high earners, and close the carried-interest loophole. Fourth, reform the estate tax by lowering the exemption threshold and instituting a progressive rate schedule that captures the truly massive inheritances that now escape taxation. This suite of policies would achieve much of what the authors aim for—making the superrich pay their fair share, reducing the concentration of wealth, and funding public goods—without the acute legal, administrative, and political vulnerabilities of a standalone, high-rate wealth tax.

Saez and Zucman have done democracy a service by sparking this debate. But a debate is only as good as the range of options it illuminates. A wealth tax is a powerful idea; it need not be the only one. Let’s build a tax system that is both ambitious and resilient, grounded in our constitutional traditions and capable of withstanding the counterattack that will inevitably come from those who have benefited so tremendously from decades of quiet, legalized plunder.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A radical wealth tax faces significant constitutional risks that could lead to its nullification by the Supreme Court, whereas reforms to the income tax rest on firmer legal ground.
  2. 02The IRS currently lacks the funding and infrastructure to handle the immense administrative burden of valuing complex private assets and combating the sophisticated financial engineering used to avoid wealth taxes.
  3. 03Implementing mark-to-market taxation for publicly traded securities would effectively capture the wealth of billionaires like Bezos and Buffett within the familiar framework of the income tax without the valuation difficulties of a broad wealth tax.
  4. 04Closing loopholes such as the 'step-up in basis' and taxing capital gains at death would ensure that dynastic wealth is eventually subjected to taxation without the disruptions of an annual radical wealth tax.
  5. 05A radical wealth tax that deliberately erodes its own tax base is counterproductive during times of immense fiscal need when the government requires sustainable revenue rather than symbolic degrowth of the ultra-wealthy.
