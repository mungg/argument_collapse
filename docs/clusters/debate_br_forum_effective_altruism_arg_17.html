"Peter Singer’s effective altruism relies on a narrow, utilitarian conception of 'improving the world' that wrongly dismisses the intrinsic and civic value of the arts."

Peter Singer is right that a child’s life saved from malaria is a weightier good than a child’s wish to be Batkid for a day. That much is morally clear. But the inference he draws—that funding the arts is therefore an inferior use of resources, something to be postponed until extreme poverty is eliminated—rests on a dangerously narrow conception of what counts as “improving the world.” Art, at its best, is not a luxury competing against lifesaving bed nets; it is part of what makes life worth living in the first place, and its cultivation is essential to a society’s soul. By dismissing donations to opera houses and museums as less effective, Singer treats human flourishing as if it were solely a matter of bodily survival and fails to see that a world without beauty is itself a form of deprivation.

To see why, consider what art does. It does not merely provide pleasurable experiences, though it certainly does that. Great art can deepen our understanding of the human condition, console us in grief, sharpen our moral sensitivities, and sustain a shared culture across generations. When the entertainment mogul David Geffen gave $100 million to renovate a concert hall, Singer suggests he could have done more good by directing the money to the global poor. But this comparison assumes that the two goods are neatly substitutable. A concert hall is not just a container for pleasant sounds; it is a civic space where a community enacts its commitment to something transcendent, something that belongs to no single utility function. The experience of hearing a symphony in a hall like that can be a profound good for thousands of people over decades, and it can indirectly nourish the human capacities—empathy, wonder, the ability to see things from another’s perspective—that make altruism itself possible. Reducing this to a question of “lives saved” misses the point.

Singer acknowledges that in a world without extreme poverty, promoting the arts would be a worthy goal. The concession is notable but too easy. It implies that the poor have no urgent need for beauty, culture, or meaning; they just need to stay alive. Yet anyone who has spent time in impoverished communities knows that people everywhere create art, sing, dance, and tell stories, often in the midst of terrible hardship. These activities are not residual satisfactions to be deferred until material conditions improve; they are part of what sustains hope and resilience. The effective altruist’s sequencing—first eradicate poverty, then worry about the arts—assumes a linear hierarchy of needs that does not match how human beings actually live. Even in extreme poverty, people are not merely stomachs to be filled but souls that yearn for expression and transcendence.

There is a deeper philosophical issue at stake. Singer’s framework is fundamentally utilitarian: the good is defined in terms of welfare, typically measured by the reduction of suffering and the promotion of preference satisfaction. On this view, art has only instrumental value—as a means to the end of happier individuals. But many people, including many philosophers, reject this reduction. They hold that art has intrinsic value, that a world containing the Sistine Chapel or Beethoven’s Ninth Symphony is simply a better world, even if no one’s subjective welfare were improved. If that is the case, then the choice between funding an art institution and buying bed nets is not a straightforward comparison of two quantities on a common scale. It is a choice between incommensurable goods, one that demands practical wisdom, not just a cost-benefit analysis.

None of this is to deny that extreme poverty is a moral emergency. We should give generously to effective organizations that save lives, and most of us should give far more than we do. But a truly rich ethical life also makes room for the cultivation of beauty and the support of institutions that preserve and transmit our shared cultural heritage. The danger of effective altruism is that, in its zeal to maximize welfare, it becomes Philistinic, treating everything that cannot be justified by a GiveWell spreadsheet as ethically suspect. That is a truncated vision of the good. We do not have to choose between saving children and sustaining the arts; we can, and should, do both. The most good we can do may sometimes include lighting a candle not only against the darkness of disease but against the darkness of meaninglessness.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Art is not a luxury but a fundamental component of human flourishing that deepens moral sensitivity and makes life worth living.
  2. 02The assumption that material survival must be fully secured before pursuing cultural goods ignores the fact that beauty and meaning are universal human needs, even for those in poverty.
  3. 03Reducing the value of art to instrumental welfare metrics fails to account for the intrinsic value of cultural achievements that make the world better regardless of subjective utility.
  4. 04The choice between saving lives and supporting the arts involves incommensurable goods that cannot be resolved through simple cost-benefit analysis.
  5. 05A truly rich ethical life should balance the moral emergency of extreme poverty with the preservation of cultural institutions that protect against a 'darkness of meaninglessness.'
