"While Peter Singer’s focus on global poverty is a vital starting point, a truly impartial effective altruism must expand its scope to include the massive moral claims of nonhuman animals, future generations, and existential risks."

Peter Singer’s defense of effective altruism is compelling, but perhaps not radical enough. The most familiar public face of the movement is global health: malaria nets, deworming, cash transfers, and other interventions aimed at extreme poverty. These are worthy causes, and Singer is right that they often beat sentimental philanthropy by a wide margin. Yet if the principle is truly to do the most good impartially, effective altruists must look beyond the human poor of the present moment. They must give serious attention to nonhuman animals, future generations, and low-probability catastrophes whose expected moral significance may be enormous.

Singer, of course, has long argued that animals’ suffering counts. His essay mentions this, but the discussion is brief. That brevity reflects the movement’s public caution. Saving children from malaria is easier to explain than reducing the suffering of chickens in industrial agriculture. But the numbers matter. Tens of billions of land animals are raised and killed each year in conditions that impose severe confinement, pain, fear, and deprivation. If suffering counts regardless of species, then factory farming may be one of the world’s greatest moral catastrophes.

The usual objection is that human suffering is more important. Perhaps some human experiences involve forms of self-awareness, memory, and aspiration that many animals lack. But this cannot justify ignoring the scale and intensity of animal pain. A hen in a battery cage or a pig in a gestation crate may not write poetry, but she can suffer. If a modest donation to corporate campaigns, legal reform, or meat reduction efforts can spare large numbers of animals from extreme misery, effective altruists should consider that a leading candidate for doing the most good.

This is where effective altruism’s impartiality becomes uncomfortable even for many who accept global poverty giving. People are willing to admit that African children matter as much as American children, at least in theory. They are less willing to admit that a chicken’s suffering counts for anything substantial when weighed against a human preference for cheap meat. Singer’s own philosophical work has prepared the ground for a broader moral circle. The movement should not shrink from following that reasoning.

The second frontier is the future. Climate change already threatens food systems, coastal communities, ecosystems, and political stability. Its worst effects will fall on people who contributed least to the problem and on generations that cannot vote or donate today. A framework that discounts suffering because it is distant in space should not discount suffering because it is distant in time. The child born in 2100 has no less claim to moral consideration simply because she is not yet here.

Climate change is difficult for effective altruists because the causal chain between a donation and an outcome is uncertain. But uncertainty cuts both ways. If warming contributes to droughts, displacement, disease, conflict, and biodiversity loss, then successful mitigation or adaptation could have enormous benefits. Donors may find it hard to know whether to support clean energy innovation, policy advocacy, rainforest protection, or adaptation in poor countries. That is a reason for better analysis, not for relegating climate to the margins.

The third and most controversial frontier is existential risk: events that could destroy humanity or permanently curtail its future. Nuclear war, engineered pandemics, runaway climate scenarios, and perhaps future artificial intelligence all raise questions about the survival and trajectory of civilization. Many people recoil from this topic because it seems speculative, science-fictional, or grandiose. But effective altruism asks us not to confuse emotional vividness with moral importance. The death of one identifiable child moves us more than the possible loss of millions; that bias should not govern our priorities.

Of course, expected-value reasoning can become reckless. If someone claims that a tiny probability of an astronomical future benefit justifies almost anything, alarm bells should ring. Singer is right to warn against utopian reasoning that licenses present atrocities. Effective altruists must maintain constraints, epistemic humility, and democratic accountability. But rejecting fanaticism does not mean ignoring future risk. We insure houses against unlikely fires. We should also devote serious resources to reducing risks that could kill millions or billions, especially when current funding is tiny relative to the stakes.

This broader agenda also changes how we evaluate careers. “Earning to give” is one path. But a scientist working on vaccine platforms, a policy expert improving nuclear security, an advocate reducing factory farming, or an engineer accelerating clean energy may have impacts far beyond what they could donate. Effective altruism should not be identified too strongly with high salaries and charitable transfers. Its deeper message is cause neutrality: ask where your talents and resources can reduce the most suffering or protect the most value.

Global poverty remains a powerful starting point because the evidence is strong and the need is urgent. Nobody should use speculative future benefits as an excuse to neglect children dying now. But neither should the emotional clarity of current poverty blind us to other vast moral claims. Effective altruism’s promise is that it can expand the circle of concern beyond the visible, the near, and the humanly familiar. That expansion should include animals whose suffering is hidden in farms and slaughterhouses. It should include future people whose interests are unrepresented. It should include catastrophic risks that our institutions are poorly designed to handle.

Singer’s essay invites us to do the most good we can. If we take that invitation seriously, we will not stop at bed nets, important as they are. We will ask what beings suffer most, what futures are most at risk, and what neglected actions might matter on the largest scale. The answer may unsettle donors who thought effective altruism was simply smarter charity. It is more than that. It is a demand to widen moral imagination wherever evidence and reason lead.
