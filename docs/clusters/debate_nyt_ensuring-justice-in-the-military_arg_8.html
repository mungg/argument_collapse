"The military should adopt a 'third way' reform that establishes an independent Military Special Victims Corps to handle charging decisions while maintaining the commander's ultimate accountability through a system of mandatory reviews and specialized victim advocacy."

The debate over military sexual assault too often settles into a false choice: either leave commanders in full control or strip them of all authority. There is a middle ground that respects the unique demands of military command while creating a professional, insulated prosecution capacity — a third way that can break the political logjam.

The proposal, which borrows from the successful model of special victim units in some large civilian jurisdictions, is to establish a Military Special Victims Corps. This would be a permanent, uniformed legal career track for prosecutors, investigators, and victim advocates who specialize exclusively in sexual assault and related crimes. These uniformed experts would be assigned to regional offices, independent of local unit commanders, and would have sole authority to make the initial charging decision after a thorough investigation. Crucially, however, the final disposition authority would remain with the commander — but only after the commander has received a detailed, non-binding recommendation from the special prosecutor, and only if the commander’s decision is subject to automatic, expedited review by the service secretary’s legal advisor.

This structure addresses the chief complaint — that commanders often shut down cases for non-legal reasons — while preserving the commander’s ultimate accountability for the unit. A commander who deviates from the prosecutor’s recommendation without compelling, written justification would face intense scrutiny and career implications. The built-in independent review provides an audit trail that the current system lacks. For the victim, the presence of a specialized prosecutor and dedicated victim advocate from the outset would restore a measure of confidence that their case is being handled by someone who has no personal stake in the accused’s career.

At the same time, this system avoids decoupling criminal justice from command entirely, which would create a parallel authority that undermines the commander’s ability to instill discipline day-to-day. The commander still owns the ultimate decision, forcing him or her to be directly answerable for the unit’s climate and for every decision not to prosecute. That ownership is essential because it’s the commander, not a remote prosecutor, who must create the environment where victims feel safe reporting and where potential offenders know they will be caught.

We should also mandate that every victim be assigned a Special Victim’s Counsel — a military attorney separate from the prosecution — whose sole duty is to advocate for the victim’s interests throughout the process, including the right to submit a statement to the review board if the commander rejects prosecution. These attorneys already exist in some pilot programs and have shown promise.

This third way is not a watered-down compromise. It is a structural solution that combines the independence and expertise of a specialized corps with the accountability of the chain of command. It creates a system where no single person can bury a case, yet where the ultimate responsibility for justice stays where it belongs: with the leader who commands the unit.

  2. 02Requiring commanders to provide written justification and undergo automatic legal review when deviating from a prosecutor's recommendation creates an audit trail that prevents cases from being dismissed for non-legal reasons.
  3. 03Preserving the commander's final disposition authority ensures they remain directly answerable for the unit's climate and prevents the erosion of military discipline that a parallel legal authority might cause.
  4. 04Mandating the assignment of a dedicated Special Victim’s Counsel restores victim confidence by providing them with a legal advocate whose sole duty is to protect their interests throughout the judicial process.
