"Social networks are not a passing fad but a permanent shift in the Internet's development that integrates a persistent social layer into all digital interactions."

The word “network” makes the phenomenon sound newer than it is. Human beings have always lived in networks of kinship, work, religion, neighborhood and friendship. What is new is that these networks are being mapped, archived and made portable by software. That change will not disappear, but it may become less visible.

In a few years, people may not say, “I am going to a social network,” any more than they now say, “I am going to use an electrical appliance.” Social features are spreading into phones, photo services, games, news sites, music, shopping and television. A restaurant recommendation, a campaign donation, a baby picture and a job lead can all travel through the same web of identity and trust. The “site” may matter less than the social layer underneath.

This is why the Facebook I.P.O. is both relevant and limited. It matters because Facebook is trying to become the company that owns or organizes that layer. It is limited because the broader shift does not depend on Facebook alone. If users tire of one interface, they will carry their expectations elsewhere. They will still expect to see what friends recommend, to broadcast selectively, to maintain profiles and to move through the Internet with some persistent identity.

Thomas Friedman’s caution about Egypt is also right in this frame. Social software is infrastructure, not architecture. It can connect people bringing bricks; it cannot decide what they should build. A telephone tree can summon a meeting, but it cannot make the meeting wise.

So the current excitement may fade, and some of today’s products will look dated quickly. But the social layer itself is not a fad. It is the next stage of the Internet’s ordinary development: from pages, to search, to people.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The core innovation of social media is the digital mapping and archiving of pre-existing human kinship and social structures.
  2. 02Social features are becoming an invisible, ubiquitous infrastructure integrated into all types of online services rather than remaining confined to standalone sites.
  3. 03The long-term shift toward social connectivity is independent of the success or failure of any single platform like Facebook.
  4. 04Social software functions as a neutral communication infrastructure that facilitates connection but does not dictate the outcomes or quality of human organization.
  5. 05The evolution of the Internet is moving naturally from a focus on static pages and search toward a focus on people and identity.
