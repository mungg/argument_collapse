"While Elaine Scarry correctly diagnoses the erosion of democracy caused by executive-centered defense, her radical prescription for a decentralized, citizen-only military is unrealistic and should be replaced by a model that subjects professional military power to rigorous democratic oversight."

Elaine Scarry has given us a work of genuine moral seriousness. “Citizenship in Emergency” challenges us to consider whether the very structures we have built to preserve our safety are hollowing out the democracy they are meant to protect. Her argument that the events of September 11 reveal the bankruptcy of speed-based, executive-centered defense is timely and, in its core thrust, correct. Yet her essay also illustrates the difficulty of moving from critique to prescription. Scarry’s vision of a fully democratized, nuclear-free, draft-based defense is so far removed from current political and strategic realities that it risks consigning her powerful analysis to the realm of utopian polemic. A more realistic path toward the democratic accountability she seeks is both necessary and possible—but it lies elsewhere.

I want to accept the force of Scarry’s diagnosis while resisting the sweep of her remedy. The diagnosis is, in brief, that a half-century of Cold War logic has invested the executive branch with near-dictatorial power over the most fundamental decisions of life and death, all in the name of a speed that September 11 showed to be largely mythical. The president can launch nuclear weapons without consulting anyone. The president wages wars without declarations. The president’s subordinates now propose to commandeer civilian communications networks in emergencies, preempting the very tools citizens used to organize their own defense on Flight 93. This is, as Scarry says, a slow-motion constitutional emergency.

The remedy she offers, however, is a radical decentralization of defense. She wants us to abandon nuclear weapons, return to a draft, and essentially rebuild the military on the model of the citizen-passengers of United 93: distributed, egalitarian, and operating within a “civil frame.” This is a category error. The passengers on that flight performed a singular act of resistance within a tiny, enclosed territory where they had unique access and total stakes. A nation-state faces threats ranging from intercontinental ballistic missiles to armored divisions to biological agents dispersed in a subway. No amount of civic virtue and cell-phone consultation can intercept a missile or defeat an army. These tasks require professional soldiers flying advanced aircraft, intelligence analysts parsing satellite imagery, and, yes, a chain of command that can act with dispatch when the moment demands.

The realistic lesson of 9/11 is not that we should dismantle centralized military power but that we must ensure it is firmly under democratic control and complemented by citizen resilience. This means several concrete steps short of Scarry’s maximalism. First, Congress must reclaim its constitutional authority over war and peace. The Authorization for Use of Military Force passed last September was too broad and too hasty. Any further military action, particularly against Iraq, must be preceded by a full, formal declaration of war or a tightly constrained authorization, debated openly and voted on by the people’s representatives. This alone would go far to restore the civil frame Scarry prizes.

Second, the nuclear question demands action but not unilateral disarmament. The policy of presidential first use should be revoked and replaced with a doctrine of “no first use” enshrined in law. This would not eliminate our deterrent capability—we would retain the capacity to retaliate against a nuclear attack—but it would remove the president’s unilateral ability to initiate nuclear genocide. It would also signal to the world that the United States takes its Non-Proliferation Treaty obligations seriously, strengthening our hand in demanding that others forswear these weapons. Complete abolition remains a distant goal; in the meantime, we can dramatically reduce the democratic deficit of our nuclear posture.

Third, homeland security should be built around the principle of subsidiarity. The passengers on Flight 93 succeeded because they were empowered by information and trusted to act. We should be investing in public education about emergency procedures, hardening cockpit doors, placing air marshals on flights, and ensuring that first responders have the resources they need. These are citizen-centered, distributed defenses that work with, not against, the grain of democratic life. They are not a substitute for the military, but they are a vital complement.

Scarry’s call for a draft is, I think, a distraction. The draft in a mass democracy is a blunt instrument. It did not prevent Vietnam; it made the military less professional and the home front more bitterly divided. A better way to ensure that the public bears the cost of war is radical transparency: requiring that the full human and financial costs of any proposed military operation be publicly estimated and debated, that the identities of the fallen be publicized, and that war be funded by explicit taxation rather than deficit spending. These measures would do more to “test the arguments tens of thousands of times” than forcing young people into uniform against their will.

Scarry is right that something died on September 11 beyond the nearly 3,000 innocent people murdered that day. What died was the illusion that we could permanently outsource our collective safety to a technocratic elite without losing our democratic souls. Reviving that soul does not require us to pretend that the passengers of Flight 93 are a template for the Strategic Air Command. It requires us to subject the Strategic Air Command—and the president, and the National Communications System—to the unyielding light of public deliberation. That is a fight we can win in this Congress and the next, if we have the will. Scarry’s essay is a clarion call to that fight, and for that we should be grateful. But the weapons we need are not pitchforks and boiling water; they are the Constitution, an informed citizenry, and the political courage to say no to the executive’s overreach, while still sustaining the defenses a dangerous world demands.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The passengers of Flight 93 represent a unique case of localized resistance that cannot serve as a template for defending a nation-state against complex modern threats like missiles or biological agents.
  2. 02Democratic accountability should be restored by requiring Congress to issue formal, constrained declarations of war rather than broad authorizations for executive action.
  3. 03The danger of nuclear autocracy can be mitigated by legally adopting a 'no first use' policy, which preserves deterrence while removing the president's unilateral power to initiate nuclear war.
  4. 04Homeland security should follow the principle of subsidiarity, empowering citizens through information and resources without dismantling centralized military structures.
  5. 05A military draft is an ineffective and divisive tool for democratic engagement; instead, transparency regarding the financial and human costs of war should be used to ensure public deliberation.
