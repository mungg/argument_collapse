"While offshore balancing has theoretical appeal, a sustained and predictable American presence in Asia is essential to prevent regional instability, nuclear proliferation, and a coercive Chinese hegemony."

Stephen Walt’s case for offshore balancing draws much of its appeal from Europe and the Middle East, but Asia is where the strategy faces its hardest test. If the United States reduces its role too much or plays “hard to get” too cleverly, it may produce precisely what it seeks to avoid: arms races, nuclear proliferation, unstable nationalism, and a regional order increasingly shaped by China’s rise. Asia does not require American empire, but it does require sustained American presence.

Walt recognizes that the United States should maintain significant air and naval forces in Asia and continue security partnerships with current allies. That qualification is important. Yet it sits uneasily with his broader desire to reduce forward commitments and encourage others to do more. In Asia, American alliances are not merely insurance against a future hegemon. They are the framework that makes the region’s extraordinary economic success politically possible. Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, the Philippines, Thailand, Singapore, Australia, and others have all made strategic calculations in light of American power. Remove or dilute that power too quickly, and dormant disputes could sharpen.

Start with Japan. For sixty years, the U.S.–Japan alliance has allowed Japan to remain militarily restrained while becoming an economic giant. That arrangement reassured Japan’s neighbors, many of whom retain bitter memories of Japanese imperialism. If Washington signals that Japan must provide much more of its own security, the result may not be a tidy local balance. It may be constitutional revision, expanded power-projection capabilities, and perhaps eventually a nuclear debate. Some Americans might welcome Japanese burden-sharing, but Asia’s reaction could be anxious. A heavily armed Japan facing a rising China is not inherently stabilizing.

South Korea presents a different problem. The United States has already been adjusting its force posture there, moving troops away from the demilitarized zone and rethinking old arrangements. Some change is overdue. South Korea is wealthy and democratic; it should carry more responsibility for its defense. But the North Korean threat remains acute, and the peninsula is still one of the few places where war could erupt with little warning and enormous consequences. A U.S. presence helps deter Pyongyang, reassure Seoul, and inhibit unilateral moves by either side. If South Koreans doubted American reliability, domestic pressure for an independent nuclear capability could grow, especially if North Korea’s arsenal becomes undeniable.

Taiwan is still more delicate. The United States has no formal alliance, but it has obligations under the Taiwan Relations Act and a strategic interest in peaceful resolution. China’s military modernization, missile deployments opposite Taiwan, and growing nationalism create risks. At the same time, Taiwanese moves toward formal independence could trigger crisis. American policy has worked best when it combines deterrence of Chinese coercion with restraint of Taiwanese adventurism. That requires active engagement, not offshore ambiguity alone. If China believes America is withdrawing, it may be tempted to press harder. If Taiwan believes America’s support is unconditional, it may overreach. Maintaining balance requires constant diplomacy backed by credible force.

China is the central question. Walt sensibly avoids declaring China an inevitable enemy. A policy that assumes a coming clash could help create one. China has benefited enormously from the open international economy, and its leaders have many reasons to avoid military confrontation. The United States should welcome China’s peaceful rise, integrate it into institutions, and cooperate on trade, health, energy, North Korea, and counterterrorism where possible. But it would be naïve to ignore the implications of sustained Chinese growth. A richer China will have greater military capabilities and a stronger desire to shape its neighborhood. Even if its intentions are defensive, its neighbors may not see them that way.

The purpose of American presence in Asia should therefore be neither containment in the Cold War sense nor withdrawal in the realist sense. It should be reassurance and hedging. Reassure China that the United States does not seek to encircle or humiliate it; reassure allies that they need not accommodate China out of fear; hedge against the possibility that China becomes coercive. This is a subtle strategy, and it depends on credibility. Credibility, in turn, depends not only on the ability to surge forces from Hawaii or California but on the habits of cooperation built through bases, exercises, intelligence sharing, and diplomatic consultation.

Offshore balancing often assumes that regional powers will naturally balance against a local hegemon. In Asia, that assumption is only partly true. Many states fear China’s dominance, but they also depend on China economically and do not want to choose openly between Beijing and Washington. Southeast Asian governments may welcome U.S. naval presence while avoiding formal alignment. India may balance China in some respects while guarding its strategic autonomy. Russia may sell arms to China while fearing Chinese influence in Siberia. Local balancing will be uneven, hesitant, and constrained by history. The United States cannot simply wait offshore and expect a coalition to assemble itself.

Nor should the United States underestimate nontraditional threats in Asia. Terrorist networks in Southeast Asia, piracy, proliferation, pandemics, and natural disasters all require cooperation. The recent Indian Ocean tsunami has demonstrated both the vulnerability of the region and the value of rapid American military logistics. U.S. naval and air capabilities delivered aid at a scale few others could match. Such missions generate goodwill and show that military presence can serve human purposes. They also build relationships useful in crises. An offshore posture too narrowly focused on great-power balancing would miss these benefits.

Walt is right, however, that American power must be disciplined. The Iraq war has damaged U.S. standing in Asia too. Many Asians admire American dynamism but distrust ideological crusades. Muslim populations in Indonesia and Malaysia watch U.S. actions in the Middle East closely. Abu Ghraib, Guantanamo, and civilian casualties undercut America’s claim to moral leadership. A sustainable Asia strategy must therefore combine presence with restraint. Bases should be negotiated respectfully; status-of-forces issues should be handled sensitively; counterterrorism should not become a license for supporting abuses by local security services.

The United States should also avoid forcing Asian countries into a premature anti-China bloc. Nothing would more quickly validate Chinese hard-liners than an American campaign to contain China before it has chosen a hostile course. The better approach is open architecture: strengthen alliances, deepen ties with India and Southeast Asia, maintain naval superiority, but keep institutions inclusive where possible. Encourage China to become a responsible stakeholder in existing rules, while making clear that coercion will be resisted. This requires patience and confidence, not alarmism.

Walt’s critique of “global hegemony” is valuable because Asia would react badly to American unilateralism masquerading as leadership. But his enthusiasm for playing hard-to-get could be dangerous. Allies are not supplicants to be manipulated; they are partners whose domestic politics matter. If Washington treats reassurance as a game of scarcity, it may provoke hedging against the United States as well as against China. The goal should be to make American power predictable, not elusive.

Asia’s peace has rested on a paradox: American primacy has allowed regional states to avoid fully militarized rivalry. That order must adapt to China’s rise and to the growing capabilities of America’s allies. Burden-sharing should increase. Diplomacy should be multilateralized. But a substantial U.S. role remains essential. Offshore balancing, if interpreted as lean naval engagement plus robust alliances, may be workable. If interpreted as strategic distance and conditional reliability, it is a gamble Asia cannot afford.
