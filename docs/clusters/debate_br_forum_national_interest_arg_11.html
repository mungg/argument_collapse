"While offshore balancing offers a necessary corrective to the failures of global hegemony, it is morally and strategically insufficient because it relegates the prevention of mass atrocities to a peripheral concern."

Stephen Walt writes as a realist, and realists perform a useful service in American debate: they ask what matters most, what power can accomplish, and what costs we are willing to bear. After the Iraq debacle, those questions are indispensable. Yet Walt’s essay also reveals the moral poverty of realism when it is elevated from a cautionary disposition into a grand strategy. Offshore balancing may preserve American primacy. It may reduce some anti-American resentment. But it gives too little weight to people whose suffering does not immediately threaten concentrations of power, oil, or wealth.

Walt says offshore balancing does not preclude humanitarian intervention, and he grants that the United States may use force to halt genocide or mass murder when it can do so at acceptable cost. That caveat is welcome, but it is too thin to bear the moral burden. The twentieth century has taught, repeatedly, that governments can murder vast numbers of their own citizens without threatening regional balances in ways that trigger classic strategic alarms. Armenians, Jews, Cambodians, Bosnians, Rwandans, and now the people of Darfur do not fit neatly into a map of vital interests. If American policy is guided primarily by offshore balancing, the danger is that mass killing will always appear peripheral until it is too late.

Rwanda should haunt any discussion of restraint. In 1994, the United States did not rush headlong into a foolish war. It did almost nothing. The Clinton administration, scarred by Somalia, avoided even using the word genocide while hundreds of thousands were slaughtered in a matter of weeks. No hostile great power was about to dominate central Africa. No vital oil supply was at stake. The result was not prudence but shame. Walt mentions Rwanda as something President Clinton should have prevented, but his own strategic framework does not explain why a future president would treat a similar case as important enough to act.

Bosnia and Kosovo point in a different direction. In Bosnia, years of European hesitation and American reluctance produced camps, ethnic cleansing, and massacres such as Srebrenica. When NATO finally acted and the United States pressed diplomacy at Dayton, the killing stopped. Kosovo was legally controversial, especially without explicit Security Council authorization, but it prevented the likely expansion of ethnic cleansing by Slobodan Milosevic’s forces. These were not wars for empire. They were imperfect but necessary uses of power to defend human beings when diplomacy had failed.

Realists often warn that humanitarian intervention can become a cover for imperial ambition. They are right. Iraq was sold partly in humanitarian language once the weapons case began to weaken, and no one should confuse regime change by invasion with a general doctrine of rescue. But the abuse of humanitarian rhetoric does not invalidate humanitarian responsibility. The answer to Iraq is not to say that America should intervene only where vital interests are at stake. It is to develop stricter criteria: large-scale killing or imminent threat of it; exhaustion of plausible nonmilitary measures; reasonable chance of improving conditions; multilateral support when possible; and a serious plan for protection after intervention. Such criteria would rule out many adventures. They would not rule out all action.

Walt’s emphasis on legitimacy could support this broader view, but he deploys legitimacy mainly as a way to preserve American influence. That is too instrumental. Legitimacy is also about law, rights, and the claims of vulnerable people. If the United States wants others to accept its power, it should show not only restraint but solidarity. A world in which the strongest democracy stands aside during mass slaughter will not be made legitimate by the fact that it balances offshore.

Darfur is the immediate test. Since 2003, Sudan’s government and allied Janjaweed militias have burned villages, killed civilians, raped women, and driven huge numbers of people from their homes. The Bush administration, to its credit, has called the atrocities genocide. But naming the crime has not stopped it. The African Union has deployed monitors, but they are too few and too weak. A serious response would include stronger sanctions, an arms embargo with enforcement, pressure on Khartoum’s patrons, logistical support for African forces, a no-fly zone if feasible, and readiness to protect civilians. This need not mean a massive American ground occupation. It does mean treating African lives as more than a test of whether a hostile hegemon is emerging.

Walt might reply that Iraq has exhausted American capacity and that offshore balancing would make humanitarian action easier by avoiding unnecessary wars. That is an important point. Iraq has consumed troops, money, attention, and diplomatic capital that might have been used elsewhere. A less militarized Middle East policy would leave the United States better able to respond to genuine emergencies. But that only strengthens the case for building humanitarian protection into grand strategy, not leaving it as an afterthought. If we are to husband power, we should ask: for what? Surely one answer is to prevent the worst crimes human beings commit against one another.

The problem is not only military. Humanitarian crises require institutions, early warning, diplomacy, peacekeeping capacity, refugee assistance, and post-conflict reconstruction. The United States has invested far too little in these tools. Walt rightly criticizes the starvation of the international-affairs budget and the neglect of language and area expertise. A humane foreign policy would reverse that neglect. It would strengthen the United Nations’ capacity to deploy peacekeepers quickly, support regional organizations, create civilian reconstruction reserves, and fund international justice mechanisms. Such measures are not sentimental. They are practical ways to prevent crises from requiring large American interventions later.

Walt’s realism is most convincing when he criticizes preventive war. The invasion of Iraq has made humanitarian intervention harder by associating moral language with unilateral force and occupation. Around the world, governments now suspect that talk of human rights is a pretext for regime change. That is a tragedy for those who genuinely need protection. Rebuilding trust will require precisely the forbearance Walt recommends. But it will also require demonstrating that multilateral action can defend civilians without becoming imperial rule.

There is also a strategic reason to care about humanitarian catastrophes. Mass violence destabilizes regions, produces refugees, spreads disease, fuels criminal networks, and creates grievances that armed movements exploit. Afghanistan’s collapse became a security threat not because anyone thought it was a vital region in the traditional sense, but because misery and state failure created space for violent networks. A rigid distinction between moral interests and national interests is often false. Preventing atrocities is both right and prudent.

Still, humanitarian advocates must learn from realists. Not every evil can be stopped by American power. Good intentions do not guarantee good outcomes. Occupation can inflame nationalism. Military force can kill the people it is meant to save. Domestic support is finite. The United States must act with allies whenever possible and must be honest about costs. A doctrine of rescue that ignores limits will collapse into disillusionment.

The better grand strategy would combine Walt’s restraint with a stronger commitment to human security. Reject preventive war. Reduce unnecessary military footprints. End blank checks to allies. Rebuild alliances and international law. But also declare that genocide and mass ethnic cleansing are not peripheral simply because they occur in poor places. The United States should not be the world’s policeman, but neither should it become the world’s well-armed bystander.

A great power’s maturity is measured not only by its ability to avoid foolish wars. It is measured by its willingness to act, carefully and collectively, when inaction would make it complicit in preventable slaughter. Walt has shown how American primacy can be squandered. We must also ask how it can be justified.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A grand strategy focused strictly on vital economic and power centers fails to account for the fact that governments can commit genocide without threatening regional balances of power.
  2. 02Historical failures like Rwanda demonstrate that a policy of strategic restraint without a clear humanitarian mandate leads to moral shame and preventable slaughter.
  3. 03Legitimacy in international affairs should be based on solidarity with vulnerable populations and the defense of human rights, not just the instrumental preservation of American influence.
  4. 04Humanitarian catastrophes create practical security threats, such as regional instability and the rise of violent networks, making the distinction between moral and national interests false.
  5. 05The United States should adopt a 'human security' framework that combines military restraint with a commitment to multilateral action and the strengthening of international peacekeeping institutions.
  6. 06The abuse of humanitarian rhetoric to justify the Iraq War should not invalidate the genuine responsibility to intervene in cases of large-scale killing when strict criteria for intervention are met.
