Diversified Gemini

"While Martin Gilens correctly identifies that the affluent have disproportionate political influence, his analysis understates the current crisis because it fails to account for the catastrophic concentration of power within the top 1 percent following the Citizens United decision."

Martin Gilens presents a chilling portrait of American democracy, one in which the voices of the poor and the middle class are systematically drowned out by the preferences of the affluent. His meticulous research confirming that our government is vastly more responsive to those at the top of the income distribution is a vital contribution to political science. However, by relying on data that ends in 2006, Gilens’s analysis, as grim as it is, actually understates the magnitude of the crisis we face today in July 2012. We are no longer living in the political ecosystem of the 1980s or 1990s. We are living in the post-*Citizens United* era, and the implications for representational equality are catastrophic.

Gilens uses the 90th income percentile to represent the "affluent." In 2012, a household at the 90th percentile makes roughly $140,000 a year. These are successful professionals—doctors, lawyers, small business owners. They may have more disposable income to donate to a preferred candidate than a working-class family, but they are not the ones buying our elections. The Occupy Wall Street movement over the past year correctly identified the real fault line in American economic and political power: the 99 percent versus the 1 percent, and more accurately, the top 0.1 percent.

Since the Supreme Court’s disastrous *Citizens United* decision in 2010, the campaign finance landscape has been fundamentally rewired. We are currently witnessing the first presidential election dominated by Super PACs, organizations that can raise and spend unlimited sums of corporate and private wealth. A handful of billionaires—figures like Sheldon Adelson on the right and George Soros on the left—are now capable of single-handedly keeping presidential campaigns afloat and dictating the legislative agendas of entire political parties. Gilens notes that campaign donations are the most likely mechanism for the representational inequality he observes. If that was true when campaign contributions were strictly capped and soft money was regulated, what happens when billionaires can drop ten million dollars into a Super PAC with total anonymity?

Because he underestimates the concentration of power at the very apex of the wealth distribution, Gilens is overly optimistic about his proposed solution: partisan competition. He suggests that when elections are closely contested and control of government is up for grabs, policymakers broaden their appeal and become more responsive to the middle class and the poor.

But this theory assumes that politicians competing in tight races will turn to the broad electorate for support. In the current arms race of campaign spending, a highly competitive election actually makes politicians *more* dependent on the ultra-wealthy. When a candidate needs to raise $50 million to survive a bruising Senate race, they do not spend their time courting the median voter; they spend their time in the living rooms of hedge fund managers and fossil fuel executives. Partisan gridlock and close elections no longer force politicians to the center; they force politicians to dial for dollars.

We can see this dynamic playing out in the halls of Congress right now. The public overwhelmingly supports allowing the Bush tax cuts for the wealthiest Americans to expire, yet the Democratic Party is deeply fractured on the issue, terrified of alienating elite donors. The public wants stricter regulations on Wall Street to prevent another 2008 financial collapse, yet the Dodd-Frank reforms are being systematically watered down by lobbyists funded by the very banks that were bailed out. In these instances, partisan competition has done nothing to save the middle class.

Gilens is right to say that democracy requires all citizens to have influence over the policies their government adopts. But his hope that nonpartisan districting or a slight uptick in partisan rivalry will correct the ship is a dangerous illusion. Until we confront the structural rot of a privately financed electoral system—until we overturn *Citizens United* and move toward publicly funded elections—the government will continue to be a subsidiary of the highest bidder, and the voices of the poor and middle class will remain nothing more than a statistical rounding error in the eyes of Washington.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The real divide in political power is not between the 90th percentile and the poor, but between the ultra-wealthy 1 percent and the rest of the population.
  2. 02The rise of Super PACs and unlimited corporate spending has fundamentally rewired the campaign finance landscape, allowing a few billionaires to dictate legislative agendas.
  3. 03Contrary to Gilens's theory, intense partisan competition now increases dependency on elite donors rather than forcing politicians to court the median voter.
  4. 04Current policy failures, such as the inability to end tax cuts for the wealthy or implement strict Wall Street regulations, demonstrate that partisan rivalry is failing to protect middle-class interests.
  5. 05Meaningful representational equality cannot be achieved through minor reforms like nonpartisan districting but requires a total overhaul of the privately financed electoral system.
