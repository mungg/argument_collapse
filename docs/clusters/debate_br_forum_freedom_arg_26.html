"Aziz Rana’s proposal to reclaim freedom is incomplete because it fails to account for the gendered architecture of American liberty, which has historically relied on the legal, economic, and sexual subordination of women."

Aziz Rana’s sweeping historical argument about American freedom is a crucial intervention in contemporary political discourse, yet it suffers from a silence that borders on distortion. For all his attention to the way settler colonialism linked freedom to land and racial exclusion, he says almost nothing about the gendered architecture of that freedom. The experience of independence, self-mastery, and control that white settlers valorized was, from the beginning, a thoroughly masculine prerogative, built not only on the expropriation of Indigenous peoples and the exploitation of enslaved Black labor, but also on the legal, economic, and sexual subordination of women. An analysis that neglects this dimension can only produce a partial account of what freedom has meant in the United States—and, consequently, a partial path to reclaiming it.

Rana’s starting point—that early American freedom was a robust experience of self-rule encompassing economic independence and political participation—needs to be interrogated through a feminist lens. Whose self-rule? The yeoman farmer, artisan, or male laborer could aspire to be master of his own destiny, but his mastery was also enacted over a household in which wife and children were legally his dependents. The common law of coverture erased the married woman’s legal personality, subsuming it into that of her husband. Her labor, her body, and her property were not her own. For her, the household was not a site of independence from the state and market but a site of intimate governance, often backed by the right of chastisement and the near-impossibility of divorce. The “economic independence” that Rana highlights as the hallmark of settler freedom was, for white women, largely a myth; they were dependent on male wage-earning fathers, husbands, or brothers. When they did work outside the home, it was in conditions of severe legal restriction and economic exploitation. The zero-sum, competitive freedom of white men was thus structured not only by a racial outside but also by a gendered inside: the white family was a dominion as much as a haven.

This gendered division of freedom did not quietly disappear as the frontier closed. It was reconfigured through the rise of the family wage, the New Deal’s two-tiered welfare state, and the postwar middle-class order that Rana acknowledges as the mid-twentieth-century “bargain.” The ideal of the male breadwinner who enjoyed corporate benefits, a pension, and healthcare through his employer was the apotheosis of a certain kind of white masculine freedom. But it depended on the unpaid or low-paid care work of women, and it rendered women vulnerable to domestic violence, economic marginalization, and a political system that treated them as secondary. When Rana quotes the son of a steelworker celebrating liberation in the 1950s, we must ask: liberation for whom? For the steelworker’s wife, whose destiny was circumscribed by the kitchen, the nursery, and the expectation of cheerful subordination, that era felt less like liberation and more like a gilded cage.

The right’s contemporary appropriation of freedom is not just a matter of market fundamentalism and white nationalism; it is also a deeply gendered project. The “Freedom Caucus” does not merely oppose government social programs; it opposes reproductive freedom, seeking to compel pregnancy and childbirth as the price of female sexuality. The individualism celebrated by the right is often an antistatist veneer over a patriarchal vision in which the natural order of the family—with male authority at its head—must be shielded from the reach of the state, even as that state is deployed to police women’s bodies. Rana’s framework, for all its critique of exclusion, does not give this reality the centrality it deserves.

Conversely, the most vital left invocations of freedom in recent years have been driven precisely by the struggle against patriarchal control. The movement for reproductive justice, which Rana mentions only in passing, has insisted that true freedom for women requires not merely the negative right to an abortion, but the positive capacity to raise children in safety and dignity. The #MeToo movement reframed the workplace not just as a space of class conflict but as a site of sexual coercion, linking economic precarity to vulnerability to harassment and assault. These movements enact a freedom politics that is at once deeply personal and structurally transformative, and they have succeeded in ways that labor and anti-imperial politics have not in shifting mainstream conversations about what it means to be free. Their relative absence from Rana’s analysis is a missed opportunity, because they demonstrate that institution-building need not wait for a far-off post-neoliberal future; it is already happening in the networks of care, mutual aid, and legal advocacy that these movements have built.

A freedom project that takes gender seriously would have to revise some of Rana’s prescriptions. The workplace democracy he champions remains essential, but it must be paired with a reorganization of care work—through universal childcare, paid family leave, and wage models that value the reproductive labor that sustains all other labor. Immigration reform, too, must account for the ways in which migrant women experience distinct forms of exploitation, from domestic servitude to sexual violence, and ensure that decriminalization does not simply mainstream a low-wage female workforce into the casualized margins. And the anti-imperial dimension must confront the reality that U.S. hegemony has often exported gendered violence abroad, from the toleration of abusive regimes to the direct military targeting of women’s bodies as a tactic of war.

The arc of Rana’s argument—that freedom has been captured by a zero-sum, exclusionary logic and that the left needs to build institutions that make solidarity tangible—is sound. But the silence on gender suggests a lingering blindness to the way domination has always been structured within the very spaces the left seeks to democratize. Reclaiming freedom means not only dismantling the settler-imperial edifice but also tearing down the patriarchal walls that have enclosed the household, the bedroom, and the body. Until the left integrates this insight thoroughly into its vision, its freedom talk will continue to speak past the lived experience of half the population.
