"The purpose of Lent is to use intentional deprivation and spiritual discipline to reveal our dependencies and create space for personal transformation and service to others."

The purpose of Lent is not deprivation for its own sake, nor a religiously decorated version of a New Year’s resolution. At its best, Lent is a season for telling the truth about desire: what governs us, what comforts us, what distracts us, and what we would rather not face.

That is why giving something up can matter. A fast from meat, alcohol, sweets, shopping, Facebook or television is not morally impressive by itself. One can give up chocolate and become only more vain, more irritable or more self-satisfied. But a chosen absence can reveal dependence. It interrupts habit long enough to ask whether the thing I call “mine” has quietly become my master. The point is not to prove willpower, but to make room for repentance, prayer and mercy.

In Christian terms, Lent looks toward Easter by way of the wilderness and the cross. It recalls Jesus’ 40 days of temptation, but also Israel’s long schooling in dependence. The traditional disciplines — fasting, almsgiving and prayer — belong together. Fasting without generosity becomes self-improvement. Prayer without sacrifice can become sentiment. Almsgiving without conversion can become philanthropy at a safe distance. Lent joins them so that the believer’s body, time and money are all implicated.

There is also a public value here, even for those outside the church. A consumer culture trains people to treat every appetite as urgent and every inconvenience as an injustice. Lent insists that limits can be chosen, not merely suffered; that freedom is not identical with having options; that some hungers should be educated rather than indulged.

Still, Lent should not be romanticized. For the poor, the sick or the overburdened, “giving something up” may be the daily condition, not a spiritual exercise. The season’s deeper question is therefore not “What have you quit?” but “What are you being freed for?” If the answer is greater humility, clearer attention and more love of neighbor, then Lent has served its purpose.

"The purpose of Lent is to undergo a process of spiritual conversion and self-transcendence rather than mere self-improvement or optimization."

Lent began as a baptismal preparation in the early church, a forty-day catechumenate culminating at the Easter Vigil. Only later did it become a general season of penance for the whole community. Knowing that history matters, because it tells us what the practice is actually for: not self-improvement, but conversion. The point is to be turned, gradually and uncomfortably, toward something larger than oneself.

This is why the modern habit of giving up chocolate or Facebook, while harmless, misses what the tradition is doing. Fasting, almsgiving, and prayer—the classical triad—are meant to work together. You deny yourself something small so that you notice how attached you are to it; you give the money or time you save to someone who needs it; and you pray, because without prayer the whole exercise collapses into a diet or a productivity hack. The discomfort is diagnostic. It reveals the disordered loves you didn't know you had.

For nonbelievers, I think there is still something to learn here, though it requires honesty about what's being borrowed. A secular Lent can cultivate genuine discipline and gratitude. But stripped of its theological anchor—the conviction that we are being prepared for a particular death and a particular resurrection—it tends to become another form of self-optimization, which is the opposite of what penance is.

So yes, there is a point in giving something up. The point is to discover, for forty days, that you are not what you consume.

Lent’s purpose is preparation. For Christians, the 40 days mirror Jesus’ wilderness fast—a time of testing and prayer. Giving something up, whether food or a habit, becomes a physical reminder of dependence on God and a small participation in Christ’s sacrifice. This self-denial trains the soul to resist temptation and opens space for prayer and service. Historically, the season has been a collective journey of penance, aligning the faithful with the story of salvation leading to Easter.

But the ritual holds meaning beyond church walls. In a consumer culture that promotes constant indulgence, voluntary renunciation becomes a countercultural act of self-mastery. Behavioral research confirms that self-control operates like a muscle; periodic fasting from a chosen pleasure—caffeine, television, online shopping—can strengthen overall willpower. For the nonbeliever, Lent offers a structured framework for mindfulness, an annual opportunity to disrupt unconscious habits and reassess what is truly necessary.

The practice also cultivates empathy and community. Traditional Lenten disciplines tied fasting to almsgiving, so that what was saved through self-denial could be shared with those in need. Even today, giving up something reveals our hidden dependencies and can spark gratitude. When undertaken in a group—whether a church congregation or a circle of friends—the shared fast creates accountability and a sense of common purpose, turning personal sacrifice into a collective journey.

Thus, the point is not deprivation for its own sake. Giving something up creates space—for reflection, for recalibrating priorities, for attending to what matters. Whether the goal is closeness to God or a clearer sense of self, Lent’s purpose is to add depth and intentionality to life—a kind of spiritual spring cleaning. In a distracted age, that is a gift anyone can receive.

For nonbelievers, Lent offers something similar without theological commitments. It provides a structured framework for intentional change—proving that human beings don't need divine commands to recognize that desire, habit, and self-discipline shape the kind of people we become.

"The true purpose of Lent is a theological discipline of repentance and preparation for the resurrection, rather than a secular exercise in self-improvement."

Lent is, first and last, a theological discipline, and we lose something important when we try to repackage it as a generic self-improvement regimen. The forty days are not arbitrary. They echo Israel's forty years in the wilderness, Moses on Sinai, Elijah's journey to Horeb, and most directly Christ's forty days of fasting before his ministry. To observe Lent is to enter, liturgically, into that wilderness — to confront one's own appetites, distractions and idols in preparation for the Paschal mystery of Good Friday and Easter.

Strip out the resurrection and the practice loses its shape. Giving up chocolate or Facebook because it might make you a marginally healthier or more focused person is fine, but it isn't Lent; it's a New Year's resolution with a Catholic accent. The Christian point of fasting is not to feel virtuous, nor even to build character, but to create space — hunger, silence, inconvenience — in which prayer and repentance can do their slow work. The traditional triad has always been prayer, fasting and almsgiving, and the last is telling: the money saved from the steak or the wine is supposed to go to the poor. The discipline is oriented outward and upward, not inward.

That said, I don't begrudge nonbelievers who borrow the season. There is real wisdom in any cultural practice that asks affluent people to deny themselves something, however small, on a fixed schedule. In a society that treats every craving as a right to be satisfied immediately, even a secularized Lent registers a useful dissent. But Christians should resist the temptation to sell the season on those terms. Lent's purpose is not to make us better versions of ourselves; it is to remind us that we are dust, that we are mortal, and that we need a Savior. Everything else — the discipline, the simplicity, the small daily renunciations — flows from that.

The danger in talking about Lent as “giving something up” is that it can shrink a demanding religious season into a boutique exercise in self-denial. In affluent settings, Lent often sounds like this: no lattes, no chocolate, no wine, no Facebook. There may be nothing wrong with such sacrifices, but they can also become a way to feel virtuous without changing much of anything.

The biblical tradition behind Lent is more severe. Fasting is repeatedly judged by whether it leads to justice. The prophet Isaiah mocks those who fast while exploiting workers and ignoring the hungry. Jesus warns against public displays of piety. The early church connected fasting to care for the poor: eat less so that someone else may eat. If Lent does not loosen one’s grip on money, status and resentment, then giving up a luxury may be little more than spiritual theater.

That critique is especially necessary in 2013, after years of economic anxiety, foreclosures, unemployment and widening inequality. For many people, “giving something up” is not a seasonal discipline; it is daily life. They have given up vacations, medical care, retirement security or decent housing because they had no choice. When the comfortable temporarily renounce cupcakes, they should at least ask how voluntary deprivation relates to involuntary deprivation all around them.

This does not mean Lent is useless. On the contrary, it can be powerful if it becomes less narcissistic. Give up restaurant meals and donate the savings to a food pantry. Give up an hour of entertainment and spend it visiting someone isolated. Give up the need to be right and reconcile with an enemy. Give up indifference to public policy that punishes the poor while flattering the successful.

The purpose of Lent should not be to admire one’s capacity for discipline. It should be conversion — a change in direction. That includes private repentance, but it cannot stop there. A fast that leaves one’s neighbor untouched is incomplete.

So yes, there is a point in giving something up. But the test is not whether we suffer a little. The test is whether someone else is loved more concretely because we did.

"Modern Lenten practices have largely devolved into a superficial, self-serving form of dieting and virtue signaling that fails to achieve the season's true spiritual and communal purpose."

I'll be the skeptic. Lent, as widely practiced, has become a slightly pious form of dieting, and I'm not sure either God or the dieter is well served by it.

Consider what most people actually give up: chocolate, alcohol, Facebook, carbs. These are precisely the things that twenty-first-century, affluent Westerners feel vaguely guilty about anyway. Lent functions, in practice, as a religiously branded permission slip to do what we already wanted to do — eat less sugar, drink less, spend less time online — with the added benefit of feeling spiritually serious about it. The Gospel reading for Ash Wednesday, ironically, is Jesus warning his followers not to disfigure their faces so that people will know they are fasting. Yet smudged foreheads and humblebrag tweets about Lenten sacrifices are now part of the seasonal furniture.

There is also a class dimension worth noting. Giving things up is a luxury available to people who have plenty. For a single mother working two jobs in Detroit, "giving up Starbucks for Lent" is not a meaningful spiritual discipline; it's a description of her ordinary Tuesday. The historic point of fasting was supposed to create solidarity with the poor and free up resources to share with them. How much of contemporary Lenten observance does that, honestly?

None of this is an argument against the season itself. The older, harder version of Lent — real fasting, serious almsgiving, sustained prayer, sacramental confession, attention to one's complicity in injustice — remains a powerful spiritual technology. But that's not what most of us are doing. If Lent is to mean anything in 2013, it should make us uncomfortable, not merely virtuous. It should redirect resources to people who need them, not just trim our waistlines. Otherwise it is, as the prophets kept saying about Israel's fasts, a sound and a fury signifying not much.

Diversified Claude

"The purpose of Lent is to provide a collective, time-bound, and embodied structure for discipline that satisfies a deep human need for shared ritual and restraint."

What I find most interesting about Lent is how stubbornly it has survived in a culture that has supposedly outgrown such things. Most of the old liturgical calendar has faded from public consciousness — Ember Days, Rogation, Septuagesima — but Lent persists, and not only among the devout. Why?

My hypothesis is that Lent answers a need that secular modernity has not figured out how to meet on its own: the need for collective, time-bound, embodied discipline. We are very good at telling individuals to improve themselves, but very bad at offering them a shared framework in which to do it. Lent provides what the self-help industry cannot: a fixed start (Ash Wednesday), a fixed end (Easter), a community of people doing it with you, and a set of practices handed down rather than invented. Émile Durkheim would have recognized exactly what was going on.

This is why I think the question "is there a point in giving something up?" slightly misses the mark. The point is not really the thing given up. The point is the structure — the fact that on a specific Wednesday, millions of people simultaneously agree to take on a small, deliberate constraint, and that on a specific Sunday they will collectively release it. That rhythm of restraint and feast, lack and abundance, is something older than Christianity and probably older than agriculture. Christianity baptized it; secular culture has nothing comparable.

For believers, of course, the structure carries the substance: Lent prepares the heart for the death and resurrection of Christ. But even for the nonbeliever who participates, the form does its quiet work. You learn that your wants are not your needs. You learn that you can wait. You learn that there are seasons. In a culture organized around the instantaneous and the individual, this may be the most countercultural thing Lent has left to offer.

"The purpose of Lent is to provide a communal and spiritual rhythm that allows individuals to examine their desires, face their mortality, and open up space for healing and renewal."

Lent’s purpose is to place human longing inside a story and a calendar. That may sound abstract, but it is precisely what many people lack. We live by deadlines, school terms, market cycles and election seasons; these calendars train us to value productivity, consumption and competition. Lent offers a different rhythm: preparation, examination, restraint, sorrow, hope.

For Christians, that rhythm leads toward Easter. It is not merely a memorial of Jesus’ 40 days in the wilderness, but participation in a drama of temptation, suffering and renewal. The point is not to pretend to be miserable. It is to become more truthful. What do I love? What do I fear? What am I avoiding? What must die in me if I am to live more fully?

The practice of giving something up is one answer to those questions, but it is not the only one. Some people should not fast from food, particularly those with medical conditions or histories of disordered eating. Some already live with scarcity and need not romanticize it. For them, Lent may mean taking something on: daily prayer, Sabbath rest, therapy, acts of mercy, honest conversation, political solidarity, gratitude. Discipline should serve healing, not pride.

At its best, Lent is communal rather than heroic. It tells the individual, “You are not inventing the spiritual life from scratch.” The ashes of Ash Wednesday, the simpler meals, the subdued music, the anticipation of Holy Week — these shared signs help people face mortality without despair. “Remember that you are dust” is not an insult. It is a liberation from the fantasy that we are self-made and permanent.

For nonbelievers, the season can still have meaning as an inherited cultural form of attention. One need not accept every doctrine to see the wisdom of a yearly audit of desire. But the Christian center should not be erased: Lent is finally about learning to receive life, not mastering it.

The point of giving something up, then, is not the thing surrendered. It is the space opened. If that space fills with humility, compassion and hope, Lent has done its work.

"The purpose of Lent is a communal preparation for the joy of Easter through disciplined practices that symbolize spiritual rebirth."

The most misleading question about Lent may be “What are you giving up?” That makes the season sound like a private menu of deprivations: one person quits Facebook, another meat, another gossip. Historically, though, Lent is less an individual challenge than a communal journey. The church changes its calendar, its music, its prayers and its expectations so that Easter is not simply a date but a destination.

In the ancient church, the weeks before Easter were a final preparation for baptism and a time when notorious sinners were restored to communion. Everyone else joined the discipline because no one comes to Easter alone. That history matters. Lent is not merely about improving the already baptized; it is about remembering what baptism means: dying and rising, leaving an old life and receiving a new one.

Giving something up has a point when it makes that pattern bodily. Human beings do not think only with our minds. We learn through meals, schedules, gestures and repeated words. A fast teaches by making absence tangible. It can make prayer less abstract. It can make gratitude for ordinary bread more vivid. It can remind prosperous people that abundance is not a right and that appetite is not a king.

But Lent is distorted when each person invents a custom-made spirituality detached from worship and community. A self-selected fast can easily become another consumer product, chosen because it suits my personality and goals. The older wisdom is that Christians submit to a season they did not create. They let the church say, for a while, “less,” so that the great feast can say “more” with force.

The purpose of Lent, then, is preparation for joy. That sounds odd because we associate joy with spontaneity, not discipline. Yet anyone who has rehearsed music, trained for a race or kept vigil at a bedside knows that deep joy often has a long approach. Lent is the church’s long approach to Easter: not gloom for its own sake, but the clearing of a path.
