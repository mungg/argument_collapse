Diversified MiniMax

"Michael Sandel’s 'corruption' argument against market expansion is philosophically insufficient because it relies on a contested metaphysics of goods that cannot be easily adjudicated in a pluralistic democracy."

Sandel's argument turns on a distinction that is philosophically weaker than it first appears: the distinction between goods that are "corrupted" by market exchange and those that are not. The difficulty is that "corruption" is doing all the argumentative work, but Sandel never adequately explains what it means.

Consider the kidney example that opens his analysis. Sandel acknowledges that a purchased kidney "will work (assuming a good match) regardless of the monetary payment." So the objection to organ markets cannot be about the kidney itself. It must be about something else—perhaps the relational context in which the exchange occurs, or the attitudes toward the body that commodification encourages. But if that's the objection, then it's not clear why kidneys are categorically different from other goods that Sandel doesn't discuss. Doesn't the purchase of an expensive automobile or a luxury vacation also express and reinforce attitudes about status, success, and the meaning of life that we might want to question?

The deeper problem is that Sandel's corruption argument rests on a contested metaphysics of goods. He assumes that certain goods have "constitutive ideals" or essential natures that are damaged by commodification. But this assumption is increasingly difficult to defend in a pluralistic society. Different people, with different comprehensive doctrines, will have different views about which goods are "diminished" by market exchange and which are not. Catholics may object to surrogacy contracts; secular liberals may not. Communitarians may worry about the marketization of friendship; libertarians may see no problem. How are we to adjudicate these disputes?

Sandel's answer seems to be that we should engage in "moral inquiry" and determine "which arguments are more persuasive." But this sidesteps the difficulty. In a democracy, we cannot resolve deep moral disagreements by appealing to philosophical arguments that not everyone accepts. We need procedures for making collective decisions in the face of reasonable pluralism. Sandel gestures toward this when he says that the question of market boundaries is "about how we want to live together," but he doesn't tell us how we are to answer this question together when we disagree profoundly about the good life.

One possible response is that democracies should remain neutral among competing conceptions of the good, limiting markets only on grounds that all reasonable citizens can accept—typically, grounds having to do with preventing harm to others or addressing coercion and inequality. This is the liberal neutralist position. Sandel gestures toward this with his "fairness" objection, which focuses on whether market exchanges are truly voluntary given background inequalities. But then he insists that his main focus is the "corruption" objection, which does not depend on equality or coercion.

The corruption argument, however, cannot be neutralized in this way. It rests on substantive claims about human flourishing that not all reasonable citizens share. A perfectionist liberal might argue that certain forms of commodification are genuinely degrading, regardless of the equality conditions under which they occur. But this moves away from political liberalism and toward a comprehensive moral doctrine that some citizens will reject.

Perhaps Sandel's argument is best understood as a pro tanto case against certain forms of market expansion, not a conclusive one. The corruption argument shows that we have reason to be suspicious of market reasoning and to deliberate carefully before extending markets into new domains. But it does not settle which markets should be permitted. That determination requires the kind of democratic deliberation that Sandel rightly values.

The essay's greatest contribution is to make that deliberation necessary. In a culture that increasingly assumes market solutions to every problem, Sandel's insistence that some things should remain outside the market's reach is a valuable counterweight. Even if his philosophical foundations are shaky, his practical summons to collective moral reflection is one we should heed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Sandel fails to provide a clear or consistent definition of what 'corruption' actually means or why it applies to some goods and not others.
  2. 02The argument assumes that goods have essential natures or 'constitutive ideals,' a premise that different groups in a pluralistic society will reasonably disagree upon.
  3. 03Sandel sidesteps the procedural challenge of how a democracy should make collective decisions when citizens hold fundamentally different moral views on human flourishing.
  4. 04By prioritizing the 'corruption' objection over the 'fairness' objection, Sandel moves away from neutral political liberalism toward a comprehensive moral doctrine that not all citizens share.
  5. 05While Sandel’s philosophical foundations are shaky, his work serves as a valuable practical summons for society to engage in necessary democratic deliberation regarding market boundaries.
