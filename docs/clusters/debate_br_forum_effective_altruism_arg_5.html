"While effective altruism successfully challenges philanthropic complacency, it is fundamentally limited by its focus on individual charity and must evolve to address the systemic political and economic structures that produce global inequality."

Peter Singer’s essay is a masterful articulation of one vision of ethical living, and it contains much that is admirable. Yet it also reveals, in what it leaves out, the profound limitations of the effective altruism movement. Singer asks us to “do the most good we can,” as measured by a utilitarian calculus of suffering and lives saved. But by focusing almost exclusively on individual consumption and charitable donation, he treats the symptoms of global poverty while ignoring the structures that produce it. The result is a philosophy that moralizes the behavior of the relatively well-off while leaving the engines of inequality untouched. If we truly want to do the most good, we must not only give to effective charities but also fight for a world in which such charity is less necessary.

Singer’s signature move is to translate moral demands into personal giving decisions. He tells the story of Matt Wage, who chose a Wall Street career over academic philosophy in order to donate half his income to highly effective charities. It’s a compelling narrative, and Wage’s choice is genuinely sacrificial. But notice what it doesn’t question: the basic structure of a global economy in which a handful of arbitrage traders can earn six-figure bonuses while a child dies of malaria for want of a $5 net. Effective altruism takes that background as given and asks how to mitigate its worst effects. It is, in other words, a philosophy of amelioration, not transformation.

This is not merely an abstract criticism. Consider the tax avoidance practices of the very financial industry that Wage joined. A 2014 report from the U.K. charity ActionAid estimated that developing countries lose more than $100 billion annually due to corporate tax dodging—several times what they receive in aid. If Wage’s firm engages in legally questionable tax strategies, as many do, then his ability to donate relies on a system that simultaneously drains public resources from the very countries he seeks to help. The net good he does may be less than the harm the system enables. Effective altruism, with its narrow focus on individual consumption and charity, has no way to account for this structural feedback loop. A movement that genuinely prioritized “the most good” would demand corporate tax transparency, crack down on illicit financial flows, and support global tax reform. Yet these issues are notably absent from the EA playbook.

A related problem is the movement’s apolitical orientation. Singer writes that effective altruists “do not discount suffering because it occurs far away,” but he treats that suffering as a technical problem to be solved by efficient giving, not as the outcome of historical injustices and ongoing power asymmetries. The global poor are not merely unlucky; they are often victims of trade policies, debt regimes, and resource extraction that benefit the affluent world. By framing the issue as one of charitable choice, effective altruism implicitly adopts the perspective of the donor rather than the rights-holder. It transforms citizens with claims to justice into recipients of benevolence. This is a morally incomplete picture. When a child dies of malaria, it is not simply because a bed net was not donated; it is also because her government lacks the tax base to fund public health, often due to policies endorsed by international financial institutions and wealthy states.

To be clear, I am not suggesting that effective altruists abandon direct giving. In the immediate term, bed nets save lives, and those lives matter. The point is that a purely philanthropic strategy is insufficient and may even be self-defeating if it diverts energy and attention from political organizing. The same millennials who are inspired by effective altruism could be channeling some of their time and resources into campaigns for fair trade, debt cancellation, or climate justice—issues that affect the structural conditions under which poverty persists. Some effective altruists are already beginning to discuss these questions, but Singer’s essay gives them short shrift. His mention of justice, freedom, and equality is brief and instrumental: these values are “essential for building communities in which people can live better lives,” but they are not presented as fundamental demands in their own right.

An effective altruist might object that political advocacy is harder to measure than direct aid, making it difficult to determine whether one is indeed “doing the most good.” That is true, and the movement’s emphasis on evidence is a genuine strength. But the complexity of systemic change does not excuse us from pursuing it. If we let the limitations of our current metrics dictate the scope of our moral concern, we risk becoming the proverbial drunk who looks for his keys only under the lamppost because the light is better there. A truly effective altruism would invest in better metrics for systemic impact and be willing to act on the best available evidence, even when precise quantification is elusive.

Effective altruism has done a tremendous service by challenging the complacency of mainstream philanthropy. But if it wants to live up to its own standard—doing the most good—it must evolve from a movement of charitable donors into a force for global justice. That means embracing political action alongside personal giving, and recognizing that the most effective intervention may sometimes be not a check to a charity but a demand for a more just world order.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Effective altruism acts as a philosophy of amelioration rather than transformation by treating the global economic background as a given rather than questioning the structures that allow extreme wealth and poverty to coexist.
  2. 02The movement fails to account for structural feedback loops where the industries that enable high-level giving may simultaneously cause greater harm through practices like corporate tax avoidance.
  3. 03By framing global poverty as a technical problem for donors to solve, effective altruism ignores historical injustices and power asymmetries, turning rights-holders into mere recipients of benevolence.
  4. 04A purely philanthropic strategy may be self-defeating if it diverts resources and attention away from political organizing for fair trade, debt cancellation, and climate justice.
  5. 05The movement's reliance on quantifiable metrics should not excuse it from pursuing systemic change, even if the impact of political advocacy is harder to measure than direct aid.
Diversified DeepSeek

"To truly maximize its impact, the effective altruism movement must expand beyond individual charitable giving to include political advocacy and the pursuit of systemic change."

Peter Singer’s essay offers a stirring portrait of a movement that is changing how a generation thinks about its obligations to the global poor. The basic insight of effective altruism—that our charitable giving should be guided by evidence and reason rather than mere sentiment—is beyond dispute. Yet as the movement matures, it must confront a tension at its core: the most effective ways to improve the world may not always be the ones that fit neatly into a donor-centric cost-effectiveness spreadsheet. If effective altruism is truly to maximize its impact, it must expand its toolkit to include political advocacy and systemic change, not merely individual donations.

Singer’s lead example, Matt Wage, illustrates both the power and the limitation of the current paradigm. Wage forsakes an academic career for finance, enabling him to donate six-figure sums annually to highly effective charities. This is, by any reasonable measure, a deeply moral choice. But notice that his impact is mediated entirely through the act of giving: he earns, he donates, and charities deliver bed nets. The model leaves untouched the vast structural forces that determine why bed nets are needed in the first place. Consider that malaria thrives in settings of weak public health systems, poor governance, and environmental degradation. Many of these conditions are shaped by the policies of wealthy states—trade rules that disadvantage African agriculture, intellectual property regimes that keep drugs expensive, climate emissions that expand mosquito habitats. If Wage’s talent and passion were directed toward changing those policies, he might prevent orders of magnitude more suffering than even his generous donations can achieve.

This is not a hypothetical. The campaign for the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria has mobilized billions of dollars from governments, leveraging resources far beyond what private charity could muster. Advocacy for debt relief in the late 1990s freed up fiscal space in poor countries, allowing them to invest in health and education. The movement to eliminate agricultural subsidies in wealthy nations could do more for African farmers than decades of charitable projects. These political victories were not won by donors writing checks but by citizens organizing, lobbying, and shifting public opinion. Effective altruism, with its network of bright, committed young people, is ideally positioned to contribute to such efforts. But its current culture remains surprisingly apolitical.

Singer’s essay mentions justice, freedom, and equality only briefly, treating them as instrumentally valuable for living better lives. He does not discuss the possibility that addressing the root causes of poverty might require challenging powerful interests or embracing a moral language of rights and entitlements rather than charity. This is a missed opportunity. Effective altruism could broaden its theory of change without abandoning its commitment to evidence. Evaluating the effectiveness of political campaigns is undoubtedly harder than comparing deworming charities, but it is not impossible. Organizations like the Center for Global Development and Innovations for Poverty Action are already studying policy advocacy. Effective altruists could support research into the cost-effectiveness of different political strategies, just as they now fund development RCTs. They could ask not only “which charity saves the most lives per dollar?” but also “which advocacy organization can most cost-effectively influence legislation that saves lives?”

Some effective altruists are already moving in this direction. The effective altruism forum includes lively discussions on immigration reform, climate policy, and global governance. But these conversations remain on the margins, and the movement’s flagship organizations still overwhelmingly promote direct giving. Singer’s essay, with its focus on personal donation and career choice, reinforces this narrow focus. That is a mistake. If the goal is to do the most good, we cannot ignore the fact that the most powerful levers for global welfare are often political. A truly comprehensive effective altruism would train its members not only to maximize their donation budgets but to become effective citizens and advocates.

Matt Wage’s choice was admirable, but we should also imagine a Matt Wage who goes into politics, or who founds an advocacy group, or who devotes his quantitative skills to analyzing which policy changes could unlock billions in aid or improve the international trade regime. Such a path might save not hundreds but millions of lives. The movement Singer describes is still young, and its core insight—use reason and evidence to do the most good—is profoundly right. But if that insight is taken seriously, it leads inevitably beyond charity and toward the harder, messier, but ultimately more transformative work of building a just world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Individual donations address the symptoms of global poverty while leaving the underlying structural and political causes untouched.
  2. 02Political victories and policy changes can mobilize resources and prevent suffering on a scale that far exceeds the capacity of private philanthropy.
  3. 03The movement's current focus on donor-centric cost-effectiveness spreadsheets overlooks the transformative potential of challenging powerful interests and advocating for global justice.
  4. 04Effective altruism should apply its commitment to evidence and reason to evaluate the cost-effectiveness of different political strategies and advocacy organizations.
  5. 05A comprehensive approach to doing the most good requires training members to be effective citizens and advocates rather than just maximizing their personal donation budgets.
