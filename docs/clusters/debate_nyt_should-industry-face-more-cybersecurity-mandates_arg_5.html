Diversified MiniMax

"Cybersecurity mandates should be developed through international cooperation and diplomatic frameworks rather than as isolated domestic requirements to effectively address foreign threats and maintain economic competitiveness."

Any serious discussion of cybersecurity mandates must address the international dimension that domestic debates typically ignore. The threats we face originate from foreign actors operating beyond American jurisdiction. Our response cannot be purely domestic.

International cooperation matters for several reasons. First, attribution remains difficult in cyberspace; governments that could help identify attackers may lack incentives to cooperate. Diplomatic pressure and international frameworks could improve attribution and create consequences for state-sponsored attacks.

Second, many cyber attacks originate from jurisdictions with weak rule of law or explicit tolerance for malicious activity. Working with friendly nations to isolate these jurisdictions, combined with diplomatic engagement, could reduce the threat volume more effectively than domestic mandates.

Third, standards harmonization affects competitiveness. If American mandates impose costs that foreign competitors avoid, we create incentives for companies to locate operations elsewhere. International coordination on baseline standards levels the playing field while raising global security.

The Transnational Cybersecurity Dialogue launched under Secretary Clinton offers a foundation for this work. Extending such frameworks to include both state actors and private standards bodies could produce international convergence on security practices. Companies operating globally would face consistent requirements rather than navigating fragmented jurisdictions.

Meanwhile, diplomatic efforts should address the sanctuary that attackers enjoy in certain states. The 2012 Olympic Games partnership with China on cyber issues demonstrated that engagement is possible even with strategic competitors. We should expand such channels.

Domestically, we should align our approach with international partners, avoiding the fragmentation that market forces and varying national standards create. Our mandates should be designed with interoperability in mind, creating standards that could become international norms rather than American peculiarities. In cybersecurity, as in so many domains, our security depends on international stability that no domestic mandate can guarantee.

  4. 04Domestic mandates should be designed for interoperability with international partners to create global security norms rather than fragmented national regulations.
