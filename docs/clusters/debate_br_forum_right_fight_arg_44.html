Diversified Gemini

"Daniel Richman’s vision of an integrated, accountable domestic intelligence network is a theoretical pipe dream because it ignores the severe fiscal and administrative realities that prevent cash-strapped local governments from participating effectively."

In “The Right Fight,” Daniel Richman provides a compelling analysis of the complex dynamics between federal law enforcement and local police in the post-9/11 environment. He argues that the optimal domestic intelligence network must fully incorporate state and local authorities, and that doing so will organically generate a system that balances effective counterterrorism with democratic accountability. Richman’s vision is intellectually satisfying, positing that the institutional friction of federalism and the local imperatives of community policing will naturally curb federal overreach. However, his analysis gives insufficient weight to the overwhelming fiscal and administrative realities that govern local governance. Stripped of its theoretical elegance, Richman’s ideal cannot be realized without a fundamental, and highly unlikely, restructuring of how homeland security is funded in the United States.

To understand why Richman’s model of local integration is currently unworkable, one must look at the municipal balance sheets of America’s cities in 2004. Local governments are facing severe budget shortfalls. The economic downturn of the early 2000s, combined with dwindling federal aid, has forced mayors and city councils to make draconian cuts to basic services. In this environment, the federal mandate to participate in a massive, nationwide counterterrorism effort is not viewed as a partnership; it is viewed as an oppressive, unfunded mandate that threatens to bankrupt the very agencies tasked with carrying it out.

Richman touches upon the fiscal grievances of local governments, noting that the Bush administration has begun phasing out the COPS program and that homeland security funds are distributed based on a politically motivated state-minimum formula rather than threat assessment. He correctly observes that "a 911 call does not get a state trooper," and that funneling money through state bureaucracies alienates the urban centers that bear the greatest risk. But he treats these fiscal disputes as mere hurdles to be overcome on the way to a "happier ending," rather than as fundamental structural flaws that invalidate his premise.

The reality is that local police departments cannot perform the expansive intelligence-gathering and community-liaison roles Richman envisions if they cannot afford to keep officers on the street. The shift in federal priorities from violent crime to counterterrorism has drained the resources necessary for the very "community policing" that Richman claims will protect civil liberties. Community policing is notoriously labor-intensive. It requires officers to be free from the constant burden of responding to emergency calls so they can walk beats, attend community meetings, and build the relationships that Richman correctly identifies as crucial for obtaining intelligence.

When federal grants for local crime-fighting dry up, and when local police are simultaneously burdened with new homeland security duties—such as guarding critical infrastructure every time the federal threat level is elevated to "Orange"—the community policing model collapses under the weight of resource scarcity. Officers are pulled off neighborhood beats and assigned to static security details at bridges, tunnels, and monuments. The time and energy required to cultivate trust within immigrant communities simply evaporate.

Furthermore, the bureaucratic mechanisms by which the federal government currently distributes homeland security funds actively undermine the integration Richman desires. The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) requires funds to flow through state governments, purportedly to ensure "statewide coordination." In practice, this creates a massive administrative bottleneck. State governments, facing their own budget crises, often skim off administrative fees and redirect funds to pet projects or rural constituencies, leaving major urban centers starved of the resources they need. A police chief in a major metropolitan area cannot "exact a toll from federal authorities as the price of gathering and supplying information," as Richman suggests, if that chief is entirely dependent on a hostile state legislature for the crumbs of federal funding.

Richman posits that because of fiscal constraints, the federal government will pay for local cooperation not in cash, but by giving local police "more of a voice in federal domestic-intelligence policy." This is a profound misreading of bureaucratic behavior. The federal government rarely cedes operational or policy control unless forced to by statute. And local mayors and police chiefs, struggling to balance their budgets and keep crime rates down, cannot pay their officers’ overtime with "a voice in policy." They need hard dollars. If the federal government continues to demand local participation in counterterrorism sweeps, interviews, and JTTFs without providing direct, sustained, and massive funding directly to the municipalities, local compliance will be minimal, grudging, and entirely ineffective.

Moreover, the fiscal disconnect highlights a deeper structural inequality in the "partnership" Richman envisions. The costs of counterterrorism are highly localized, while the benefits are national. If a terrorist cell is apprehended in Detroit, the entire nation benefits from the averted attack. But the costs—the thousands of hours of investigative time, the massive overtime bills for heightened security, the potential political fallout and community alienation—are borne almost entirely by the taxpayers and local officials of Detroit.

This fundamental asymmetry means that local officials will always have a powerful incentive to under-invest in counterterrorism intelligence gathering, regardless of the theoretical benefits of integration. Why should a mayor risk the wrath of local immigrant communities and divert scarce funds away from solving local murders, just to feed information into a federal database that provides no immediate local benefit? Richman believes this tension will lead to a healthy negotiation over tactics, resulting in a more accountable system. But it is far more likely to lead to passive resistance and bureaucratic paralysis.

If the federal government truly wants state and local governments to be "full partners" in preventing acts of terror, it must fundamentally alter the economic foundation of the relationship. Congress must scrap the absurd formula that guarantees Wyoming a disproportionate share of homeland security funds while shortchanging New York and California. Funding must be distributed strictly on the basis of threat, vulnerability, and population density. More importantly, these funds must bypass state bureaucracies and go directly to the urban police departments that are expected to do the actual work on the ground.

Until this structural fiscal reality is addressed, Richman’s vision of a seamlessly integrated, democratically accountable intelligence network will remain a theoretical pipe dream. You cannot build a robust national defense architecture on the backs of cash-strapped local municipalities and expect the result to be anything other than a fragmented, resentful, and ultimately insecure system.
