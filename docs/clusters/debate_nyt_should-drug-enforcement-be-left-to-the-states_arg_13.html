"The federal government should stop prosecuting local drug cases and instead redefine its role to focus on protecting constitutional rights, funding public health, and targeting violent interstate trafficking."

The federal government should stop prosecuting most local drug cases, but not because states are automatically more just. On drug policy, both federal and state governments have done deep damage. The real question is how to reduce that damage while protecting people from addiction, violence and discriminatory policing.

Federal drug enforcement has helped produce severe racial and class disparities. The old crack-powder sentencing gap, only partly reduced by the Fair Sentencing Act, remains a symbol of how punitive policy can fall hardest on black communities. Federal mandatory minimums give prosecutors extraordinary leverage and often produce sentences far beyond what public safety requires. When Washington takes an ordinary street-level case and makes it federal, mercy becomes harder.

But simply “leaving it to the states” is not a civil rights solution. Many of the most troubling marijuana arrests and street-level drug sweeps are local. Stop-and-frisk tactics, selective enforcement and underfunded public defense are state and municipal problems as much as federal ones. Some states are moving toward treatment and legalization; others may respond to drug use with even harsher penalties. Federal retreat without federal oversight could merely relocate injustice.

So the right answer is a redefined federal role. Federal prosecutors should avoid possession cases, low-level sales and state-regulated marijuana activity. The Justice Department should not use federal law to sabotage states that are reducing incarceration or treating addiction as a health issue. Clemency, sentencing reform and fewer mandatory minimums should be central to national policy.

At the same time, Washington should use its authority to protect constitutional rights. It can investigate police departments with patterns of discriminatory enforcement. It can collect and publish data on arrests, charging and sentencing by race and geography. It can support indigent defense, reentry programs and treatment access. It can pursue violent trafficking organizations without sweeping addicts and low-level couriers into decades-long sentences.

State experimentation is valuable only if the people experimented on have rights. Colorado and Washington should be allowed to test marijuana legalization. Vermont should be supported in expanding treatment. But no state should be applauded for innovation if its policy simply shifts punishment onto poor neighborhoods or people of color.

The federal government should not be the nation’s super-prosecutor for ordinary drug cases. Yet it should not wash its hands of drug policy either. Its job should be to set a floor of fairness, fund public health responses and reserve prosecution for genuinely interstate or violent crime. Federalism is useful; justice is the goal.

  4. 04Washington should shift its focus toward investigating discriminatory enforcement patterns and collecting data to ensure a baseline of fairness across all states.
