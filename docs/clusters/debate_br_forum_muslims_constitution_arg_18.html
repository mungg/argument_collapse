# A More Measured Critique

I want to engage seriously with the lead essay's arguments, which I find largely compelling but occasionally overstated. The authors are right that Islamophobia predates Trump and is embedded in American institutions. They are right that the War on Terror has been accompanied by massive civil liberties violations. They are right that the airport protests, while inspiring, were often grounded in a narrow conception of who deserves protection.

But I have questions about some of the essay's claims and its implied solutions.

First, the claim that the material support ban has been "the primary criminal charge in prosecutions the government calls terrorism-related" deserves scrutiny. The vast majority of terrorism-related prosecutions have involved actual plots or activity, not merely political speech or association. The cases that have drawn criticism—entrapped defendants, prosecutions based on speech rather than action—are real but not representative. A more careful analysis would distinguish between the cases where the statute has been applied appropriately and where it has been abused.

Second, the authors write that "courts historically often endorsed public fears and hatreds" and cite Dred Scott and Korematsu as evidence that courts cannot be trusted. But this is a selective reading of history. Courts also ended slavery (after a fashion), struck down separate but equal, and ordered desegregation. They have sometimes been in advance of public opinion, sometimes behind it. The question is not whether courts are trustworthy but whether we can build the kind of legal infrastructure and public support that makes judicial protection possible.

Third, the essay's six "misapprehensions" are presented as self-evident, but some are more contestable than others. The first—framing a defense of Muslims based solely on innocence—is a real problem with the airport protests. But the alternative is not clear. Should we defend the rights of people we suspect might be dangerous? Yes, absolutely—but this does not require celebrating them. The due process principle applies to everyone, including people who are, in fact, dangerous. The innocence framing is problematic because it suggests that we only defend rights when we believe people are innocent, not because rights are rights. But the solution is not obvious.

I am also skeptical of the implied solution to Islamophobia. The authors call for scrutinizing and challenging the policies they describe, but they do not say what they want as a replacement. Should the material support ban be repealed entirely? This would make it easier for terrorist organizations to raise money in the United States. Should surveillance of mosques be ended? This would make it harder to detect terrorist plotting. These are not easy questions, and the essay does not engage with them seriously.

I support the resistance to Trump's policies. I share the authors' alarm at the direction of American politics. But I want a more careful analysis of what has gone wrong, what the trade-offs are, and what a genuinely protective regime would look like. The essay is good at diagnosing problems but thin on solutions, and this limits its usefulness for building a sustained political movement.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay mischaracterizes the material support ban by failing to distinguish between its legitimate application in actual terror plots and its occasional abuse in cases of entrapment or speech.
  2. 02The lead essay’s cynical view of the judiciary is based on a selective reading of history that ignores the courts' capacity to advance civil rights and protect marginalized groups.
  3. 03The critique of the 'innocence framing' fails to provide a clear practical alternative for how to advocate for the rights of individuals who may actually pose a danger.
  4. 04The lead essay fails to engage with the difficult security consequences of its implied solutions, such as how to prevent terrorist financing or plotting without the tools it seeks to abolish.
  5. 05A sustained political movement requires a more careful analysis of the trade-offs between liberty and security than the lead essay provides.
