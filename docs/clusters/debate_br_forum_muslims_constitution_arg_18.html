"To effectively oppose Islamophobia and the travel ban, the movement must move beyond domestic pluralism to challenge the inseparable link between the domestic war on terror and the global structure of American military intervention."

The lead essay powerfully exposes the domestic machinery that has made Muslim belonging conditional in the United States. But its most consequential claim may be the one that appears almost in passing: the domestic war on terror and the global war on terror are inseparable. That claim deserves to be placed at the center. If we discuss the travel ban only as discrimination at the border, we will miss the larger structure in which Muslim-majority countries are treated as theaters of force and Muslim migrants as spillover risks from those theaters.

Consider the moral geography of the ban. The named countries—Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen—were presented as sources of danger to Americans. Yet several have also been sites of American military action, sanctions, covert involvement, or diplomatic intervention. Iraq was invaded and occupied. Libya was bombed in a NATO intervention led in part by the United States. Somalia and Yemen have been targets of drone strikes and special operations. Syria has been the site of U.S. airstrikes and proxy entanglements. Sudan lived for years under sanctions and international isolation. Iran has been subject to intense U.S. pressure and decades of hostility. The people most harmed by instability are then told that their instability makes them suspect.

This is not simply hypocrisy. It is a recurring imperial pattern: intervention produces disorder, disorder produces migration, migration produces panic, panic produces exclusion. The intervening power describes itself as the victim of the very chaos it helped create. Refugees become security threats; asylum becomes generosity rather than obligation; the border becomes a moral laundering device.

Akbar and Theoharis are right that Trump’s order intensifies earlier practices. The Obama administration did not impose a Muslim ban, and differences matter. But Obama normalized targeted killing, expanded drone warfare, prosecuted leakers, used watch lists, and maintained a security vocabulary in which Muslim communities were repeatedly asked to partner against extremism. Liberal cosmopolitanism softened the edges of coercion but did not end it. The same country that celebrated diversity at home dropped bombs abroad. The contradiction was sustainable because the victims of foreign policy remained distant, while the domestic targets of surveillance were told that their discomfort was the price of safety.

The challenge, then, is to build an anti-Islamophobia politics that is also antiwar. Many opponents of the ban are not there yet. They object to discrimination against visa holders, students, doctors, and translators, but they do not object to the wars that helped make visas matters of life and death. They say refugees should be admitted because they helped American troops, not because American troops should not have been there in the first place. They defend Muslims as patriotic Americans while leaving untouched the assumption that American power is fundamentally benevolent.

Still, an antiwar response must avoid two errors. The first is reducing all jihadist violence to “blowback.” U.S. policy has contributed enormously to violence and instability, but movements such as ISIS and al-Qaeda are not mechanical reactions. They have ideologies, leaders, funding sources, local alliances, and brutal ambitions of their own. They kill Muslims who oppose them. They exploit grievances but are not reducible to grievances. A left analysis that cannot condemn their authoritarianism and cruelty without hesitation will fail morally and politically.

The second error is treating Muslim communities only as victims of empire. Muslims debate war and peace in many registers. Syrian Americans may demand protection from Assad’s barrel bombs while also fearing Islamophobic exclusion. Iraqi Americans may oppose the 2003 invasion and also want ISIS destroyed. Yemeni Americans may condemn U.S. support for Saudi bombing while criticizing Houthi repression. Iranian Americans may oppose both U.S. threats and the Islamic Republic’s abuses. There is no single “Muslim” foreign-policy position. Anti-Islamophobia should create space for these debates, not conscript Muslims into a simplified anti-imperial script.

But acknowledging complexity should not obscure responsibility. The United States has used military force across the Muslim world with remarkably little democratic accountability. The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force has stretched far beyond the people who planned September 11. Drone strikes occur in countries where Congress has not declared war. Civilian casualties are often disputed, hidden, or rhetorically minimized. “Precision” has become a moral anesthetic. The public is asked to trust secret intelligence, secret legal memos, and executive branch assurances. Courts rarely intervene. Congress rarely reclaims authority. The same deference that Akbar and Theoharis identify in domestic terrorism cases operates globally.

This global deference returns home. When officials insist that the battlefield is everywhere, the border becomes a checkpoint in an endless war. When extremist ideology is treated as a contagious substance, religious and political speech become objects of epidemiological concern. When the enemy is networked, association becomes incriminating. When war has no clear endpoint, emergency powers become ordinary governance.

The material support regime embodies this merger of war and law. It imports the logic of enemy affiliation into domestic criminal law. To be sure, the state may prohibit sending money or weapons to groups that commit violence. But in conflict zones, political, religious, humanitarian, and military structures often overlap. A charity may operate in territory controlled by a designated group. A peace advocate may need to speak with armed actors. A family may pay money under coercion. A scholar may communicate with controversial organizations. The more expansive the law, the more it chills contact with places already isolated by war. The result is that Muslim Americans with ties abroad may be frightened away from charity, travel, advocacy, and even grief.

This is also why the “good Muslim” narrative is so politically useful to militarism. The good Muslim condemns terrorism, supports U.S. troops, expresses gratitude, and asks for inclusion. The bad Muslim criticizes U.S. violence, mourns foreign victims too loudly, questions alliances with dictators, or practices religion in ways deemed intense. The category of extremism polices the boundary. It does not merely identify those who would commit violence; it disciplines those who would challenge the story America tells about its violence.

Bill Clinton’s convention line captured this perfectly: if you are Muslim and love America and freedom and hate terror, stay and help us win. The invitation sounds generous only if one ignores its conditions. Why must Muslims be addressed as a separate category? Why is their place tied to helping “us” win? What if they oppose both terrorism and the war framework itself? What if they believe American policy has produced more insecurity? Are they still welcome?

The response to Trump should therefore be more ambitious than restoring the pre-Trump consensus. Blocking the travel ban is urgent. But returning to a world of polite drone warfare, secret watch lists, and community surveillance would be an inadequate victory. A genuine alternative would include repeal or serious revision of the 2001 AUMF, congressional debate over military action, transparency on civilian casualties, an end to support for reckless allied bombing campaigns, a refugee policy shaped by responsibility rather than fear, and domestic reforms that protect dissent.

Some will object that this agenda is too broad. A travel ban can be litigated; foreign policy cannot be solved in an airport terminal. But the breadth is not optional. The ban itself rests on a story about dangerous countries, dangerous populations, and American innocence. If that story is false, the answer must address the whole story. Otherwise each crisis will be treated as discrete: a bad executive order here, an unfortunate drone strike there, an overzealous prosecution somewhere else. The pattern will remain.

The airport protests showed a moral energy that has been absent for too long. People who had never attended a demonstration came with signs saying refugees were welcome. Lawyers appeared with laptops. Taxi workers struck. Translators volunteered. It was a glimpse of civic resistance. But the movement now faces a choice. It can become a defense of American pluralism narrowly understood: Muslims should be included because America is a nation of immigrants and religious freedom. Or it can become a challenge to the way American power sorts human beings into protected citizens, suspect residents, disposable foreigners, and collateral damage.

The first path is easier and not worthless. The second is necessary. Islamophobia is not only a prejudice in the American mind. It is a relationship between a powerful state and populations it has learned to fear, manage, and attack. It is domestic and international, cultural and legal, rhetorical and military. To oppose it fully is to insist that Muslim lives matter not only when they are inside our airports, not only when they serve our armies, not only when they reassure us, but also when they are under our drones, behind our watch lists, in our prisons, and beyond our borders.
