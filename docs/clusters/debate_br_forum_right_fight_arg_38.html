"Integrating local law enforcement into federal counterterrorism efforts risks corrupting the local criminal justice system by turning state prosecutors and courts into tools for pretextual federal intelligence gathering."

In “The Right Fight,” Daniel Richman builds a comprehensive theoretical model of how local police departments can and should integrate with federal counterterrorism efforts. His focus on the dynamics of policing—community relations, intelligence gathering, and mayoral accountability—is insightful. However, by concentrating almost exclusively on the actions of the police, Richman overlooks a far more consequential and legally troubling aspect of the post-9/11 domestic intelligence apparatus: the role of the prosecutor and the profound distortion of the local criminal justice system.

When we shift the lens from the street cop to the District Attorney, the integration of local and federal counterterrorism efforts looks less like a healthy, democratic negotiation and more like a systemic subversion of the rule of law. If local police become the primary intelligence gatherers for the federal government, local courts and local prosecutors will inevitably be co-opted into holding and leveraging terror suspects using pretextual state charges. This dynamic threatens to corrupt the integrity of local criminal courts and erode the fundamental constitutional rights of defendants in ways that structural "friction" cannot remedy.

Since September 11, the Department of Justice has adopted a strategy of aggressive preventive detention. Lacking sufficient evidence to charge suspects with actual terrorism offenses, federal prosecutors have routinely used minor, unrelated federal charges—such as credit card fraud, minor immigration violations, or making false statements—to hold individuals in custody while the FBI investigates their potential terror ties. The use of "material witness" warrants has also been dramatically expanded to jail individuals indefinitely without criminal charge.

If Richman’s vision is realized and local police become the primary nodes of the domestic intelligence network, this strategy of pretextual prosecution will inevitably migrate to the state and local level. Richman himself notes that "the threat of prosecution (even prosecutions having nothing to do with terrorism) is one of the best tools around for prying loose closely held information." He points out that local enforcers bring "the bulk of serious criminal charges in the United States."

But what happens when this local prosecutorial power is explicitly harnessed for federal intelligence purposes? Consider the scenario: A local police officer, acting on a tip or a directive from a Joint Terrorism Task Force (JTTF), stops a young Muslim man for a broken taillight or minor drug possession. The FBI suspects the man has information about a terror cell but lacks the evidence for a federal warrant. The local police arrest the man on the state charge, and the local District Attorney is quietly asked by federal authorities to request exorbitant bail, delay discovery, and drag out the state proceedings to give the FBI time to interrogate the suspect and build a federal case.

This scenario completely subverts the legal standards of the local criminal justice system. Bail laws in state courts are designed to ensure the defendant’s return to court based on the specific local charge, not to facilitate federal intelligence interrogations. The right to a speedy trial, the rules of evidence, and the presumption of innocence are all compromised when a minor state charge is secretly used as a proxy for an unprovable federal terrorism suspicion.

Richman relies on the idea that local officials, including chief prosecutors, are electorally accountable. But the electorate is completely blind to this specific abuse of power. When a local DA requests high bail for a minor offense under the guise of "public safety," the judge and the public do not know that the true motive is a classified federal intelligence request. The proceedings occur in the open, but the true rationale is hidden in the shadows. There is no democratic accountability for actions that are inherently secret.

Furthermore, this dynamic fundamentally changes the role of the local defense attorney and the state judge. They are thrust into a shadow game where the normal rules of plea bargaining and case resolution do not apply, because the local prosecutor is not actually interested in the local charge; they are acting as a holding pen for the FBI. This makes a mockery of state courts and treats local criminal codes as mere tools for federal intelligence gathering.

Richman briefly acknowledges the role of prosecutors, noting that federal agencies historically enjoyed federal-local cooperation in violent crime cases because they could be "strategic in their case selections." But violent crime prosecutions, whether state or federal, share the same ultimate goal: punishing the violent act. In counterterrorism, the goal is often intelligence extraction, not conviction for a specific past crime. When local prosecutors are asked to use their immense discretion to facilitate intelligence extraction on behalf of the federal government, the potential for abuse skyrockets.

We saw hints of this danger in the massive sweeps following 9/11, where hundreds of immigrants were swept up on minor state or local infractions and subsequently disappeared into the federal detention system. If we institutionalize this relationship, as Richman advocates, we risk normalizing the use of local jails as federal holding cells and local criminal laws as mere pretexts for intelligence gathering.

To protect the integrity of the criminal justice system, we must enforce a strict separation between local criminal prosecutions and federal intelligence investigations. If the federal government suspects an individual of terrorism, it must meet the legal burden required to obtain a federal warrant or a federal indictment. It should not be permitted to use local police and local District Attorneys to do an end-run around the Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Amendments.

Richman’s "optimistic story" relies on institutional structure to provide accountability. But the structure of the criminal justice system is designed to provide accountability through transparency, adversarial proceedings, and judicial oversight. Injecting the necessary secrecy and preventive imperatives of national security intelligence into local courthouses will not make counterterrorism more accountable; it will simply make local justice more arbitrary and opaque.
