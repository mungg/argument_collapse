**Money Is a Symptom, Not the Disease**

Governor Roemer's speech belongs to a venerable American genre: the insider who, having served honorably, returns from Washington to tell us the system is rigged. From the Progressives through Common Cause through John McCain and now Buddy Roemer, the refrain is the same. The republic is sound; only the corrupting influence of money stands between us and good government. Strip the money out and statesmanship will flower.

I want to push back on this story, not because money in politics is harmless—it isn't—but because it has become a kind of all-purpose explanation that obscures more than it reveals.

Consider Roemer's centerpiece example: the Research and Experimentation tax credit. He treats its periodic reauthorization as proof of corruption. But there are other plausible reasons Congress prefers temporary tax provisions, including budget scoring rules that make permanent extensions look prohibitively expensive over ten-year windows and a legitimate desire to revisit whether incentives are still working. The same logic applies to the Bush tax cuts, which expire because of reconciliation rules, not because Republicans wanted donors to call them every two years. Reasonable people can disagree about whether the R&E credit should be permanent, but the fact that it isn't proves nothing about bribery, soft or hard.

Or take the financial crisis, which Roemer presents as the morality play of our time: Wall Street bought deregulation, Glass-Steagall fell, the house of cards collapsed. This is partly true and partly a comforting fable. The repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999 did not cause the 2008 crisis in any direct sense. Lehman Brothers and Bear Stearns were pure investment banks; the Glass-Steagall wall would not have protected them. Countrywide and Washington Mutual were thrifts. AIG was an insurer. The actual mechanism of crisis—subprime origination, securitization, ratings failures, and the shadow banking system's reliance on repo funding—operates largely outside the Glass-Steagall framework. Blaming campaign contributions for the crisis allows us to skip past the harder questions: Why did regulators believe their own models? Why did homeowners, borrowers, originators, ratings agencies, and investors all participate in a bubble that, in retrospect, looks obvious? "They were bought" is a more satisfying story than "we were all wrong," but it is not necessarily a more accurate one.

The deeper problem with Roemer's frame is that it cannot explain political outcomes that money cannot buy. The financial industry spent enormous sums opposing the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. It got the CFPB anyway. Big Pharma is a perennial top spender. The Medicare Part D program is generous to it, but the Affordable Care Act extracted substantial concessions too. The plaintiffs' bar has been generous to Democrats for decades and has watched tort reform advance at the state level repeatedly. If money were the master variable Roemer claims, these outcomes would be hard to explain.

What actually drives Congressional behavior is, to a much greater extent than reformers admit, ideology and constituency. The most polarized Congress in a century is not polarized because donors disagree—donors mostly want stability. It is polarized because the parties themselves have sorted ideologically, because the median Republican primary voter and the median Democratic primary voter live in different epistemic worlds, and because gerrymandering has insulated members from general-election accountability while exposing them to primary challenges from the flanks. None of this gets fixed by a $100 donation cap.

I would go further: the small-donor revolution Roemer celebrates may make polarization worse. The data we have from the 2008 and 2010 cycles suggest that small donors are more ideologically extreme than large donors, not less. Howard Dean's small-donor army, the Tea Party's small-donor army, MoveOn's small-donor army—these are not centers of moderation. A Congress more responsive to small donors and less responsive to large institutional interests might be a more honest Congress. It will also likely be a more ideologically rigid one.

This does not mean we should do nothing. Disclosure should be vigorous and immediate. The revolving door between Congressional staff and lobbying firms should be slowed by longer cooling-off periods. Bundling should be disclosed in detail. Earmarks, which Roemer does not mention, deserve continued scrutiny. And yes, public financing experiments at the state and municipal level are worth running.

But we should be honest about what these reforms can do. They can make corruption marginally harder. They can improve disclosure. They cannot turn Congress into the deliberative body Roemer remembers from his early years—because that Congress was not the product of clean campaign finance. It was the product of a particular political culture, a particular set of party structures, and a particular national mood, none of which can be restored by a donation cap.

Roemer has earned the right to his frustrations. But the country he wants will not arrive when the money leaves. The country he wants requires that we ask harder questions than "who paid for this?"

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Temporary tax provisions like the R&E credit are often the result of budget scoring rules and legislative procedures rather than a deliberate scheme to solicit campaign donations.
  2. 02The 2008 financial crisis was caused by complex systemic failures and widely held economic misconceptions rather than being a simple consequence of Wall Street buying deregulation.
  3. 03The fact that major industries often lose legislative battles despite heavy spending proves that money is not the master variable of political outcomes.
  4. 04Congressional behavior is primarily driven by ideological sorting and the pressures of polarized primary electorates rather than donor influence.
  5. 05Empowering small donors, as proposed by Roemer, may actually increase political polarization because small donors tend to be more ideologically extreme than large donors.
  6. 06Campaign finance reform cannot restore a lost era of statesmanship because that culture was the product of specific historical and social conditions, not just the absence of special-interest money.
