"While Brandon Terry’s effort to recover Martin Luther King Jr.’s radicalism is necessary, his treatment of sexism as a mere addendum fails to recognize that gender is a constitutive dimension of King’s political and ethical architecture that requires a fundamental reconstruction of his legacy."

Brandon Terry’s essay “MLK Now” is a necessary provocation. It insists that we confront the radical, unsettling King—the critic of empire, the theorist of systemic racism, the advocate of mass civil disobedience for economic justice—rather than the sanitized prophet of national consensus. Yet even as Terry acknowledges that sexism is the “most glaring weakness” in King’s thought and proposes adding a fourth evil to racism, militarism, and poverty, the essay itself reveals the limits of a mere addendum. Gender is not an add-on; it is a constitutive dimension of the entire political and ethical architecture Terry seeks to reclaim. Until that recognition reshapes our retrieval, the King we recover will remain politically and morally compromised, particularly for those whose struggles King never fully saw.

Terry’s own discussion of the movement’s legacy illustrates the problem. He praises the “magnificent universal quality” of nonviolent direct action, which could include people of all walks of life. Yet the civil rights movement’s public face was overwhelmingly male, and its internal culture frequently relegated women to supportive, subordinate roles. Rosa Parks was an experienced NAACP investigator, yet the mythology of the movement reduced her to a tired seamstress whose singular, spontaneous act was more palatable than that of a seasoned organizer. Ella Baker, who insisted on participatory democracy and challenged King’s top-down charismatic authority, was sidelined in favor of a masculine leadership model that equated prophetic voice with male presence. Fannie Lou Hamer’s searing testimony at the 1964 Democratic National Convention unsettled a nation, but she was never given a central organizational role comparable to King’s. The powerful women-led welfare rights and tenants’ unions Terry notes in passing were innovations born more from the movement’s margins than its core.

King’s gender essentialism, which Terry briefly mentions, was not incidental. He publicly extolled a domestic ideal for women that was inconsistent with his own wife Coretta’s expansive activism. This doctrinal patriarchy shaped the moral framework of nonviolent love and sacrifice. The ethic of agape, of voluntary suffering for the redemption of the oppressor, was articulated in a masculine voice that cast the active, visible sufferer as male. The women who sustained the movement—organizing boycotts, raising funds, providing hospitality, and often enduring state violence—were expected to embody a quieter, more self-effacing form of sacrifice, one that conformed to patriarchal expectations of femininity. Terry’s attempt to distinguish King’s “politics of character” from the indefensible version of respectability politics fails to account for the gendered demands of such character. Virtues such as humility, thrift, and self-sacrifice, when prescribed to women in a patriarchal culture, become instruments of subordination, not liberation.

The analytical lens Terry applies to racism—its entanglement with irrational fear, its sociopolitical embeddedness, and its cognitive dimensions—must be gendered. Racist terror has a specific, visceral manifestation in the sexual vulnerability of black women, from the violence of enslavement to contemporary patterns of street harassment, sexual assault, and the policing of reproductive autonomy. The contempt for black life that King diagnosed has never been solely about the shredded dignity of the black male body; it has been equally about the degradation of black women as sites of uncontrollable sexuality and deviance. When King argued that mass protest could dissolve the stereotype of the passive, grinning Uncle Tom, he was addressing a male archetype. He had far less to say about the Jezebel or the welfare queen, images that have sustained punitive welfare policies and mass incarceration in its gendered forms.

Terry’s call to treat King as a profound interlocutor requires that we engage critically with him, not merely recover his radicalism. The Movement for Black Lives, which Terry notes has often reached past King to more “radical” ancestors such as Assata Shakur and Audre Lorde, is not merely performing a superficial rejection of respectability. It is insisting on an intersectional politics that refuses the separation of racism from sexism, homophobia, and transphobia. The focus on black women, queer, and trans people in contemporary movements is not a departure from King’s radical promise but a fulfillment of what his vision, at its best, might have aspired to had he been able to transcend the patriarchal limits of his church and his time. Yet that transcendence cannot be accomplished by simply adding sexism to a list of evils; it requires rethinking the entire fabric of the organizing tradition.

This is especially urgent given the ongoing crisis of the black church, which Terry identifies as a profoundly weakened institution. The sexual and financial corruption scandals, the marginalization of women, and the hostility toward LGBTQ+ people make the church an ambivalent home for a liberatory politics today. King’s theological commitments are not simply a curiosity to be translated into secular terms; they are bound up with institutional structures that have often been sites of gendered harm. A retrieval of King that does not place feminist and queer critique at the center risks replicating those harms under the guise of recovering moral seriousness.

Terry rightly warns against the cynicism that would discard King’s legacy wholesale. But the most faithful way to honor King’s “divine dissatisfaction with injustice” is to refuse a restoration that treats gender as a footnote. A politics of character for our time must be built around the ethical insights of black feminist thinkers—those who, in the decades since King’s death, have articulated a vision of love, justice, and community that does not depend on patriarchal hierarchy. The same courage and clarity of thought Terry admires in King ought to lead us to say plainly: King’s failure to confront sexism is not a flaw to be patched but a wound that compels a far more thorough reconstruction. Only then can we honestly claim the radical King as a resource for the present, and only then can we fulfill the promise of a movement that leaves no one behind.
