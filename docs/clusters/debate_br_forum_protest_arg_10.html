"The right to protest must be expanded from a defense of expressive hostility into a right to organize that challenges the undemocratic power structures and managerial sovereignty of modern universities."

Alex Gourevitch’s essay is a defense of protest, but its deeper subject is power. That is why it is more convincing than many campus free speech arguments. He does not treat the university as a neutral marketplace of ideas temporarily disturbed by unruly students. He treats it as an institution structured by trustees, donors, police, federal money, real estate, administrative bureaucracy, and labor hierarchy. The crackdown on Gaza protests did not fall from the sky. It was the predictable response of institutions that have learned to manage dissent as a reputational risk.

Still, if the right to protest is to be rebuilt, we should go further than Gourevitch does. The right to be hostile must become the right to organize.

Campus protest is not merely expressive. It is a form of collective power by people who usually have very little. Students do not control endowments. Graduate workers often have little security. Adjuncts are disposable. Staff are expected to keep the university running while having minimal voice in its governance. Faculty governance has been weakened for decades. The people most invested in the intellectual and social life of the university are often least empowered to decide its direction.

That imbalance explains why administrators reached so quickly for police. They experienced encampments not only as speech but as a challenge to managerial sovereignty. Students built temporary communities outside official channels. They debated, cooked, prayed, studied, held teach-ins, and issued demands. They interrupted the smooth functioning of the university-brand. That is precisely what made the encampments politically meaningful.

The language of safety then became a way to restore hierarchy. Administrators did not say, “We are clearing this encampment because trustees and donors want it gone.” They said, “We are protecting students.” They did not say, “We cannot tolerate a challenge to our investment policies.” They said, “The community is being disrupted.” They did not say, “We fear congressional Republicans and federal funding threats.” They said, “We must enforce rules.”

This is not to say that safety concerns were invented. Jewish students have faced real antisemitism. Muslim, Arab, and Palestinian students have faced real racism and repression. Students have been doxxed, harassed, and threatened. But the institution’s response has not been to build collective safety. It has been to monopolize the definition of safety and attach it to policing.

A labor movement would recognize the pattern. Employers routinely invoke “order,” “access,” and “respect” to discipline pickets. They describe disruption as coercion when it is workers doing the disrupting, but as efficiency when management restructures lives. They claim neutrality while hiring union-busting consultants. Universities are not exempt from this logic. They are employers, landlords, investors, contractors, and political actors. When students demand divestment or workers demand a contract, they are confronting power, not merely expressing views.

This is why Gourevitch’s comparison to labor pickets deserves more attention. A picket does not simply communicate disagreement. It pressures. It asks others not to cross. It makes normal operations morally and practically harder. Yet labor law and democratic tradition have recognized that such disruption can be legitimate because without it workers have little leverage. Campus protest likewise often seeks leverage: divestment, amnesty, disclosure, bargaining, an end to institutional complicity. If the only acceptable protest is one that cannot pressure anyone, then the university has protected speech while disabling politics.

The challenge is to defend disruptive pressure without romanticizing every tactic. Movements can reproduce domination. They can marginalize dissenters, tolerate bigotry, or mistake intensity for strategy. Encampments can become insular. Chants can become cruel. Activists can evade accountability by saying everything is justified by the cause. A serious left cannot accept that. But accountability should come from democratic movement culture and fair institutional processes, not from police raids or donor-driven discipline.

What would a pro-protest university look like?

First, it would democratize governance. Students, faculty, graduate workers, staff, and contingent instructors should have real representation in decisions about protest rules, investment policy, policing, and discipline. Boards should not be insulated clubs of wealth. Presidents should not govern as crisis managers for donors. If the campus is a community, its members should govern it.

Second, universities should abolish or radically shrink their dependence on armed police for protest management. The UCLA failure, where counterprotesters attacked a pro-Palestinian encampment while authorities delayed intervention, exposed the hollowness of official safety talk. Police are often deployed against protesters, not for them. Universities need trained de-escalation teams, clear protective protocols, and a strong presumption against calling municipal police unless there is serious violence or imminent danger.

Third, discipline should be restorative and proportionate. If students violate protest rules by camping overnight or refusing to disperse, the default should not be suspension, expulsion, arrest, or degree withholding. Universities should use warnings, mediated agreements, community repair, and modest sanctions. Severe penalties should be reserved for violence, threats, serious harassment, or major property destruction.

Fourth, universities should protect political boycotts and divestment campaigns as core forms of campus democracy. It is absurd to celebrate student activism in brochures while treating actual demands on institutional investments as illegitimate. Students are right to ask where tuition, endowment income, and research partnerships go. Even those who disagree with divestment should defend the right to organize for it.

Fifth, equal protection must be real. If Jewish students report antisemitic harassment, it should be investigated. If Palestinian students report anti-Palestinian racism, it should be investigated. If Muslim students are surveilled or doxxed, the institution should respond. But none of these protections should become a veto over political protest. The standard must be conduct: threats, exclusion, targeted harassment, discrimination—not the mere fact that a movement’s message is painful.

Gourevitch is right that the subjective safety standard has backfired. Many progressives once believed that expanding administrative definitions of harm would protect the vulnerable. But administrators are not agents of liberation. They are agents of institutions. They will use whatever language is available—civility, safety, inclusion, antisemitism, professionalism, disruption—to protect institutional authority. When the right learned to speak that language, the trap snapped shut.

The answer is not indifference to harm. It is collective power over the conditions that produce vulnerability. A Palestinian student whose family is under bombardment does not need an administrator’s bland wellness email; she needs political space to grieve and organize. A Jewish student afraid of antisemitism does not need the state to deport activists; he needs a campus culture that can distinguish antiwar politics from hatred of Jews and confront the latter without criminalizing the former. A graduate worker threatened for protesting does not need a listening session; she needs a union, due process, and protection from retaliation.

The right to be hostile, then, is not the right to be careless. It is the right to confront power in public, to accuse, to assemble, to disrupt, and to demand. Hostility is sometimes the voice of moral clarity in a world of euphemism. Universities that train students to avoid it train them for obedience.

But protest rights will remain fragile if they depend on enlightened administrators. They must be embedded in democratic governance and backed by organized constituencies. Faculty senates, unions, student governments, civil liberties groups, alumni, and community organizations should negotiate binding protest protections before the next crisis. Amnesty for punished student protesters should be part of that agenda. So should limits on donor influence, transparency in investments, and restraints on police.

The campus is one of the few remaining places where large numbers of young people live in proximity, argue about the world, and can act collectively. That is why it is contested. The crackdown on Gaza protests is not only about Gaza, though Gaza is reason enough. It is about whether universities will be governed as democratic commons or as fortified brands.

Gourevitch has named the freedom at stake. The next step is to build the power capable of defending it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Campus protests and encampments are politically meaningful specifically because they function as a form of collective power that disrupts the smooth operation of the university-brand.
  2. 02Administrators have weaponized the language of safety and subjective harm to restore institutional hierarchy and protect the interests of donors and trustees.
  3. 03The university should be viewed through the lens of labor struggle, where disruption is a legitimate tool for marginalized groups to gain leverage against powerful institutional actors.
  4. 04True protection of protest requires democratizing university governance to include students, faculty, and staff in decisions regarding investment, policing, and discipline.
  5. 05Universities must replace armed policing with de-escalation teams and shift disciplinary procedures toward restorative, proportionate sanctions rather than severe punishments like expulsion.
  6. 06The standard for administrative intervention should be based on objective conduct—such as threats or targeted harassment—rather than the psychological discomfort caused by a movement's political message.
