"Instead of pursuing economically destructive carbon mitigation as an 'insurance policy,' we should prioritize global economic growth and technological development to maximize our adaptive capacity to climate change."

Kerry Emanuel’s assessment of our changing climate is a masterclass in the physical sciences, carefully detailing the mechanics of atmospheric warming, the thermodynamics of hurricanes, and the potential instabilities of the cryosphere. As one of the world’s leading atmospheric physicists, his summary of the climatic system’s behavior is virtually unassailable. However, when Emanuel transitions from describing atmospheric physics to prescribing global policy, his argument falters. He frames the issue as a simple risk-management problem, suggesting that even if we are uncertain about the severity of climate impacts, we ought to make sacrifices to curtail greenhouse-gas emissions as an "insurance policy." This analogy, while intuitively appealing, fundamentally misrepresents the economic and human realities of climate policy as we face them at the dawn of 2007.

To understand why Emanuel’s insurance analogy is dangerously misleading, we must examine the actual cost of the premium he is asking humanity to pay. An insurance policy is only rational if the cost of the premium is significantly lower than the expected cost of the catastrophe, and if paying the premium does not impoverish the policyholder in the present. The aggressive curtailment of greenhouse gases—which, given the current state of technology, inevitably means a drastic reduction in the use of affordable fossil fuels—is not a modest premium. It is an economic shock of staggering proportions, particularly for the developing world.

Fossil fuels are the engine of modern human prosperity. They provide the cheap, reliable energy necessary to purify water, power hospitals, manufacture fertilizer, transport goods, and maintain the complex infrastructure that keeps billions of people alive. In places like China and India, the rapid expansion of coal-fired electricity is pulling hundreds of millions of people out of crushing, subsistence-level poverty. To demand that these nations curtail their emissions is to demand that they slow their economic development. The "sacrifice" Emanuel mentions is not merely a matter of Americans driving smaller cars or paying more for air conditioning; it translates to prolonged energy poverty for the world's most vulnerable populations.

Furthermore, Emanuel uses the devastation of Hurricane Katrina as a prime exhibit of the costs of climate change. He notes that Katrina may cost the United States $200 billion and claimed over 1,200 lives, and he connects this to the increased power output of tropical cyclones. But this attribution masks the true drivers of the Katrina catastrophe. The tragedy of New Orleans in 2005 was not primarily a failure of the global climate; it was a catastrophic failure of local engineering, urban planning, and emergency response. New Orleans is a city built largely below sea level, relying on an aging and inadequately maintained levee system. When a strong Category 3 storm made landfall, the infrastructure failed.

This distinction is crucial because it points to a far more effective "insurance policy" than carbon mitigation: adaptation and economic resilience. Consider Emanuel’s other example, the 1970 cyclone in Bangladesh that killed roughly 300,000 people. Since 1970, despite a rising global population and warming oceans, mortality from tropical cyclones in Bangladesh has plummeted. Why? Because Bangladesh has invested in early warning systems, concrete storm shelters, and better disaster response protocols. They did not stop the storms; they adapted to them.

Wealth is the ultimate climate defense. A wealthier society can build stronger sea walls, engineer drought-resistant genetically modified crops, install robust air conditioning systems to survive heat waves, and construct sturdy homes that can withstand Category 5 winds. If we adopt draconian carbon mitigation policies that artificially raise the price of energy and stifle global economic growth, we rob future generations of the very wealth and technological capacity they will need to adapt to whatever the climate throws at them—whether driven by anthropogenic carbon or natural variability.

The recently published Stern Review in the United Kingdom attempted to argue that the costs of inaction far outweigh the costs of action. However, as numerous economists have pointed out, the Stern Review relied on artificially low discount rates to inflate the future costs of climate damage while minimizing the immediate economic destruction caused by steep carbon taxes. When viewed through the lens of mainstream economics, such as the framework provided by the Copenhagen Consensus led by Bjørn Lomborg, massive investments in immediate carbon mitigation rank as a remarkably poor use of global resources. If our goal is to reduce human suffering, investing billions of dollars to slightly lower the global temperature a century from now is highly inefficient. Those same billions, invested today in clean water, malaria eradication, agricultural research, and infrastructure development, would yield massive, guaranteed, and immediate dividends in human well-being.

Emanuel also expresses a profound concern about our collective ignorance and the possibility of "climate surprises"—sudden jumps in the climate system that could devastate civilizations. He points to the ice-core records as evidence that the climate can react in complex, non-linear ways. This is a valid scientific observation, but it is a poor foundation for policy. If the climate system is indeed as fragile and prone to sudden shifts as he suggests, then it is entirely possible that mitigation efforts—which are virtually guaranteed to fall short given the geopolitical realities of nations like the US and China—will be insufficient to prevent these jumps.

If we are facing a system that can suddenly flip into a new, dangerous state, putting all our chips on a marginal reduction in CO2 emissions is a fool's errand. The only rational response to an inherently chaotic and dangerous natural system is to maximize our adaptive capacity. We must build robust, wealthy, and technologically advanced societies capable of managing large-scale disruptions.

None of this is to say we should ignore carbon emissions or abandon research into alternative energy. We should absolutely invest heavily in research and development for next-generation nuclear power, advanced solar technology, and energy efficiency. When clean energy becomes cheaper than coal, the market will naturally decarbonize without the need for economically destructive global mandates. But until that technological tipping point is reached, we must not sacrifice the tangible, immediate benefits of economic growth on the altar of uncertain, distant climate modeling.

In the end, Emanuel’s question—"Can we stop global warming?"—may be the wrong question entirely. The historical record, which Emanuel himself cites, shows that humanity has survived ice ages, interglacial periods, and regional climate shifts. We survived them not by attempting to control the composition of the global atmosphere, but by adapting to our surroundings. The climate is going to change in the 21st century, just as it always has. We cannot stop it without inflicting unconscionable economic self-harm. Instead of an impossibly expensive and likely ineffective global insurance policy of emissions curtailment, we should pursue a policy of relentless economic and technological development. A richer, more advanced world is the only true insurance we have against the vagaries of a changing climate.

  2. 02Historical data shows that adaptation and wealth, rather than climate control, are the most effective defenses against natural disasters.

  4. 04If the climate system is prone to sudden, unpredictable jumps, building robust and wealthy societies is a more rational response than attempting to manage the atmosphere.
  5. 05Policy should focus on research and development to make clean energy naturally cheaper than fossil fuels rather than imposing global mandates.
  6. 06The devastation of events like Hurricane Katrina is primarily a failure of infrastructure and planning rather than a direct result of global warming.
