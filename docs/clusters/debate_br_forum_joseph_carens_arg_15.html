"Granting legal status to long-term unauthorized migrants is a necessary democratic remedy to ensure that all people governed by the state have a meaningful voice and are protected from arbitrary domination."

Joseph Carens’s essay rests on a claim that sounds simple but has radical implications: people who live in a society become members of it. This claim should be understood not merely as a humanitarian appeal, but as a democratic principle. Democracy is government by the people, but who are “the people”? If millions of residents are governed, taxed, policed, educated, and economically incorporated while being denied secure status, then the democratic community is governing a population that has no meaningful voice in shaping its laws.

Unauthorized migrants are not tourists. Long-settled migrants are subject to the same criminal laws, traffic rules, school systems, labor markets, and local conditions as citizens. They pay sales taxes and often payroll and income taxes. Their children sit in public classrooms. Their labor contributes to common prosperity. Yet because of their status, they live under a threat that prevents open participation. They cannot vote, often cannot organize safely, and may fear even attending public meetings. This creates a democratic deficit.

Carens focuses on the moral wrong of deportation after settlement. I would add the wrong of domination before deportation. A deportable resident lives under arbitrary power. A boss can threaten to call immigration. A landlord can ignore repairs. A domestic abuser can exploit a partner’s fear of removal. A police officer’s traffic stop can become a gateway to exile. Even if deportation never occurs, the threat structures daily life. Republican political thought has long defined freedom not merely as non-interference, but as non-domination. By that standard, unauthorized status is a condition of unfreedom.

Legalization is therefore a democratic remedy. It transforms people from subjects of discretionary power into rights-bearing residents. Eventually, it should allow them to become citizens. Carens is right to insist that permanent residence should include access to citizenship. Some propose legalization without citizenship as a compromise. That may seem attractive politically, but it risks creating a permanent second class: people allowed to work and pay taxes but never to share fully in sovereignty. A democracy should be wary of such arrangements.

The United States has struggled with this before. Slavery, the disenfranchisement of women, racial exclusion from naturalization, and Jim Crow all involved populations governed without equal membership. Unauthorized migration is not the same as those injustices, and analogies should be made carefully. But the democratic structure is familiar: a group is present, necessary, and governed, yet excluded from full standing. Over time, that exclusion becomes harder to justify.

Carens’s time threshold recognizes this. Recent arrival does not necessarily create a democratic claim to membership. But after years of residence, the relationship between person and state changes. The state is no longer merely deciding whether to admit an outsider. It is ruling an insider. The longer that rule continues without representation or secure status, the more it conflicts with democratic legitimacy.

This democratic argument also explains why case-by-case humanitarian discretion is insufficient. A benevolent official may spare a sympathetic family, but domination remains if people must plead for mercy. Rights should not depend on whether a story moves a bureaucrat or attracts newspaper attention. A time-based rule would make membership a matter of public principle rather than private pity.

Of course, citizenship is not only residence. It involves commitment to a political community. But commitment is cultivated through inclusion. People who are told for years that they do not belong may understandably withdraw. Legalization invites investment: learning English, buying homes, joining associations, reporting crime, planning businesses, and naturalizing. Exclusion produces the very separateness that critics then cite as evidence against inclusion.

The democratic case for amnesty must also address existing citizens’ right to self-government. Citizens may say: we never consented to these new members. That concern deserves a response. But consent in a democracy is not a single moment of unanimous approval. It is expressed through laws, institutions, enforcement choices, markets, and public practices. For decades, the United States has combined restrictive formal laws with economic reliance on unauthorized labor and inconsistent enforcement. Citizens have benefited from this arrangement even while disavowing it. Amnesty would not impose an alien reality on the polity; it would acknowledge a reality the polity has helped create.

Furthermore, democratic self-government is constrained by justice. A majority cannot maintain a rightless class simply because exclusion is convenient. Once people are settled among us, our power over them must be justified to them as well as to ourselves. “You broke the law years ago, so you may live indefinitely in fear” is not a justification worthy of a liberal democracy.

What would a democratic amnesty look like? It would provide legal status to long-term residents with clean serious-criminal records. It would include a path to citizenship, not merely work authorization. It would prioritize those brought as children and those with citizen or resident family members. It would protect workers who report abuse. It would reduce detention and ensure due process. It would also create lawful future channels so that democratic membership is not continually produced through illegality.

Critics worry that such a policy would dilute citizenship. I think the opposite is true. Citizenship is diluted when millions of our neighbors are governed from the shadows. It is strengthened when the boundaries of the political community are brought into alignment with the community of people who actually share social life.

Carens asks us to see the moral importance of time. Democracy gives us another reason to do so. The passage of time turns border crossers into residents, residents into neighbors, and neighbors into people who are governed as part of us. A democratic state may control entry. But it cannot indefinitely deny membership to those over whom it exercises settled power. Amnesty is not a departure from democracy. It is democracy catching up with its own people.
