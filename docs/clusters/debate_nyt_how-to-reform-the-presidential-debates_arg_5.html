Diversified DeepSeek

"Presidential debates should be reformed by dedicating each session to a single policy domain rather than covering multiple topics in one evening."

We should abandon the kitchen-sink approach to debate topics and instead dedicate each encounter to a single policy domain. A ninety-minute free-for-all that touches on ten different issues guarantees superficiality. Candidates learn to pivot to pre-scripted talking points no matter what is asked, and viewers never see a sustained clash over the trade-offs inherent in real governance.

Imagine instead a series of debates, each devoted entirely to one subject: a full evening on foreign policy and national security, another on economic growth and inequality, a third on healthcare and entitlement reform. With that kind of focus, the moderator can design a ladder of questioning that probes a candidate's philosophy, their specific proposals, their past votes, and the internal contradictions in their positions. Follow-up becomes natural because the entire conversation is in the same territory. A candidate who tries to dodge a question about Medicare solvency by talking about job creation immediately looks evasive, and the audience can see it.

This single-topic model has historical precedent: the Lincoln-Douglas debates of 1858 were each structured around a central theme. It would also relieve the press of the temptation to judge debates purely on gaffes or “who won the evening.” By the end of a single-issue session, voters would have a much richer understanding of how each candidate thinks about a problem they will actually face in office.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing on a single subject prevents candidates from using pre-scripted talking points to pivot away from difficult questions.
  2. 02A single-topic format allows moderators to engage in sustained, deep questioning that exposes contradictions in a candidate's philosophy and proposals.
  3. 03The thematic approach shifts the focus of media and public evaluation away from superficial gaffes and toward a candidate's actual governing logic.
  4. 04Concentrating on one issue at a time forces candidates to address specific trade-offs and policy details that are lost in a multi-topic free-for-all.
Diversified Gemini

"To improve the quality of presidential debates, the parties should implement issue-specific formats that dedicate an entire session to a single policy area."

The sheer number of issues covered in a single two-hour debate guarantees that none of them will be discussed with any depth. Bouncing from ISIS to tax reform to criminal justice to education in the span of an hour reduces complex national crises to superficial soundbites.

To reform the debates, the parties should mandate issue-specific formats. Instead of general debates, we should have one debate dedicated entirely to the economy, another solely on foreign policy and national security, and another on domestic social issues.

Forcing candidates to spend two hours discussing only healthcare and domestic policy, for example, would exhaust their rehearsed talking points within the first twenty minutes. Once the platitudes run out, they would have no choice but to dive into the actual mechanics of their proposals. A single-issue focus prevents candidates from pivoting away from their weak spots. You cannot pivot to your strong stance on border security if the entire night is dedicated to the tax code. If we demand serious policy platforms, we must provide a format that gives candidates the time and space to explain them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current multi-issue format forces candidates to rely on superficial soundbites because there is insufficient time to discuss complex crises in depth.
  2. 02A single-issue focus would exhaust candidates' rehearsed talking points and platitudes, forcing them to explain the actual mechanics of their proposals.
  3. 03Dedicated formats prevent candidates from using pivot tactics to avoid discussing their policy weaknesses.
