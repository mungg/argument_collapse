You are performing argumentative role analysis of an op-ed essay responding to a debate prompt. Label each paragraph by its dominant argumentative role in the essay's progression.

## Per-paragraph annotation

For each paragraph, identify its dominant argumentative role from the labels below. Use `none` when no label applies. This layer is only about argument — style, tone, and evidence type should not influence the choice.

Multi-labeling is allowed. Assign no more than two labels per paragraph, and use two labels only when necessary. Do not combine `none` with any other label. Assign two labels only when each label independently dominates a substantial part of the paragraph's argumentative function. Avoid redundant secondary labels — e.g., do not add `support` alongside `rebuttal`/`reframing` when the supportive function is implicit; the headline move suffices.

### Decision flow (when in doubt)

Walk through these in order. Use the first label that fits the paragraph's *primary* act.

1. Does the paragraph FIRST establish the essay's central position? → `thesis`. **At most one paragraph per essay may be labeled `thesis`.** Later restatements/recaps default to `support` (or `implication`/`proposal` if the recap extends into consequences or a call to action).
2. Does the paragraph directly engage a specific opposing claim that the essay treats as the *other side* of the debate, and give reasons against it? → `rebuttal`. Not just any critique of an alternative view — there must be an identifiable interlocutor stance the essay is responding to.
3. Does it acknowledge that an opposing concern, criticism, or alternative view has real force? → add `concession` (often as a secondary label to thesis/rebuttal/support).
4. Does it present an opposing position neutrally, foregrounding it as the paragraph's main object without taking a stance against it? → `counterclaim`.
5. Does it REPLACE the framing of the debate itself (not just offer an alternative analytical lens within the existing framing)? → `reframing`. If it just develops the author's view by contrasting with an alternative theory while staying inside the same debate, that's `support`, not `reframing`.
6. Does it draw consequences, stakes, or projections from claims already made? → `implication`.
7. Does it call for specific action (what should be done, by whom, under what conditions)? → `proposal`.
8. Otherwise, the paragraph develops the author's affirmative case (sub-claims, reasons, examples, comparisons, contrast with alternatives, critique of competing theories) → `support`. **`support` is the default for affirmative case-building.**

**Don't choose `rebuttal` or `reframing` just because a paragraph contains contrast or critique. Choose them only when contrast/critique is the paragraph's HEADLINE act and engages a specific opposing position (rebuttal) or replaces the debate's frame (reframing).** Otherwise the paragraph is `support` developing the author's affirmative line.

**Rhetorical questions** function as the move they imply, not as their literal interrogative form. A closing "Should we accept X?" calling for change → `proposal`. "How can we ignore Y?" affirming a point → `support`. "But isn't this just Z?" engaging an objection → `rebuttal`.

### Label set

**`thesis`**
The paragraph that first establishes the essay's central position. **At most one paragraph per essay can carry the `thesis` label.** Later restatements (e.g., a concluding recap) count as `support`, not `thesis`. The position can be implicit or inferred — subtle declarations count. When the same statement functions as the central position AND takes the form of a recommendation or consequence claim, label that statement `thesis` (not `[thesis, proposal]` or `[thesis, implication]`). A paragraph can still carry both labels if it contains *additional* distinct proposals or implications beyond the central position.

Examples:
- A paragraph asserting: "The current immigration system is broken, and only comprehensive reform can repair it."
- An opening paragraph that crystallizes the position before any developed support.

**`support`**
A paragraph that advances a supporting sub-claim, reason, or line of reasoning to strengthen the writer's main argument. Also use for *bare* later restatements of the thesis — recaps that add nothing new. If a closing recap extends the thesis into projected consequences, label `implication` instead; if it extends into a call for action, label `proposal` instead. Don't reflexively co-label `support` with `implication`/`proposal` on the same paragraph; pick the dominant move. **`support` is for affirmative development of the author's own line of reasoning — not for paragraphs whose work is engaging or dismantling an opposing position. If the paragraph references an opposing argument and gives reasons against it (even without overt rebuttal markers like "but" or "this is wrong"), prefer `rebuttal`.**

Examples:
- A paragraph arguing that a policy has succeeded because measurable outcomes improved after implementation.
- A paragraph diagnosing a system's flaws to show why the writer's position is needed.
- A closing paragraph that recapitulates the central position without projecting consequences (`implication`) or calling for action (`proposal`).

**`reframing`**
A paragraph that changes the lens of the debate — rejecting a misleading framing, narrowing the issue, or substituting a better way of seeing the question. **Reserved for paragraphs whose primary act is replacing the framing itself. If the paragraph engages an opposing argument and gives reasons against it, prefer `rebuttal`. If it shifts perspective only as a side effect of developing the author's affirmative line, prefer `support`. `reframing` is the right label when the paragraph's headline move is "the debate as posed is wrong; here's a better way to frame it."**

Examples:
- A paragraph arguing that the issue is not whether homeownership rates are down, but for whom declining ownership is actually harmful.
- A paragraph rejecting a false binary and replacing it with a more useful frame.
- A paragraph opening with "The real question is not X but Y" and developing the better frame.

**`rebuttal`**
A paragraph that answers, defeats, or undercuts an opposing position. Prefer `rebuttal` over `support` *only* when the paragraph engages a specific opposing claim that the essay frames as the *other side* of the debate (whether named explicitly or implicit as the position the essay is pushing back against). **Critiquing a theoretical alternative or competing framework as part of developing the author's own affirmative case is `support`, not `rebuttal` — even when that critique involves direct contrast or pointing out flaws.** The interlocutor test: is the paragraph responding to a position the essay treats as a counter-argument it must defeat, or is it building the author's case by contrast with alternatives? Only the former is rebuttal.

Examples:
- A paragraph showing why the critics' worry, while reasonable on its face, doesn't survive scrutiny.
- A paragraph that systematically dismantles a proposed objection.
- A paragraph that opens by characterizing an opposing claim (e.g., "The argument that X...") and then provides evidence or reasoning showing why it doesn't hold — no overt "but" needed.
- A paragraph that takes a current framework or assumption and argues it is misguided, devalues something, or produces harmful outcomes.

**`concession`**
A paragraph that acknowledges an opposing concern, criticism, or alternative view has real force or partial truth. **Frequently appears as a *secondary* label alongside another move — e.g., opening paragraphs that concede a point before stating the thesis (`[concession, thesis]`), or paragraphs that concede before refuting (`[concession, rebuttal]`). When a paragraph has a "while X is true / however understandable Y is, [main move]" structure, the concession should be co-labeled even when the main move dominates.**

Examples:
- "To be sure, this reform would impose real short-term costs on rural hospitals."
- "The concern that stricter standards could slow innovation is a serious one."
- An opening paragraph: "However understandable the emotional reflex may be, charging pre-teens as adults is fundamentally unjust." → label `[concession, thesis]`.
- A mid-essay paragraph: "While the proposal has real merit, it ignores X." → label `[concession, rebuttal]`.

**`counterclaim`**
A paragraph that presents an opposing position as its main object of attention, without taking a clear stance against it within the paragraph itself. **This is not a rare label. Use `counterclaim` whenever the paragraph foregrounds an opposing view or dynamic — even when the author later (in a different paragraph) argues against it. If you find yourself labeling a paragraph `support` because the opposing material "supports the overall essay's argument by being later refuted," that's wrong — that paragraph is `counterclaim`.** `counterclaim` should not co-occur with `rebuttal` or `concession` (those involve taking a stance within the paragraph).

Examples:
- A paragraph beginning "Critics of this policy argue that it would lead to..." and continuing to develop the concern in good faith.
- A paragraph faithfully representing why others believe the opposite of the writer's position.
- A paragraph that presents an opposing economic dynamic neutrally (e.g., as Marx-aligned framing) to set up later argument, without yet pushing back.

**`implication`**
A paragraph that draws out what follows from claims already made — consequences, stakes, projections, or takeaways. If a single statement *is* both the essay's central position AND a consequence claim, label that statement `thesis`, not `implication`. The two labels can co-occur on the same paragraph when distinct implications appear alongside the thesis.

Examples:
- A paragraph spelling out what is likely to happen if current trends continue, drawing predictions from the prior analysis.
- A paragraph stating "this means that..." and developing the inferential consequences for several lines.
- A closing paragraph that recapitulates the central position and projects the consequences of acting (or failing to act) on it. In those cases prefer `implication` alone rather than co-labeling with `support`.

**`proposal`**
A paragraph that advocates a specific course of action — what should be done, by whom, or under what conditions. If a single statement *is* both the essay's central position AND a recommendation, label that statement `thesis`, not `proposal`. The two labels can co-occur on the same paragraph when distinct proposals appear alongside the thesis.

Examples:
- A paragraph laying out what cities should do to address housing shortages.
- A paragraph closing with a clear directive about what action is needed.
- A closing paragraph that recapitulates the central position and pivots to "therefore, we must…". In those cases prefer `proposal` alone rather than co-labeling with `support`.

**`none`**
Use `none` when no argumentative role applies — the paragraph is purely transitional, mechanical, or otherwise carries no argumentative move. Mutually exclusive with all other labels.
